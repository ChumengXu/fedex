Your Monday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Turning pledges into action.
Representatives of 195 nations reached a landmark deal on Saturday that will commit almost every country to lowering planet-warming carbon emissions. It was six years in the making.
The tough task of delivering on those promises now begins, but scientists are optimistic because of the cooperative tone of the negotiations.
Seven concrete measures have been agreed to. And here’s what they mean for the planet, businesses and politics.
• The president’s day.
President Obama will be at the Pentagon this morning for a National Security Council meeting on the Islamic State terrorist group. He’s expected to deliver a statement afterward.
And the White House is hosting 20 receptions this month, which will provide plenty of opportunities for awkwardness in the holiday photo line.
• Iowa’s new front-runner.
Senator Ted Cruz surged to a 10-point lead in a poll of likely Republican caucus-goers in Iowa, suggesting that a long fight for the nomination is ahead.
And Hillary Clinton is now focusing on trying to find common ground on specific issues, and less so on shattering the glass ceiling, as she tries to attract younger women to her campaign.
• Baltimore trial nears end.
Closing arguments will be heard today in the trial of William G. Porter, the first of six officers in court over the death of Freddie Gray, who suffered a spinal cord injury while in police custody in April.
• Inquiry into San Bernardino shootings.
Tashfeen Malik, who with her husband carried out the recent massacre in California, spoke openly on social media about her support for violent attacks, but she passed background checks by immigration officials.
The California police and the F.B.I. opened a hate crimes investigation after two mosques were vandalized over the weekend. A suspect was charged with arson in a separate attack on a mosque on Friday.
• Three years after Sandy Hook.
Twenty children and six adults died on Dec. 14, 2012, at Sandy Hook Elementary School in Newtown, Conn. The gunman’s mother was also killed.
But less than 2 percent of the more than 33,000 gun deaths in the U.S. were due to mass shootings. Slate has an interactive map to show the shootings near you in the past year.
BUSINESS
• Volkswagen’s management culture — confident, cutthroat and insular — is under scrutiny as potentially enabling the company’s lawbreaking behavior.
• Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, the mortgage giants, have been held to far more punishing standards than the big banks, our special report shows.
• The Seattle City Council plans to vote today on a bill to give Uber and Lyft drivers the right to collectively negotiate pay.
• Martin Shkreli, who has became a symbol of pharmaceutical industry greed, has a new plan to sharply raise the price of another drug.
• Wall Street stock futures are positive. European markets are higher, and Asian indexes finished mixed.
OVER THE WEEKEND
• Canada and its new prime minister, Justin Trudeau, warmly greeted refugees from Syria.
• French voters dealt a sharp setback to the far-right National Front in regional elections, one week after the party finished on top in the first round.
• The Times followed the odyssey of one transgender woman as she underwent gender reassignment surgery
• Sheriff deputies near Los Angeles repeatedly fired at an armed man even after he had fallen, video showed.
• About 90 people were killed in Burundi in political violence.
• Women in Saudi Arabia were elected to local councils for the first time, but they will make up less than 1 percent of council members nationwide.
• “The Hunger Games: Mockingjay Part 2” was the box-office winner, and “In the Heart of the Sea” was a flop.
• Catching up on TV: Episode recaps for “Homeland” and “The Affair.”
NOTEWORTHY
• Scoreboard.
The Carolina Panthers remain unbeaten, and the New England Patriots regained the top seed in their conference. Here are all of the N.F.L. Week 14 scores and highlights, as well as our playoff simulator.
The Golden State Warriors’ 24-game winning streak ended with a loss on Saturday to the Milwaukee Bucks.
And Alabama’s Derrick Henry won the Heisman Trophy.
• Songbirds.
“Adele Live in New York City” is a special taped at Radio City Music Hall before the release of her record-shattering third album, “25” (10 p.m. Eastern, NBC).
Also, Apple has won an exclusive streaming deal with Taylor Swift to show a film from her world tour.
• Artist’s view of immigration.
The graffiti artist Banksy unveiled his latest works — immigration-themed murals in and around a refugee camp in France.
The main image is a portrait of the Apple co-founder Steve Jobs, whose father immigrated to the U.S. from Syria.
BACK STORY
If you’ve watched American films, you’ve most likely heard the work of John Williams.
“Star Wars” fans will get to listen to the composer’s latest work, the soundtrack to “The Force Awakens,” beginning on Monday, when the film has its premiere in Los Angeles.
Mr. Williams wrote the scores for all six previous “Star Wars” movies, winning an Oscar for “A New Hope” (1977).
The American Film Institute declared it the greatest American movie score of all time.
He has won five Oscars and been nominated 49 times. That’s more than twice as many nominations as Meryl Streep, the most-nominated in the acting category, with 19.
Mr. Williams, born in Queens in 1932, has provided soundtracks for “Jaws,” “Close Encounters of the Third Kind,” “E.T. the Extraterrestrial,” the “Indiana Jones” series, “Jurassic Park” and “Schindler’s List.”
And those are just the films he did in partnership with Steven Spielberg. There are about 80 others. On television, he’s best known for the theme to NBC’s “Nightly News,” its Olympics broadcasts and “Sunday Night Football.”
Listen to some of Mr. Williams’s most memorable works, which have helped make classical music part of the soundtrack of American life.
