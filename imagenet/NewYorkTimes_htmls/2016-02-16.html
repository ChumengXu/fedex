Your Tuesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Supreme Court battle lines.
President Obama’s vow to choose a replacement for Justice Antonin Scalia, who was found dead on Saturday, and Republicans’ determination to block the nomination will command much of the attention of Washington and on the campaign trail today.
Some of this term’s Supreme Court cases could result in 4-to-4 deadlocks and could strengthen the court’s liberal wing.
The president, who has a constitutional duty to fill a high court vacancy, faces a challenge in getting the simple majority in the Senate needed to confirm a nominee. Here are some of the judges he might be considering.
Since Republicans took control of the Senate, the confirmation process for Mr. Obama’s nominees to the 12 regional federal courts of appeal has essentially been stopped.
• Stumping for votes.
Ted Cruz is trying to reframe the Republican primary race around the nomination of a justice. He’ll be in South Carolina, where Jeb and George W. Bush are taking aim at the front-runner, Donald J. Trump. Jeb Bush escalated his attacks on Mr. Trump during the Republican debate on Saturday.
Bernie Sanders campaigns today in South Carolina and Georgia, and Hillary Clinton meets with civil rights groups in New York. The success of the Sanders fund-raising operation is startling some political operatives.
• Lawlessness at sea.
President Obama will sign legislation this week that effectively bans American imports of fish caught by slave labor in Southeast Asia.
Mr. Obama is seeking closer ties to the region’s leaders at a summit meeting in California, to counter China’s growing economic and diplomatic influence.
• Syria’s battlefield grows bloodier.
The U.N. envoy to Syria, Staffan de Mistura, is in Damascus today, meeting Syria’s foreign minister in the hopes of restarting peace talks in Geneva.
Violence escalated on Monday, with airstrikes at four medical facilities leaving 50 dead, including children. Here’s our Q. and A. on the chaotic and unpredictable battle for Aleppo.
• Music’s big night.
Taylor Swift won album of the year for “1989,” and “Uptown Funk,” a collaboration between Mark Ronson and Bruno Mars, took record of the year at the Grammy Awards on Monday night.
Kendrick Lamar swept the rap field. The results reflected the longtime stodginess of the Recording Academy, our critic writes. See the full list of winners, and photos from the red carpet.
• U.S.-Cuba flight agreement.
A deal to allow passenger air service between the U.S. and Cuba for the first time in over 50 years is expected to be signed in Havana today. Services are expected to begin this year.
President Obama has said he wants to go to Cuba, but the spread of the Zika virus is of concern for the White House.
How to travel to Cuba.
• A pope’s deep ties to a philosopher.
Newly released letters that the future Pope John Paul II wrote to an American woman portray a startling affection.

• An exodus of capital, in which companies and individuals are moving money outside China, is casting doubt on the nation’s economic prospects.
• Dropping oil prices are putting pressure on the recycling industry, as new plastics — made from the byproducts of oil and gas production — are becoming cheaper.
• U.S. stocks jumped 2 percent on Friday, snapping a five-day losing streak. Here’s a snapshot of world markets.
OVER THE WEEKEND
• Pope Francis, on a six-day visit to Mexico, called for social justice, lamenting the income gap and denouncing the harsh treatment of indigenous peoples.
• Taliban suicide attacks and an intense fight for the northern city of Kunduz made 2015 the worst year for Afghan civilian casualties since the U.N. began tracking the data, officials said.

• “Deadpool” set a record for biggest domestic opening for an R-rated movie, taking in about $135 million at North American theaters.

BACK STORY
Facebook celebrated its 12th anniversary this month, and we wrote about in Back Story.
But social networking got its start 38 years ago today, when the first public, dial-up Computerized Bulletin Board System went online in Chicago.
Ward Christensen and Randy Suess had the idea of creating a virtual message board that people could connect to using a telephone modem, to post notes to one another. It was to be much like the physical bulletin boards in public spaces that people had been using for decades.
Yes, they did it even before Mark Zuckerberg of Facebook was born.
There were all sorts of limitations, of course. Few people had personal computers back then. Connecting also required a modem, a device that allowed computers to communicate over telephone lines by converting digital signals into sounds on one end of the call, and from sounds back to digital signals at the other end.
If you connected to a local bulletin board system, or BBS (you would have been charged long-distance fees for one outside your area), you could read or leave a message for other computer hobbyists.
Then the wait began. A reply could take days or weeks, but such connections could very well have made you a social networking pioneer.
