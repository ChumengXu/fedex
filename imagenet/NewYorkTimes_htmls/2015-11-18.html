Your Wednesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Police raid near Paris.
A woman died when she detonated an explosive vest during an early-morning raid in the suburb of St.-Denis today, and the police arrested five people.
The target of the operation was a Belgian suspected of organizing the attacks that killed 129 people on Friday.
• France’s emergency measures.
The French Parliament today is scheduled to debate whether to prolong a state of emergency, declared by President François Hollande over the weekend, for three months.
And the anti-Islam message voiced by the far-right National Front party, led by Marine Le Pen, is gaining more support among voters.
• Beyond France.
There isn’t a consensus on a strategy to defeat the Islamic State militants, but some themes have emerged — like taking a decisive role in the Syrian conflict and in addressing a growing radical strain of Islam — a panel of experts tells us.
• Obama calls out China.
President Obama today pressed China to stop its construction on reclaimed islands in the South China Sea, raising the contentious issue at the start of a two-day economic summit meeting in the Philippines.
The waters are a vital route for commerce and trade, and Mr. Obama announced $250 million in military contributions to several Asian nations to support their efforts to stand up to China.
• Inquiry into fantasy sports widens.
The New York State attorney general has expanded his investigation into daily fantasy sport sites, and has issued a subpoena to Yahoo.
Yahoo, which expanded from season-long fantasy sports into daily games in July, has been gaining ground on FanDuel and DraftKings, two sites New York is seeking to prohibit in the state.
• Salt Lake City’s milestone.
Jackie Biskupski has been elected the first openly gay mayor of Utah’s capital, where the Mormon Church and a small-town judge delivered setbacks this month to gays.
• Severe weather.
A winter storm brought more than a foot of snow to the Colorado region, while parts of the central and southern Plains suffered power failures and a series of tornadoes.
Severe thunderstorms and a few tornadoes could hit parts of the southern U.S. today, the National Weather Service says.
BUSINESS
• The Federal Reserve today releases an account of its policy-making committee’s October meeting, where it signaled that an interest rate hike could come by year’s end.
• The mobile payments start-up Square, and Match Group, which owns dating websites, are set to price shares in their initial public offerings today, two of the biggest tests for I.P.O.s this year.
• Ford’s luxury brand, Lincoln, will debut face-lifts to its models at the Los Angeles Auto Show.
• Wall Street stock futures are trending negative. European markets are lower, and Asian indexes closed mixed.
NOTEWORTHY
• Literary prize night.
The National Book Awards are presented tonight in New York, where the novelist Don DeLillo will receive the lifetime achievement honor.
Ta-Nehisi Coates, Hanya Yanagihara, Lauren Groff and Sally Mann are among the finalists.
• Baseball honors.
The Cy Young Award, given each year to the best pitchers in Major League Baseball, are announced today. The Cubs’ Joe Maddon and the Rangers’ Jeff Banister won the best manager prizes. The Astros’ Carlos Correa and the Cubs’ Kris Bryant were named top rookies.
And the Red Sox star David Ortiz could announce as early as today that the 2016 season will be his last.
• A first for country music.
The Library of Congress awards today its annual Gershwin Prize for Popular Song to Willie Nelson. He’s the first country music artist to receive the honor.
• Please don’t eat the winners.
Sugar-and-spice creations that won the annual National Gingerbread House Competition in North Carolina go on display today (slide show).
BACK STORY
Thirty years ago today, a mischievous 6-year-old and his stuffed tiger appeared in a comic strip.
It was the start of a 10-year run for “Calvin and Hobbes,” a peek into childhood that a Wall Street Journal essay this year called America’s strangest, funniest and most profound comic strip.
The strip took its name from the serious philosophers John Calvin and Thomas Hobbes, but the comic’s Calvin believed in having the most fun with the least amount of effort and the wildest imagination.
Calvinball, for instance, is a sport with no rules except those Calvin makes up as he goes along. (The Washington Post this year compared Donald J. Trump’s campaign to Calvinball.)
At its peak, “Calvin and Hobbes” ran in 2,400 newspapers. Then in 1995, the creator, Bill Watterson abruptly ended it with little explanation and has been largely out of view since.
“I’m proud of the strip, enormously grateful for its success and truly flattered that people still read it,” he said in 2010, “but I wrote ‘Calvin and Hobbes’ in my 30s, and I’m many miles from there.”
Still, anything is possible. The cartoons “Bloom County” and “Doonesbury,” which soared in the same era, only to be retired, made comebacks.
