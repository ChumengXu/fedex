Your Wednesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• North Korea’s first hydrogen bomb?
North Korea, one of the world’s most isolated and dangerous states, declared that it detonated its first hydrogen bomb today. It may be weeks or longer before other nations can determine what kind of test was conducted.
China, Russia, the European Union and others condemned the action as a breach of international law. The U.N. Security Council tentatively scheduled an emergency meeting today.
• At the White House.
President Obama’s executive actions on gun sales were met with skepticism in San Bernardino, Calif., where a mass shooting took place last month.
And the president’s 2016 calendar is already full of travel plans. Experience shows that the final year in the White House can be of enormous consequence.
• Campaign swings.
Bernie Sanders says Hillary Clinton is too cozy with the financial industry and that his regulatory proposals are bigger and bolder, while Mrs. Clinton isn’t mentioning him in Iowa.
Ted Cruz, on a bus tour in Iowa, is shrugging off Donald J. Trump’s questioning of Mr. Cruz’s citizenship; and Jeb Bush has shared the story of his daughter’s heroin addiction.
• Oregon occupiers’ demand.
The armed group that seized a wildlife reserve in Oregon wants to reverse decades of U.S. policy by returning federal lands to the control of ranchers and local governments.
The federal government, which owns 47 percent of all land in the West, regulates grazing on public lands.
• New York City moves on wages.
Mayor Bill de Blasio is set to announce today a $15-an-hour minimum wage for the city’s public work force.
City officials said it would be among the highest in the country. It matches a similar increase for state employees last year.
• The complicated Mideast map.
While almost all governments in the Middle East are controlled by either Sunnis or Shiites, the countries’ populations are a mix of the two branches of Islam. That’s a crucial factor in the region’s conflicts.
There are growing fears that the feud between Sunni-governed Saudi Arabia and Shiite-led Iran will wreck efforts to solve the area’s crises, including civil wars in Syria and Yemen.
• More scrutiny for migrants in Germany.
Germany said today that more than one million people had registered as asylum seekers there last year.
Reports that young women in Cologne had been groped and robbed on New Year’s Eve by men who appeared to be of “North African or Arabic” origin are a new political challenge for Chancellor Angela Merkel.
• San Bernardino gun buyer in court.
Enrique Marquez faces charges of conspiring to provide material support to a husband-and-wife team that killed 14 people at a holiday party last month when he’s arraigned today in federal court.
BUSINESS
• Pickup trucks and sport utility vehicles helped the auto industry set an annual U.S. sales record of 17.5 million vehicles in 2015, surpassing the previous record in 2000.
• Shares of Fitbit, a maker of fitness tracking devices, are due to open trading even lower today after they sank 18 percent on Tuesday.
The company just introduced a fitness watch priced at $200, less than the Apple Watch, but analysts say Apple is gaining market share.
• The developer of Lumosity “brain training” games is refunding $2 million to customers to settle U.S. accusations it misled buyers about the cognitive benefits of its apps and programs.
• Global stock markets are down. Wall Street futures indicate a decline of nearly 2 percent at the start of trading.
NOTEWORTHY
• Best in the ballpark.
The Baseball Hall of Fame ballots are announced today, and there is only one sure bet: Ken Griffey Jr. But Mike Piazza, Jeff Bagwell and Tim Raines may also be headed for enshrinement in Cooperstown, N.Y.
• The future of TV.
One of our tech columnists warns against buying the 4K TV, named for its high-definition resolution display.
The technology is taking center stage at International CES, the consumer electronics show in Las Vegas that opens to the public today.
• Soon to be a TV relic.
“American Idol,” a former ratings behemoth, begins its 15th and final season (8 p.m., Eastern, Fox).
• Digital downloads.
The New York Public Library releases more than 180,000 photographs, postcards, maps and other public-domain items from its special collections in downloadable high-resolution files today, one of the largest releases of free visual material.
• Inside the doping report.
Many of the athletes mentioned in a recent Al Jazeera investigation — which contends that some of the biggest stars in pro baseball and football are taking banned performance-enhancing drugs — are clients of one trainer.
• Breeds apart.
The American hairless terrier and the sloughi, an ancient North African hound, join 187 other dog breeds recognized by the American Kennel Club.
• The odds are one in 292.2 million.
But will that stop you from buying a ticket for tonight’s estimated $450 million Powerball jackpot?
BACK STORY
It’s a day to ponder poetry’s greats.
Carl Sandburg was born on this day in 1878 in Galesburg, Ill., and Kahlil Gibran was born on this day in 1883 in Lebanon.
Chicago became known as the “City of the Big Shoulders” because of Sandburg’s 1914 poem of the same name. Sandburg is also remembered for “The People, Yes” (1936), a book-length poem that was a tribute to Depression-era Americans.
Then there’s his best-selling two-volume biography of President Lincoln’s early years, published in 1926; followed by four more volumes on Lincoln’s war years, which received the Pulitzer Prize for history in 1940. He was awarded a Pulitzer for poetry in 1919 for “Corn Huskers” and another for “Complete Poems” in 1951.
Gibran is famous for “The Prophet” (1923), a collection that made him one of the best-selling poets ever.
The narrator, Almustapha, is a wise man exiled on an island, where he reflects on life’s questions. Scholars have linked the character’s struggle to Gibran’s own longing to return to Lebanon from New York.
He also published “The New Frontier” (1925). One of its lines might sound familiar: “Are you a politician asking what your country can do for you or a zealous one asking what you can do for your country?”
