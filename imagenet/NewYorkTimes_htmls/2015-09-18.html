Your Friday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Cuba in the spotlight.
The Obama administration today is expected to announce regulatory changes to loosen travel, commerce and investment restrictions on Cuba.
The thaw in relations with Cuba was helped along by Pope Francis, who arrives in Havana on Saturday before a U.S. visit that starts Tuesday.
• Capitol Hill’s collision course.
Senate Republicans say they will take up legislation outlawing all abortions after 20 weeks of pregnancy, as the House moves forward with a bill that would end government financing for Planned Parenthood.
The intensifying fight over abortion threatens to paralyze budget talks and force a government shutdown at the end of the month.
• Migrants march on.
Saying they had no choice, Croatian officials today closed all but one of the country’s border crossings with Serbia after more than 11,000 migrants arrived in the past 48 hours.
In Hungary, workers began laying razor wire on the country’s southern border with Croatia today, extending its 109-mile barrier on Serbia’s border to keep migrants out.
• Moving on up?
Many Republicans say Carly Fiorina could help with a longstanding party problem — drawing female voters — after what was widely considered a standout performance in Wednesday’s debate.
The Democratic candidate Bernie Sanders appears tonight on “The Late Show With Stephen Colbert.”
• Greeks at the ballot box, again.
Greece holds its fourth national election in three years on Sunday. Alexis Tsipras, the prime minister who oversaw the country’s last multibillion-dollar bailout, is hoping to return to office.
• What it costs to be poor.
Six people filed a class-action lawsuit against the criminal district court in New Orleans, accusing judges and court officials of running an “illegal scheme” in which poor people are jailed indefinitely if they fall behind on payments of court fees.
Accusations of funding criminal justice systems by fining poor defendants are receiving growing attention, partly because of the protests in Ferguson, Mo., where a similar suit was filed.
MARKETS
• Wall Street stock futures are flat this morning after the Federal Reserve’s decision on Thursday to hold fire on raising interest rates.
The expiration of certain options and futures contracts today, one of four so-called quadruple-witching days in the year, may give U.S. trading an extra degree of volatility.
European shares are mostly lower, and Asian indexes ended mixed.
• Some analysts said they were surprised that the language in the Fed’s statement was so cautious about the health of the economy, making an October rate increase less likely, as well.

BACK STORY
Today, a personal reflection from Victoria, one of your Morning Briefers:
For many years, “Eugene M. Jewell” was just a name inscribed on a metal bracelet that I, like so many others, wore in the 1970s.
Nearly five million similar bracelets were sold by a student group, starting in 1970, to raise awareness about those missing in action or held prisoner in the Vietnam War. Each had a name, a rank and the date of disappearance.
In 1971, I paid $2.50 and agreed not to remove the band until my bracelet’s “name” came home. But the metal dug into my wrist, the war ended, and the bracelet went into a box.
Today, on National POW/MIA Recognition Day, thanks to an Internet that makes us all a little less anonymous, I know that Eugene Jewell was more than an engraving.
He was 24 and a first lieutenant in the Air Force when he took off in an F-4 Phantom fighter jet for a mission over North Vietnam. His aircraft was shot down and lost on Sept. 4, 1965.
The Defense Department says 1,627 Americans who fought in the Vietnam War remain unaccounted for, including Captain Jewell (he was promoted in absentia).
When Captain Jewell was declared missing, his wife was back home in Topeka, Kan., with their 1-year-old daughter, Deborah, and pregnant with their second child.
Last year, Deborah Jewell wrote on the Vietnam Veterans Memorial website:
“It always is a surprise when I read about folks who have/are wearing a bracelet with my Dad’s name on it, and to hear about how their hearts ache for the loss and hurt our family feels.”
