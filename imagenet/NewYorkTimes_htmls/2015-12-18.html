Your Friday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• White House wrap-up.
President Obama holds his year-end news conference at 2 p.m. Eastern today, before traveling with his family to Honolulu for a two-week vacation.
Along the way, he will stop in San Bernardino, Calif., to meet privately with the families of victims of the mass shooting this month.
• Charges in San Bernardino massacre.
Enrique Marquez, who supplied the assault rifles that were used to kill 14 people in San Bernardino, has been charged with crimes including conspiring to support terrorists. A bail hearing is scheduled for Monday.
Meanwhile, hate crimes against Muslims and mosques across the U.S. have tripled since the attacks in Paris and San Bernardino, according to new data.
• On Capitol Hill.
The Senate is expected to approve a $650 million tax cut package that the House passed on Thursday, a companion to the $1.1 trillion year-end spending bill.
• “Lies, deceit and greed.”
That’s how an F.B.I. official characterizes the business schemes of Martin Shkreli, the brash entrepreneur who is now facing securities fraud charges.
The charges are not related to the huge price increases on lifesaving drugs that brought Mr. Shkreli notoriety. Federal officials said he devised a quasi-Ponzi scheme, using money from his first biopharmaceutical company to pay off investors in his hedge funds.
• On the campaign trail.
In a major blow to Bernie Sanders’s campaign, the Democratic National Committee said it was suspending its access to the committee’s voter database, after a software error allowed at least one of his staff members to review Hillary Clinton’s private campaign data.
The Democratic presidential candidates hold their third debate, in Manchester, N.H. (8 p.m. Eastern, Saturday, ABC).
• Afghan detainee abuse.
U.S. soldiers accused the Afghan police and Navy SEALs of abusing detainees. But the SEAL command opted against a court-martial and cleared its men of wrongdoing in the case, which was previously undisclosed.
The vivid details of detainee abuse, and how soldiers said they were urged to keep quiet, are found in a naval report.
• The defense secretary’s emails.
The Senate Armed Services Committee will review Ashton B. Carter’s messages to determine whether he compromised sensitive national security information while using a personal email account this year to conduct government business. Mr. Carter arrived in Afghanistan today.
• “Star Wars” is here!
“Star Wars: The Force Awakens,” the seventh film in the series, has opened in U.S. and Canadian theaters. It is expected to generate one of the biggest openings — if not the biggest — in Hollywood history, surpassing the $209 million that “Jurassic World” had in June.
BUSINESS
• The Federal Reserve’s first interest rate increase since the financial crisis went smoothly, but Fed officials must now decide what to do next.
• Volkswagen has hired Kenneth R. Feinberg, a specialist in compensation funds, to devise a program to address claims related to the company’s use of deceptive software to cheat on emissions tests.
• BlackBerry will report its third-quarter results today. Analysts expect profit and revenue to be down.
The Canadian company recently introduced an Android-based smartphone, the Priv, and is focusing more on its software business.
• Wall Street stock futures are trending negative. European markets are down, and Asian indexes finished broadly lower.
NOTEWORTHY
• Home again.
Tina Fey and Amy Poehler reunite as grown siblings whose childhood home is being sold, in “Sisters,” a comedy opening nationwide today.
Then they return to another home, of sorts: They’ll host “Saturday Night Live,” with Bruce Springsteen and the E Street Band as the musical guest.
• Best sellers.
“Gratitude” by the late Oliver Sacks, about living a good life and facing mortality, is new on our hardcover nonfiction list. And “The Book of Forgiving,” by Desmond Tutu, is new on our monthly relationships list.
Get an early look at all our best-seller lists.
• Health studies.
A new version of a screening test for ovarian cancer may reduce deaths from the disease, but more study is needed.
And researchers found a connection between a liquor tax increase and a decline in new gonorrhea infections.
• Rocky’s back.
“Creed,” the latest entry in the “Rocky” franchise, is in theaters, and props and costumes from Sylvester Stallone’s collection — including “Rambo” and “Rocky” films — are being sold at auction starting today.
• Adele cuts out the middleman.
The British singer moved to stop scalpers from reselling her concert tickets, and — according to one estimate — saved buyers more than $6.3 million in markups on secondary ticketing sites.
• A need for speed.
Candace Hill is good enough to qualify for the 2016 Olympics, and at age 16, she has become the youngest track athlete in the U.S. to turn professional.
• What’s new to watch.
Joel McHale hosts “The Soup,” the clip-based comedy series that mocks the entertainment world, one last time (series finale, 10 p.m. Eastern, E!).
And “F Is for Family” is a half-hour animated comedy series set in the 1970s (streaming, Netflix).

Our special report on one mansion that stood out in the booming high-end market of Los Angeles, where hidden ownership is common, was one of our most-read articles this week.
A report on how much politicians lie and our interactive quiz on “Star Wars” were also very popular.
BACK STORY
Hillary Clinton’s unbroken lead among the Democratic contenders in national polls suggests she has a strong chance of becoming the first American woman nominated for president by a major political party.
But women have made it to presidential ballots before, as the nominees of about 20 more obscure parties.
Most counts start with Victoria Woodhull, a colorful figure who created a convention that nominated her to run as the Equal Rights Party candidate in 1872. There are no records of anyone voting for her.
Belva Ann Lockwood, a trailblazing lawyer, ran under the same banner in 1884, earning fewer than 5,000 votes. Her 1888 run had even less success.
In the past seven decades, women have been nominated by parties with platforms seeking peace, social justice, communism and an end to abortion.
Lenora B. Fulani, backed by the fringe New Alliance Party, was the first to appear on the ballot in all 50 states, earning more than 200,000 votes in 1988. That was a record for a black candidate until Barack Obama. Her second run, in 1992, fizzled.
Other big vote-getters, relatively speaking, were Green Party nominees: Cynthia McKinney with about 160,000 in 2008, and Jill Stein with almost 470,000 in 2012.
One campaign is sometimes left out of official counts. In 1940, the Burns and Allen comedy team introduced Gracie Allen’s Surprise Party campaign. “Everybody knows a woman is better than a man when it comes to introducing bills into the house,” she explained.
Times were hard, and the nation needed a laugh. She won more than 40,000 write-in votes.
