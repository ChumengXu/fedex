Your Monday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Gun control takes center stage.
President Obama meets with Attorney General Loretta E. Lynch today to discuss what executive actions he can take to curb gun violence, before a town-hall-style meeting on Thursday in which he will discuss the topic.
At the same time, gun-control advocates are using big-money donors and shifting tactics to focus on state-by-state battles.
• Four weeks until Iowa.
Former President Bill Clinton is expected to campaign today for his wife, Hillary Clinton, in New Hampshire. Bernie Sanders’s campaign came within $4 million in the last quarter of 2015 of the amount Mrs. Clinton raised.
And Donald J. Trump shrugged off his appearance in a recruitment video posted by an affiliate of Al Qaeda.
• The Saudi Arabia-Iran split.
Saudi Arabia expelled Iranian diplomats after Tehran criticized the Saudis’ execution of an outspoken Shiite cleric and protesters stormed the Saudi Embassy in the Iranian capital.
The moves by the Sunni kingdom and Iran, the dominant Shiite power, could escalate tensions in the Muslim world. Here’s how Sunni and Shia Islam differ.
• Armed militia in Oregon standoff.
An armed antigovernment group is vowing to continue to occupy a federal wildlife refuge building to protest the government’s treatment of two ranchers ordered to prison for arson.
The group is led by Ammon Bundy, son of Cliven Bundy, who inspired a 2014 standoff in Nevada over cattle grazing on federal land.
Here’s what we know about the Oregon situation.
• Receding floodwaters in the Midwest.
Officials in Missouri are assessing the damage to properties and infrastructure after last week’s record floods. But the danger isn’t over in southern Illinois.
Many people in the Mississippi River basin have come to accept that flooding is a part of life.
BUSINESS
• China shares sank 7 percent today, sharply driving down global markets. German stocks are down more than 3 percent.
Wall Street is expected to open off more than 1 percent. That’s over 250 points in the Dow Jones industrial average.
Weak economic data and the Chinese currency, as well as instability in the Middle East, weighed on traders.
• Brazil has $38 billion in bond payments to make today and Tuesday, or it risks a debt downgrade.
• Last year generated a record $4.7 trillion in announced mergers and acquisitions, with the highest percentage ever derived from deals valued at more than $5 billion.
OVER THE LONG WEEKEND
• Two young Israelis were charged in connection with an arson attack in July that killed a Palestinian toddler and his parents in the West Bank.
• The use of psychologists at the prison in Guantánamo Bay, Cuba, has been sharply curtailed, a Times report revealed.
• Camille Cosby, the wife of Bill Cosby, must testify or be deposed in a civil suit, a judge ruled. And legal experts foresee obstacles for both sides in the case.
• American officials said they didn’t know how many foreign visitors overstay their visas.
• Nordic countries, overwhelmed by migrants, are rethinking their generosity; and female migrants often become prey to sexual abuse.
• Mayor Gisela Mota of Temixco, Mexico, was shot and killed at her house less than a day after taking office.
• New York’s attorney general is seeking refunds for customers of the two largest daily fantasy sports companies, FanDuel and DraftKings.
• Surprise! “Star Wars: The Force Awakens” was the weekend box-office winner in North America.
• Here’s your recap of the final season premiere of “Downton Abbey.” Think you know the show? Test yourself here.
NOTEWORTHY
• The N.F.L.’s playoff picture.
Here are the matchups for the wild-card round: The Steelers face the Bengals, the Texans draw the Chiefs, the Packers play the Redskins, and the Vikings take on the Seahawks.
The Panthers, Cardinals, Broncos and Patriots earned first-round byes. And it’s Black Monday for members of coaching staffs.
Alabama and Clemson advanced to college football’s Jan. 11 championship game, while Stanford’s Christian McCaffrey had 368 all-purpose yards, the most in Rose Bowl history.
• “I have always had problems with deadlines.”
That’s the author George R. R. Martin, writing about how he missed the Dec. 31 deadline for the sixth novel in his “Game of Thrones” series.
HBO premieres its sixth season of the TV program in April.
• In memoriam.
Dale L. Bumpers, a former Democratic senator from Arkansas who made a passionate closing argument defending President Bill Clinton against removal from office in a Senate hearing, died at 90.
Natalie Cole, a Grammy winner whose biggest hit was “Unforgettable,” a virtual duet with her father, Nat King Cole, died at 65. And Wayne Rogers, an actor in the TV show “‘M*A*S*H,” died at 82.
BACK STORY
A century ago, the world’s tallest building was the Woolworth Building in Lower Manhattan, at 60 stories and a height of 792 feet.
Today it’s the 163-story, 2,717-foot glass-and-steel Burj Khalifa, opened in Dubai, United Arab Emirates, on this day in 2010.
What’s next in the race to the top?
Saudi Arabia has the 1,972-foot Makkah Royal Clock Tower in Mecca, currently No. 3, but it hopes to take first place from Dubai with the Jeddah Tower, expected to rise to 3,281 feet in 2018 if it opens on schedule.
Ten more towers to pierce the clouds are either under construction or are in the planning stages, according to the Council on Tall Buildings and Urban Habitat. Not one is planned in the West.
If all the projects are realized, by 2020 the global skyline would be dominated by China, which would have nine of the 20 tallest skyscrapers.
Of those, Manhattan would have only the 1,776-foot-high 1 World Trade Center and the Central Park Tower, which is expected to be just a bit shorter when completed in 2019.
New York still has many “supertall” towers in the works. It’s just that the rest of the world has caught up and surpassed it in the last century.
