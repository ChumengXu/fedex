Your Tuesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• The State of the Union.
President Obama will focus his address tonight on American potential and on the need to take on long-term challenges (9 p.m. Eastern). And he’ll offer reassurances that terrorists do not pose an existential threat to the U.S.
The speech will also be used to frame the 2016 campaign. An empty seat in the gallery will symbolize victims of gun violence in the U.S. His guests include a Syrian refugee and a Mexican immigrant.
The White House message machine is in overdrive today. Here’s how Mr. Obama followed through, or didn’t, on earlier State of the Union promises.
• On the campaign trail.
Chelsea Clinton makes her first campaign swing of 2016 on behalf of her mother, Hillary, in New Hampshire today. Polls show Mrs. Clinton and Bernie Sanders in a tight race there and in Iowa.
Jeb Bush may find lessons in his 1994 defeat for Florida’s governorship; Chris Christie is making a rightward shift on guns; and Rand Paul and Carly Fiorina were dropped from this week’s main debate stage.
• Government transparency.
The House is expected to pass legislation today, with majorities of Republicans and Democrats, that would require the government to create a single online portal for open-record requests to any government agency.
Complaints about the handling of requests by journalists, historians and business executives to open records to public scrutiny are routine across the government.
• Justices weigh a setback for unions.
The U.S. Supreme Court’s conservative majority seems ready to say that forcing public workers to support unions they have declined to join violates the First Amendment, based on oral arguments on Monday.
If the plaintiffs win, millions of government workers will be able to opt out of paying for collective bargaining, depriving unions of money and making them less effective.
• Concern over Russian missiles grows.
A U.S. Air Force commander is expressing “very serious” concern over what he described as big buildups of complex Russian missile defenses that increasingly threaten NATO military access to air space in parts of Europe.
At the same time, the U.S. has moved toward building nuclear weapons that are smaller, stealthier and more precise.
BUSINESS
• Dalian Wanda, a Chinese property and entertainment company, is buying Legendary Entertainment, a Hollywood movie producer, for as much as $3.5 billion.
It is one of the biggest purchases of an American company by a Chinese one. Wanda already owns the American cinema chain AMC Theaters.
• With crude oil prices now nearing $30 a barrel, it’s hard to conceive that they were at $130 only eight years ago. Here’s our Q. and A. on oil.
Many analysts expect more declines before prices recover.
• American Apparel, the bankrupt clothing retailer, is weighing a $300 million bid from a group of investors backing the return of Dov Charney, the company’s founder and former chief executive.
• Wall Street futures trading is choppy but flat. European shares are ahead, and most Asian indexes declined.
NOTEWORTHY
• Alabama is crowned.
No. 2 Alabama defeated No.1 Clemson, 45-40, for the College Football Playoff championship on Monday night.
It’s the Crimson Tide’s fourth national title in seven seasons under Coach Nick Saban.
• Rupert Murdoch and Jerry Hall are engaged.
Rupert Murdoch, 84, the executive chairman of News Corporation, and the former model Jerry Hall, 59, got engaged over the weekend. It will be his fourth marriage, and her second.
• Revealing reads.
“The Name of God is Mercy,” by Pope Francis, is a series of conversations with Andrea Tornielli, a Vatican reporter. It’s released today.
And Jane Mayer writes in “Dark Money,” released next week, that the father of the conservative billionaire brothers Charles G. and David H. Koch helped build a Nazi oil refinery.
• Roll out the red carpet.
Tonight is the Los Angeles premiere for “The X-Files,” a six-part series that will air on Fox this month, 13 years after the original run ended. A 21-minute preview was recently released.
The former Heisman Trophy winner and pro football player Eddie George makes his Broadway debut in “Chicago” as Billy Flynn.
• Bowie’s secret.
David Bowie, who in his 50-year career reimagined the worlds of pop music, art and fashion, told very few people about the cancer for which he was being treated before his death on Sunday. Instead, he addressed death in two music videos.
Amazon, iTunes, Google Play and Netflix are streaming movies in tribute to him, including “The Man Who Fell to Earth.”
• A survivor falls.
The last known survivor of the San Francisco earthquake and fires of 1906 died on Monday in Marin County, Calif. William A. Del Monte, who was three months old when the quake struck, was 109.
• Elephants take early retirement.
The Ringling Bros. and Barnum & Bailey Circus will retire all of its touring elephants in May, a year and a half earlier than anticipated.
BACK STORY
N.F.L. team owners are meeting today to discuss whether to let one or two teams move to Los Angeles. The Oakland Raiders, the St. Louis Rams and the San Diego Chargers all want to return to their former home.
The practice of relocating teams is nearly as old as the major sports leagues themselves. But what happens when a name no longer fits? In many cases, you just deal with it.
Take the Utah Jazz. It was apt when they played basketball in New Orleans. Salt Lake City? Not so much.
In Los Angeles, the Lakers kept the name inspired by their former home in Minnesota, “the land of 10,000 lakes.” The Dodgers, born in Brooklyn, are named for residents who were derisively called Trolley Dodgers.
The Calgary Flames refer to the Civil War burning of Atlanta — where the N.H.L. team was founded. The N.B.A.’s Memphis Grizzlies originated in Vancouver, part of a region that’s home to the bears.
The N.F.L.’s Indianapolis Colts kept their name after moving from Baltimore, where a history of horse breeding gave inspiration.
With at least one move, a name just couldn’t survive. The Houston Oilers became the Tennessee Oilers, but after two years playing under the ill-suited name the team switched to the Titans.
Victoria Shannon contributed reporting.
