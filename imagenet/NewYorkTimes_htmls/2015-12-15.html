Your Tuesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Republicans meet again.
Nine presidential hopefuls share the main stage in Las Vegas for the first Republican debate since the attacks in France and San Bernardino, Calif. (8:30 p.m. Eastern, CNN).
The focus has shifted to national security and immigration. Ted Cruz, who has surged to a lead in Iowa, is expected to get a lot of attention, as is Donald J. Trump’s call to bar Muslims from entering the country.
On the Democratic nomination side, Bernie Sanders will be campaigning in New Hampshire, and Hillary Clinton will give a speech in Minnesota detailing her plan to address homegrown terrorism.
• Capitol Hill’s ticking clock.
Funding for the government expires midnight on Wednesday, so Congress must pass a $1.1 trillion spending bill or seek another short-term extension to avoid a government shutdown.
• A pause in Yemen’s war.
Negotiations to end the nine-month conflict began in Switzerland today, as did a cease-fire by the Saudi-led coalition that has been bombing rebel positions in the country. Nearly 6,000 people have been killed.
A new branch of the Islamic State in Yemen appears determined to distinguish itself as the country’s most brutal force, carrying out attacks considered too extreme even by the local branch of Al Qaeda.
• On the president’s calendar.
President Obama will speak today at a naturalization ceremony in Washington, amid a wave of anti-immigrant sentiment in the country.
• U.S. Army charges desertion.
Sgt. Bowe Bergdahl, held by the Taliban for nearly five years, will face a court-martial for desertion and endangering troops.
Sergeant Bergdahl, 29, faces a possible life sentence for his decision to leave his outpost in 2009, a move that set off a huge manhunt in eastern Afghanistan.
• Maryland man held in ISIS plot.
The authorities say a 30-year old Egyptian-born man received at least $8,700 from terrorist operatives overseas and planned to use the money to launch an attack in the U.S.
And the U.S. is working on a plan to examine social media posts for hints of radicalism as part of its visa application process.
• Resenting the neighbors.
A new generation of multimillion-dollar palaces is taking over the wealthiest areas of Los Angeles, but their owners are hidden by shell companies.
Catch up with the rest of our “Towers of Secrecy” series.
• Prison term for insulting a dog?
A factory worker in Thailand was charged with making a “sarcastic” Internet post related to the king’s pet. He could face 37 years in prison.
BUSINESS
• European policy makers are expected today to approve data-protection rules that give people across the region a greater say over how companies use their personal data.
• Samsung’s clash with Apple isn’t over. The South Korean company has filed an appeal to the U.S. Supreme Court, arguing that the legal framework for design patents is outdated.
• Seattle’s City Council voted unanimously to approve a bill allowing drivers for Uber, Lyft and other ride-hailing apps to form unions.
• The New York City public advocate has asked the Securities and Exchange Commission to examine the gun maker Smith & Wesson’s disclosures about how often its products are involved in crimes.
• Craft brews in 16-ounce cans are so popular that there is a shortage of containers for some small breweries.
• Wall Street stock futures are positive. European markets are sharply higher, and Asian indexes finished broadly lower.
NOTEWORTHY
• “Allo, my name is Inigo Montoya … ”
Ted Cruz’s use of the catchphrase, from the classic film “The Princess Bride,” in his presidential campaign is upsetting the actor Mandy Patinkin, who played the master swordsman in the movie.
• Bill Cosby strikes back.
The actor has responded to seven women who accused him of sexual assault and defamation with a defamation suit of his own.
• Which flag to wave?
A black-and-blue flag featuring a silver fern and four red stars was confirmed today as the choice of New Zealanders to challenge the current flag, featuring the Union Jack, in a referendum in March.
• Sports roundup.
Only 11 of the 128 coaches of college teams in the Football Bowl Subdivision are black, a number that is attracting scrutiny in a sport where a majority of the players are black.
Major League Baseball players begin a good-will tour of Cuba today. The league said on Monday that it would continue to bar Pete Rose, the career hits leader, from the Hall of Fame.
The New York Giants beat the Miami Dolphins, 31-24, on Monday Night Football.
• Friendly reminders.
If you want health care coverage beginning on Jan. 1, today is the last day to sign up for plans under the Affordable Care Act.
And the Postal Service recommends sending Parcel Post for Christmas delivery no later than today.
BACK STORY
The original “Star Wars,” whose latest chapter premiered last night in Los Angeles, ranks as the second-highest-grossing movie ever, with lifetime revenue of $1.5 billion (adjusted for inflation).
The first on the list? “Gone With the Wind,” which premiered in Atlanta 76 years ago today, after several days of citywide festivities in its honor.
The movie, which went on to win eight Academy Awards, was a phenomenon even before its opening.
A day before the premiere, 150,000 people lined the sidewalks and rooftops of Atlanta for the arrival of the stars, including Clark Gable and Vivien Leigh.
Confetti and streamers rained down on their open cars, while military school bands repeatedly played “Dixie.” Hotel managers, drugstore clerks, elevator operators and others wore their grandparents’ Civil War finery.
Loew’s Grand Theater on Peachtree Street was given a new facade for the occasion: a wooden overlay of the pillars of Tara, the plantation of the movie.
Not present for the premiere: Hattie McDaniel, who starred as the servant Mammy and who became the first black performer to win an Academy Award. Georgia laws meant the black actors would have had to sit separately from their white counterparts.
News reports tell us that of all the celebrities there, it was the author and Atlanta native Margaret Mitchell who received the biggest applause and the most “rebel yells.”
Victoria Shannon contributed reporting.
