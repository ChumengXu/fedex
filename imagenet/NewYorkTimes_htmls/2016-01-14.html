Your Thursday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Developing: Attacks in Indonesia.
The center of Jakarta, the capital, was rocked by explosions and gunfire today. At least four people were killed in what the authorities described as a terrorist attack. We have live updates.
It was the first major attack in Jakarta since two hotels were bombed in 2009. Indonesia has the world’s largest Muslim population, but its government is secular.
• There are at least three Powerball winners.
Winning tickets for the record $1.5 billion jackpot were sold in California, Florida and Tennessee. And there may be more lucky tickets out there.
The winning combination: 4-8-19-27-34 and the Powerball number, 10.
• Republicans face off.
Tonight’s presidential campaign debate in North Charleston, S.C., is the next to last before the Iowa caucuses on Feb. 1. One big question is whether Donald J. Trump will go after Ted Cruz, who’s leading in polls in Iowa. (9 p.m. Eastern, Fox Business Network and streaming at foxbusiness.com).
Mr. Trump’s campaign in Iowa has made basic organizing errors, and Mr. Cruz did not disclose a loan for as much as $500,000 from Goldman Sachs, where his wife works, that he used for his 2012 Senate campaign.
• Congressional blueprints.
The House speaker, Paul D. Ryan, and the Senate majority leader, Mitch McConnell, will announce their unity against President Obama in his final year in office at a retreat today for congressional Republicans.
But they are pursuing starkly different legislative agendas. Mr. Ryan’s is much more ambitious, reflective of the comfortable majority he enjoys in the House.
• Market turbulence.
The main U.S. indexes are in what Wall Street calls a correction, down 10 percent from their most recent highs, and global markets slid today.
Investors are worrying about the strength of the world’s largest economies.
Here are snapshots of the U.S. and world markets.
• Corruption in sports.
A report to be released today is expected to focus on world track and field officials accused of blackmailing athletes.
The sport was shaken by revelations two months ago of a state-sponsored doping system in Russia.
• Growing divisions over migration crisis.
Anti-immigration groups and right-wing extremists in Europe are seizing on a wave of assaults in Germany to discredit all migrants as criminals or hooligans.
In Turkey, there are growing fears that militants are using migrants to carry out attacks against countries allied against the Islamic State. The suicide bomber who attacked Istanbul on Tuesday entered as a refugee.
• Credit where credit isn’t due.
A federal court in California has ruled that a former Marine can wear service medals he did not earn. It says it’s a form of free speech.
BUSINESS
• JPMorgan Chase is expected to start bank earnings season by reporting fourth-quarter results today. Wells Fargo and Citigroup follow on Friday.
• Al Jazeera America, a cable news channel with rock-bottom ratings, is going off the air.
• Zappos, the online shoe seller, continues to lose employees after it adopted a radical self-management system called Holacracy.
NOTEWORTHY
• Academy Award nominations.
This year’s nominees are announced today at 8:30 a.m. Eastern. The winners will be revealed in a ceremony hosted by Chris Rock on Feb. 28.
The fierce competition in some categories has studios maneuvering to shift their films and actors to other ones.
• New reads.
Among this week’s nonfiction releases: “The Confidence Game,” Maria Konnikova’s look at brilliant con artists; and “The Invitation-Only Zone,” Robert S. Boynton’s chronicle of North Korea’s program to kidnap foreigners.
“My Name Is Lucy Barton,” by Elizabeth Strout, who wrote “Olive Kitteridge,” and “The Expatriates” by Janice Y.K. Lee are new in fiction.
• Remembering David Bowie.
The news of Mr. Bowie’s death this week is still reverberating. Musicians are remembering the support that he gave them early in their careers.
Lesbian, gay, bisexual and transgender fans say they felt inspired by his gender-bending performances and cultural fluidity. And one of our writers recalls when Bowie challenged MTV on race.
• In case you missed it …
Our most popular recent articles include “52 Places to Go in 2016” and the tale of a puppy given to a firehouse that had heavy losses on Sept. 11, 2001.
Also, “Campus Sex … With a Syllabus” and “When Teamwork Doesn’t Work for Women.”
• A teacher’s $1.7 million donation.
Elizabeth Verdow, who died in 2014, spent her career teaching in Detroit public schools, and she left the gift for the Detroit Institute of Arts, where she volunteered for almost two decades.
• That’s highway robbery!
A senior prison official in Russia has been accused of stealing the pavement from a 30-mile stretch of public highway.
BACK STORY
President Obama says that his favorite perk of office is having his own plane, and that he plans to rack up the miles on Air Force One in his final year.
When President Franklin D. Roosevelt took the first airplane journey by a sitting U.S. president, it was nothing like today’s White House in the air.
He touched down on this day in 1943 in Casablanca, Morocco.
Roosevelt was aboard a Boeing 314 Flying Boat, a plane that Pan American used in its commercial transoceanic airline service. Their planes carried 74 passengers, and had a lounge and dining room.
But Roosevelt wasn’t flying for pleasure. He had to meet with Britain’s prime minister, Winston Churchill, to talk wartime strategy.
At the time, German U-boats were sinking so many ships bearing supplies from America to Britain that Churchill called it “the only thing that ever really frightened me during the war.”
Roosevelt’s four-day journey began in Florida. The crew refueled in Trinidad and Brazil. Once across the ocean, in Gambia, the president switched to a smaller plane to complete the final leg to Morocco.
It was at the Casablanca Conference that Roosevelt redefined World War II by calling for “unconditional surrender” by the Axis powers.
