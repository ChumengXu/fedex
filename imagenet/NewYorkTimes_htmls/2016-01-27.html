Your Wednesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Leader of Oregon protest is captured.
Ammon Bundy, the leader of an antigovernment group that has occupied a federal wildlife refuge for more than three weeks, was among those arrested during a traffic stop in which shots were fired on Tuesday night.
A member of his group, which is protesting federal land-management policies, was killed.
• On the campaign stump.
Donald J. Trump says he’ll skip Thursday night’s debate on Fox News, the final one before the Iowa caucuses. His demand that the anchor Megyn Kelly be dropped from the moderators was not met.
Hillary Clinton and Bernie Sanders are still deadlocked in Iowa, a new poll shows, suggesting that the Democratic race in Iowa will be down to the wire.
Mr. Trump will hold a rally in South Carolina today. Bernie Sanders will appear with Susan Sarandon in Iowa; Mrs. Clinton and her husband are also in the state; as are eight Republican candidates.
• Planned Parenthood foes dealt blows.
A grand jury’s indictment on Monday of two abortion opponents who secretly recorded Planned Parenthood officials is the latest sign that a Republican campaign against the group is in trouble.
Investigations in a dozen states have found no wrongdoing by the group’s affiliates. In Congress, efforts to defund the group have failed, and committee investigations of the group have produced no results.
• Egypt’s vanishing citizens.
The government in Cairo is widening its crackdown on hundreds of opponents, real or imagined, with what human rights groups call “enforced disappearances.”
Hundreds are being sent to secretive detention centers, where they are held in isolation for weeks or sometimes months, instead of going through the formal legal system — where tens of thousands have also been detained.
• Obama seeks to combat Zika virus.
President Obama is calling for the rapid development of tests, vaccines and treatments for the mosquito-borne Zika virus. A Virginia resident who traveled outside the U.S. has tested positive.
Here’s our Q. and A. about the Zika virus.
• Police to lose military equipment.
Local authorities in the U.S. must return surplus military equipment, like armored vehicles that run on tracks, .50-caliber machine guns, grenade launchers, bayonets and camouflage clothing, a federal order says. It’s a response to concerns that the gear was unnecessary and misused.
Separately, Cleveland has fired six police officers for their roles in a 2012 car chase that left two unarmed black people dead. More than 100 officers joined in the chase, and one climbed on the hood of the car and repeatedly fired into the windshield.
• Guidance on postpartum depression.
Women should be screened for depression during pregnancy and after childbirth, an influential panel recommends.
Here are some answers to questions about maternal mental illness, which is more common than previously thought.
BUSINESS
• The European Commission is expected to propose plans today to remove six corporate-tax loopholes. They would require unanimous approval by the European Union’s 28 national governments.
• Apple’s results for the fiscal first quarter showed that iPhone sales rose less than 1 percent from a year earlier, a sign that years of explosive revenue growth are over.
• Ahead of the Federal Reserve statement on interest rates today, here are snapshots of the U.S. and world markets.
• The rising dollar is mostly a good thing, economists say, but our “Economic Scene” columnist writes that there are downsides.
NOTEWORTHY
• Holocaust Memorial Day.
Today is the 71st anniversary of the liberation of the Auschwitz concentration camp. President Obama will be at the Israeli Embassy in Washington to deliver an address, and Israel’s president is making public a handwritten request for clemency from the Nazi war criminal Adolf Eichmann.
There’s an exhibition in Berlin of 100 works that were secretly created by 50 Holocaust-era artists.
And The Times remembers David Stoliar, the only survivor among nearly 800 Jews fleeing the Holocaust in Romania aboard a ship that was torpedoed by a Soviet submarine in 1942. He died at 91.
• To honor World War I veterans.
A plan for a war memorial would create a raised lawn with a sculpture on a 1.8-acre site near the White House that is not part of the National Mall.
• In memoriam.
Abe Vigoda, who played the mobster Sal Tessio in “The Godfather,” has died at 94.
• Travel plans?
The State Department is urging Americans whose passports expire this year to renew them now to avoid an anticipated backlog.
BACK STORY
Salzburg, Austria, has been holding a 10-day “Mozart Week” to celebrate the 260th anniversary of its native son’s birth today.
Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart sat down at a keyboard at the age of 3 and just started playing. Two years later, he composed his first piece. At 8, he wrote his first symphony. And his first opera came at age 11.
A prodigy, he spent much of his childhood on tour across Europe with his sister, who was also a musician. The trips, arranged by their father, a violinist and composer himself, strained the boy’s health and may have played a role in shortening his life.
Salzburg may be Mozart’s hometown, but he left it for the music capital of Vienna, where he reached his peak as a pianist and composer. It was there in his final decade that he composed many of his best-known pieces.
All told, he wrote 626 works, from simple minuets to his Requiem Mass, which was left unfinished when he died right before his 35th birthday.
