Your Tuesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Cleveland braces for protests.
Demonstrations are expected today after a grand jury voted not to indict a white police officer in Cleveland for fatally shooting Tamir Rice, a 12-year-old black boy who was carrying what turned out to be a pellet gun.
Lawyers for Tamir’s family have called for a special prosecutor in the case and have asked the Justice Department to investigate.
The decision caps more than a year of investigations into the shooting, which added to national outrage over white officers killing African-Americans.
• Where the candidates are.
Bernie Sanders will be in Iowa for the rest of the week, and he’ll ring in the new year in Des Moines. His wife, Jane, may be the most politically active and experienced spouse in the 2016 presidential election, with the exception of Bill Clinton, of course.
Hillary Clinton will hold a town-hall-style event today in New Hampshire. And that’s where Donald J. Trump, who is trying to make Mr. Clinton’s marital infidelity a political issue, will hold a rally today.
• Severe weather.
Many flights are delayed or canceled across the U.S. today because of severe weather. Travelers should check their flight status.
Texas, pummeled over the weekend by the deadliest tornado system in 88 years, is still trying to tally the damage.
• Mending Syria.
Several hundred wounded militants and their families, from opposing sides of the civil war, have been evacuated from besieged areas. It’s part of an attempt to calm the conflict before peace talks next month.
And while extremists have been trying to destroy ancient sites across Iraq and Syria, cultural groups are trying to create 3-D records of the heritage sites to preserve them, at least in digital form, for future generations.
• Texas fugitive held in Mexico.
Ethan Couch, 18, who killed four people in 2013 while driving drunk and who disappeared before a court hearing that could have sent him to prison, has been arrested near the resort city of Puerto Vallarta.
He was granted leniency in the case after a witness testified that he suffered from “affluenza,” or psychological problems stemming from his family’s wealth.
• Ebola milestone.
The World Health Organization declared Guinea free of Ebola virus transmission today. The epidemic is believed to have begun in that country two years ago.
Experts say, though, that there could be sporadic cases. The outbreak killed more than 11,000 people and sickened more than 28,000 across 10 countries.
• Journalists who died on the job.
The Committee to Protect Journalists reported today that 69 journalists were killed while working in 2015. Syria, unsurprisingly, was the most dangerous country.
Highlighting that danger, a Syrian journalist and filmmaker who documented atrocities by the Islamic State was killed on Sunday in a Turkish town near Syria. Rights activists are calling the death an assassination.
BUSINESS
• Hedge funds are facing as much as double-digit losses this year, and managers will be under intense pressure to perform in 2016.
The steep declines come as some investors openly question the value of hedge funds in a portfolio, in light of their hefty fee structure.
• The chairman and co-founder of the mining giant Freeport-McMoRan will step down after months of pressure from the billionaire investor Carl C. Icahn. Its shares fell more than 9 percent.
• The Standard & Poor’s/Case-Shiller survey of home prices, out this morning, will most likely show that average U.S. prices in October were up 5.5 percent from a year earlier.
• Stock markets around the world are rising today.
NOTEWORTHY
• Ounce of prevention isn’t worth a lot.
There’s more money to be made investing in drugs that will extend cancer patients’ lives by a few months than in drugs that would prevent cancer in the first place, new research shows.
That’s why preventing cancer isn’t the priority in drug development.
• Kennedy Center honors.
Stephen Colbert hosts the annual tribute to cultural titans tonight. This year’s honorees are Rita Moreno, Carole King, Seiji Ozawa, George Lucas and Cicely Tyson.
James Taylor, Steven Spielberg and Yo-Yo Ma are among those paying tributes (9 p.m. Eastern, CBS).
• Health checkup.
The links between regular exercise and improvements in our thinking and brain structure were among the biggest themes in fitness science this year.
And if you’re interested in improving your physical health and emotional and social well-being, you should try to stop slouching.
• “Otherworldly.”
Sci-fi and fantasy are the focus of our new column of book reviews, published today. This week, we like the absurdist Charlie Jane Anders and her new “All the Birds in the Sky.”
• On the gridiron.
Denver recovered a fumbled snap in overtime, sealing the Broncos’ 20-17 win over the Cincinnati Bengals, on Monday Night Football.
And look out for the Arizona Cardinals when the playoffs arrive. They’re the N.F.L. most dominant team, according to the numbers.
• In memoriam.
Ian Fraser Kilmister, known as Lemmy, the lead singer of the British rock band Motörhead, died on Monday at age 70.
Dave Henderson, a hero for the pennant-winning 1986 Boston Red Sox, died on Sunday at 57.
BACK STORY
The Carolina Panthers were foiled on Sunday in their bid to become the third N.F.L. team in the modern era to finish a regular season undefeated.
In 1972, the Miami Dolphins finished 14-0, and on this day in 2007, the New England Patriots finished a longer season at 16-0.
A major-league baseball team would have to win 162 consecutive games, while N.B.A. and N.H.L. teams would need 82 wins for the same feat.
That’s why perfection is so rare in the major American team sports.
Overseas, Arsenal impressively won 26 games, but had 12 ties, during its unbeaten 2003-4 English Premier League season.
But it’s also hard to be perfectly bad. Only the Detroit Lions finished 0-16, in 2008, and the Tampa Bay Buccaneers were 0-14 in 1976.
The Dallas Cowboys, in their inaugural season in 1960, didn’t win a game, but they had one tie.
Of course, a perfect regular season doesn’t mean a perfect ending, as Patriots fans learned painfully during the 2007-8 playoffs. Disappointed Panthers fans still have their Super Bowl dreams.
