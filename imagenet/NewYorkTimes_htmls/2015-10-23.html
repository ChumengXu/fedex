Your Friday Briefing
By VICTORIA SHANNON
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Battle over Benghazi.
Republican lawmakers spent more than eight hours aggressively questioning Hillary Rodham Clinton over the 2012 attacks on the U.S. diplomatic mission in Benghazi, Libya.
But the long day of often-testy exchanges revealed little new about the episode, which has already been the subject of seven investigations.
Watch the Benghazi hearing in three minutes.
• Ryan’s hat is in the ring.
Representative Paul D. Ryan said he would seek to replace John A. Boehner as House speaker next week after two more factions of House Republicans endorsed him.
• Campaign update for Democrats.
Lincoln Chafee, the struggling Democratic presidential candidate, said he would address the future of his campaign at the Democratic National Committee’s Women’s Leadership Forum.
President Obama, Mrs. Clinton, Senator Bernie Sanders and Martin O’Malley also speak at the forum today. On Saturday, the Democratic candidates will be together again at the Jefferson Jackson Dinner in Iowa.
• Mexico braces for Patricia.
Hurricane Patricia, a Category 5 storm with winds reaching 200 miles per hour, is heading toward Mexico.
It is expected to make what the U.S. National Hurricane Center called a “potentially catastrophic landfall” on the country’s southern Pacific Coast this afternoon or evening.
• Heading to court.
Up to 25 states and influential business groups may start legal action to block President Obama’s climate change regulations when they are formally published today.
His signature environmental policy is widely expected to end up before the Supreme Court, even as many states and companies have started drafting plans to comply with the rules.
• Deadly bus crash.
Forty-two people, mostly members of a seniors club, died in a bus collision with a truck in southwest France today.
BUSINESS
• Wall Street indexes open today bolstered by Thursday’s gain of almost 2 percent, mostly on the back of positive earnings results and hints about further stimulus from the European Central Bank.
And that was before three big tech companies — Amazon, Google and Microsoft — issued mostly positive quarterly numbers.
Stock futures indicate more gains in New York today. European and Asian shares are mostly higher.
• U.S. auto safety regulators investigating defective airbags made by Takata are now examining its side airbags, not just those made for the driver and the front-seat passenger.
• Imprimis Pharmaceuticals, a San Diego drugmaker, is offering a $1 alternative to the HIV drug whose price increase to $750 a tablet caused public outrage.
• Christian Dior, the Paris couture house, is looking for an artistic director after Raf Simons said he was leaving.
NOTEWORTHY
• Down to the wire.
The New York Mets play Game 1 of the World Series on Tuesday, but we don’t know yet whether it will be in Kansas City or Toronto.
The home-field advantage goes to the American League champion; that could be decided by the Royals-Blue Jays game tonight or, if needed, on Saturday.
Separately, the onetime Yankee Don Mattingly is out after five years as manager of the Los Angeles Dodgers. He finished with a winning record in every season, but the Dodgers have not reached the World Series since 1988.
• New to the silver screen.
In a video, our critics review three new movies: “Suffragette,” “Jem and the Holograms” and “Heart of a Dog.”
Here are other movies out in theaters today.
• Hands-free distracted driving.
Voice-activated technology in cars can take a motorist’s mind off the road for 15 to 27 seconds after he or she stops interacting with the system, new research found.
• The death of Bart Simpson.
The celebrated annual “Treehouse of Horror” edition of “The Simpsons” airs Sunday night, and Fox has already let slip that Bart will be killed by his longtime nemesis, Sideshow Bob.
• Best-selling books.
Elvis Costello’s memoir, “Unfaithful Music and Disappearing Ink,” enters the Times hardcover nonfiction list at No. 9.
Get an early look at all of our best-seller lists.
• Weekend sports.
Sunday is the 40th Marine Corps Marathon in Washington. Among the racers will be Al Richmond, 74, who has run in all of them.
And here are our N.F.L. previews and picks for Week No. 7.
• Never mind.
The billionaire Sanford Weill and his wife, Joan, will not donate $20 million to a struggling northern New York college after a judge ruled that the school could not be renamed for Mrs. Weill.

BACK STORY
With Paul McCartney, Mick Jagger and other rock stars of their era still performing and recording as septuagenarians, it should not be a surprise that Rod Stewart, 70, has a new album out today.
“Another Country” is his 29th studio album, said to consist mostly of folk-influenced original songs recorded in his Beverly Hills mansion.
Mr. Stewart’s star has dimmed and brightened since his breakout hit, “Maggie May,” in 1971. Rolling Stone magazine in 1980 called him a “bilious self-parody,” while conceding that he “sells more records than ever.”
About a decade ago, his sales soared back to 1970s levels, as he released collections of 1930s and ’40s pop standards from Cole Porter, George Gershwin, Irving Berlin and the like. His lifetime album sales exceed 100 million.
Away from music, he is known for his serial relationships with models and actresses, his rabid enthusiasm for soccer and his extensive model railroad layouts. But he likes to say that he’s now a family man — he has a four-year-old granddaughter — and he’s still touring.
“As you grow older, you become aware that you’re running out of time,” he told the British newspaper The Sun. “That makes you look back at the past and that’s what I’ve brought out in some songs.”
