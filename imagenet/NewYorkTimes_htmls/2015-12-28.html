Your Monday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Deadly weather.
The cleanup continues this morning in the southern U.S., after tornadoes swept through North Texas on Saturday night, killing at least 11 people (slide show).
Ten people were confirmed to have died in Mississippi, tornadoes also struck Tennessee, Arkansas and Alabama, and flooding claimed 13 lives in Missouri and Illinois.
The weather-related death toll is at least 43.
• U.S. military’s footprint is growing.
The White House, which has steadily expanded the global missions of American Special Operations, is now considering a Pentagon proposal to maintain at least one base in Afghanistan for years to come.
That’s despite President Obama repeatedly saying that he opposes American “boots on the ground” in far-flung parts of the world.
• Iraqi forces retake center of Ramadi.
Military officials in Baghdad today said they had seized the local government complex in Ramadi after six days of fighting to retake it after a brutal seven-month occupation by the Islamic State extremist group.
Officials cautioned that sporadic fighting continued elsewhere in the city.
Control over Ramadi would allow Iraq to cut off supplies to Falluja and make it difficult for the militants to hold that city.
• More scrutiny of Chicago’s police.
Chicago’s mayor, Rahm Emanuel, is calling on the police department and an independent police review board to immediately review training programs that guide officers on how to handle calls involving mental health crises.
Officers fatally shot two black residents on Saturday, while the city’s police department is already the subject of a federal investigation over its use of force. Mr. Emanuel is the subject of increasing criticism.
• All’s not quiet in Washington.
The House is on a break until next Monday, and the Supreme Court and the Senate are not back until the week of Jan. 11.
In the interim, a gulf is opening among Republicans: those in Congress who are convinced that the Affordable Care Act is collapsing and determined to help it fail, and Republican governors willing to expand Medicaid coverage through that law.
• A deal on wartime “comfort women.”
South Korea and Japan agreed today to resolve the long dispute over Korean women who were lured or forced into sexual slavery for Japanese soldiers in the 1930s and ’40s.
Under the terms, Japan will pay for services that support the women.
• A Syrian family’s tragedy.
The death of Aylan Kurdi, the young child whose body washed up on a beach in Turkey in September and who drew world attention to the plight of refugees, is just one chapter of a sprawling family’s desperate attempts to escape the war in Syria.
We tracked the family and found that the Kurdis, originally from Damascus and Kobani, are now spread across five different countries.
Peace talks between the Syrian government and rebel forces are set for late January.
BUSINESS
• Stock markets around the world are opening 2015’s last trading week not far from where they started the year.
That could leave U.S. stocks with their worst annual performance since 2011.
In China today, equities tumbled more than 2 percent, their biggest loss in a month, as an overhaul of the way companies will be listed weighed on the market.
• North American movie ticket sales are expected to set a record this year, exceeding $11 billion, according to Rentrak, which compiles box-office data.
The total is being bolstered by “Star Wars: The Force Awakens,” which was dominant at the box office again over the weekend. It has surpassed $500 million in domestic ticket sales and $1 billion worldwide.
OVER THE WEEKEND
• India’s prime minister dropped in on Pakistan’s prime minister, who was celebrating his birthday, in a surprise diplomatic mission.
• The mob killing of an Afghan woman in March and the legal system’s response is calling into question more than a decade of Western efforts in Afghanistan to instill a rule of law and to improve the status of women.
• The number of U.S. students earning high school diplomas is at a historic peak, yet measures of academic readiness for college or jobs are much lower.
• Foreign arms sales by the U.S. jumped by almost $10 billion in 2014, about 35 percent, even as the global weapons market remained flat.
• Peyton Manning, the Denver Broncos quarterback, denied using human growth hormone, a banned substance in the N.F.L., after a report linked him to doping.

• In memoriam.
Meadowlark Lemon, the Harlem Globetrotters’ top showman for more than two decades, died Sunday at age 83.

The Oscar-winning cinematographer Haskell Wexler (“Medium Cool” and “Who’s Afraid of Virginia Woolf?”) died Sunday at 93.

“East of Salinas,” a film in PBS’s “Independent Lens” series, is a glimpse into the lives of undocumented immigrants in the U.S. (10 p.m. Eastern, but check local listings).
And “Heroin: Cape Cod, U.S.A.” follows eight young addicts who live in what most people consider an idyllic locale (9 p.m. Eastern, HBO).
BACK STORY
“I think we’ll be just one of a crowd.”
That’s what the mother of Elizabeth Jordan Carr, born on this day in 1981, said when she arrived home in Westminster, Mass.
She was right. At the time, Elizabeth, the first baby in the U.S. to have been conceived outside a mother’s body, was national news. (More than a dozen others had already been born globally.)
Today, record numbers are seeking in vitro fertilization.
But the treatment represents a small fraction — about 61,000 babies, less than 2 percent — of the approximately four million annual births in the country.
In that first in vitro (“in glass”) fertilization, done by a husband-and-wife medical team, an egg was removed from Elizabeth’s mother and fertilized in a lab dish with sperm from her husband.
It was successful on the 42nd try. The newly created embryo was then inserted into the mother’s womb, and she had a normal pregnancy.
A study published this month found that nearly two-thirds of women undergoing I.V.F. will have a child by the sixth attempt.
And now, scientists at Cornell University say the technique has been used for the first time to bring a litter of puppies into the world.
