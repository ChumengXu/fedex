Your Friday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• A reprieve in the markets?
Many global stock indexes crept into positive territory today after two days of routs, with Wall Street likely to open higher this morning.
But the fallout remains. The Dow Jones industrial average is 10 percent below last year’s record high.
And China’s attempts to manage its outsize economy have flailed since the summer, with cascading effects around the world. A lot of the turmoil has been over currency devaluations.
We round up some index-card advice on managing your finances and how not to overreact to volatility in the markets.
• It’s also jobs day.
This morning’s U.S. unemployment report is expected to confirm that a healthy labor market generated more than 200,000 jobs in December.
That would keep a moderately stable U.S. economy at odds with much of the rest of the world, leaving experts to ponder whether the imbalance can last.
• Tough questions for the president.
Supporters of gun rights repeatedly pressed President Obama to justify his gun control efforts in a town-hall-style meeting broadcast on CNN on Thursday.
In a Times Op-Ed, Mr. Obama writes that he will not back any presidential candidate who does not support common-sense gun reform.
The stock prices of some gun makers have risen more since 2009 than shares of Apple, driven by surging sales linked to fears of regulation.
• The race for the White House.
Candidates and the outside groups supporting them are still betting on TV ads, despite signs that they are increasingly less important. We break down the 2016 campaign ads.
An overflow crowd greeted Donald J. Trump on Thursday in Burlington, Vt., Bernie Sanders’s backyard.
And Gov. Paul R. LePage of Maine, the first major elected official to endorse Chris Christie, made racially charged remarks this week about drug dealers impregnating “white girls” in Maine.
• Iraqi refugees arrested in U.S.
Two refugees from Iraq were arrested on terrorism-related charges in California and Texas, where the Republican governor wants to end the resettlement of migrants from countries overrun by terrorist groups.
Separately, New York’s mayor will appoint an independent civilian to monitor the Police Department’s surveillance tactics, as the city tries to settle lawsuits over the targeting of Muslims.
• Iran versus Saudi Arabia.
The Saudi defense minister tells The Economist that a conflict with Iran would be “the beginning of a major catastrophe in the region.”
• U.S. warns China on North Korea.
The Obama administration told China that its approach to reining in North Korea had “not worked” and that the time had come to end “business as usual.”
The U.S. is drafting a resolution for the U.N. Security Council that would impose sanctions on North Korean trade and finance.
• Sister of Iran’s last shah is dead.
Princess Ashraf Pahlavi, the shah’s twin sister, who was known for her diplomatic skill and sharp political instincts, died on Thursday at 96.
BUSINESS
• Samsung, a superpower in smartphones and electronic components, said today its operating profit increased about 15 percent in the fourth quarter, a sharp slowdown from the previous period.
• Campbell Soup will become the first major food company to disclose the use of genetically engineered ingredients in its products.
NOTEWORTHY
• New dietary guidelines.
The government is urging Americans to limit added sugars to 10 percent of daily calories to cut the risk of obesity, heart disease, Type 2 diabetes and some types of cancer.
It’s also calling out men and teenage boys for eating too much meat and eggs.
• At the movies.
We begin our look at the Oscar race, which ends Feb. 28, with questions about the power of documentaries and how close other films, like “The Big Short,” must stay to a story’s truth.
Here’s what’s coming to theaters today.
• Best sellers.
The social psychologist Amy Cuddy’s “Presence” is new to our advice, how-to and miscellaneous list.
Get an early look at all our best-seller lists from the Sunday Book Review.
• Wild-card weekend.
The N.F.L. playoffs begin on Saturday. The bottom four seeds in each conference are in action. The Broncos, Patriots, Panthers and Cardinals earned byes as the top seeds. Here’s your guide to watching.
Only seven players this season rushed for the 1,000-yard benchmark, the lowest number since 1991.
• In case you missed it …
Among readers’ favorites this week were an explanation of how Sunni and Shia Islam differ, and features on Donald J. Trump’s family history, what daily life is like in an Antarctic village and how “Detroit Cool” is spreading.
BACK STORY
He spoke of protecting borders and immigration reform, and appealed to Congress for cooperation.
Parts of what was in effect the first State of the Union address — given on this day in 1790 in what was then the capital, New York City — feel relevant today, if not for President Washington’s use of verbose 18th-century English.
His opening line: “I embrace with great satisfaction the opportunity which now presents itself of congratulating you on the present favorable prospects of our public affairs.”
He was especially pleased that North Carolina had become the 12th state, and that the government’s “credit and respectability” were growing. He also pushed for support of scientific progress and higher education.
Scholars say the Annual Message to Congress, as it was called until 1945, harks back to the British Parliament opening with the Speech From the Throne.
John Adams followed the practice of delivering a speech, which his successor, Thomas Jefferson, promptly gave up.
Jefferson put his message to Congress in writing in 1801, and the practice continued until Woodrow Wilson traveled to the Capitol to speak to a joint session of Congress in 1913, restoring the tradition.
It will continue on Tuesday, when President Obama makes his final State of the Union address.
