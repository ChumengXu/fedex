Your Thursday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Paris and Brussels on edge.
Prime Minister Manuel Valls of France said today the country cannot rule out a risk of a chemical attack, Belgian authorities raided six locations in Brussels this morning, and U.S. officials warned of possible attacks in Rome.
We still don’t know this morning whether the person thought to have organized the attack, Abdelhamid Abaaoud, is dead or alive, despite a deadly raid near Paris on Wednesday.
• Syrian refugees in the U.S.
The House is expected to vote today to suspend the Syrian refugee program in the U.S. in response to the deadly attacks in Paris.
President Obama said he would veto such legislation. Up to six of the assailants in the recent assault were Europeans who had traveled to Syria, although none are confirmed to have been Syrian citizens.
• Taking on ISIS.
President Obama, in the Philippines today, said that Islamic State militants would not be eliminated until there is a political settlement in Syria.
The attacks have drawn the U.S., Russia and France toward a possible alliance against the terrorist group, but competing national interests remain.
The militants circulated a picture of an explosive device, which looks like a soda can, that they said was used to blow up a Russian plane over the Sinai Peninsula last month, killing 224 people.
• Democratic candidates on the stump.
Hillary Rodham Clinton is set to give a major address at 10 a.m. at the Council on Foreign Relations in New York about her national security proposals and how she would fight Islamic State militants.
And Bernie Sanders, a Vermont independent, will address the subject of his political philosophy, Democratic Socialism, in a speech at Georgetown. He will also speak about the Paris attacks.
• A rising political voice.
A number of groups under the Black Lives Matter banner are trying to influence politics nationwide. The group was born after prominent police killings of blacks in the U.S.
In Chicago, more than 99 percent of the thousands of misconduct complaints against the police this year resulted in no officer being punished.
BUSINESS
• Stock in the mobile-payments company Square opens for trading at $9 today, less than the company had wanted.
And Match Group, the owner of online dating sites including Tinder, starts trading today at $12, the bottom end of the range it sought.
• The deciding votes in a new labor agreement between the United Automobile Workers and Ford are in the hands today of workers at three large assembly plants in Michigan and Illinois.
As of late Wednesday, workers rejecting it had a slight majority.
• Environmental Protection Agency officials plan to meet with Volkswagen today to review the company’s proposed solutions for its emissions-cheating problem before a U.S. deadline of Friday.
VW is also expected to announce cost cuts on Friday to deal with the financial impact of the crisis, which involves about 11 million vehicles worldwide.
• In light of the Paris attacks, leading U.S. law enforcement officials want to reopen the argument that their officers need court approval to gain access to encrypted information on smartphones.
Apple’s “full disk encryption” prohibits anyone except the owner from accessing an iPhone’s contents without a password.
• Wall Street stock futures indicate further gains today. Global markets were higher by 1 percent or more.
NOTEWORTHY
• Kick the habit?
Today is the Great American Smokeout, when the American Cancer Society encourages smokers to quit cigarettes for at least 24 hours.
President Obama tells the magazine GQ that he hasn’t had a cigarette in five years — since the Affordable Care Act, his health care law, passed.
• Vintage 2015.
Beaujolais Nouveau, the first wine of this year’s harvest from the Beaujolais region, can be legally sold as of 12:01 a.m. today.
A French law dating to 1951 says the wine cannot be released earlier than the third Thursday of November each year, which is known as Beaujolais Nouveau Day.
• Baseball’s best.
Major League Baseball’s most valuable players are announced today at 6 p.m. Eastern on MLB Network.
On Wednesday, the Jake Arrieta of the Chicago Cubs and Dallas Keuchel of the Houston Astros each won a Cy Young Award, for best pitcher in their leagues.
• A work for our times.
Ta-Nehisi Coates won the National Book Award for nonfiction for “Between the World and Me,” a blunt exploration of his experience of being a black man in the U.S..
The fiction award went to Adam Johnson for “Fortune Smiles,” a collection of surreal and comic short stories.
• What’s new to watch.
“The Art of More,” the first original drama on the streaming service Crackle, is a behind-the-scenes story about high-end auction houses. Ten episodes become available today.
“The casting is delicious, the characters and their stories grow more complex with each episode,” our reviewer writes.
BACK STORY
On four consecutive Thursdays 100 years ago, Einstein presented his general theory of relativity — which explains what gravity is — to the Prussian Academy of Sciences in Berlin.
The final form of the equation that Einstein revealed in the last of those four lectures became one of science’s monumental achievements.
One of its proposals was that gravity, especially when strong, slows time and bends space. The amount of slowing and bending depends on how much matter and energy are present.
So watches run slightly faster on the top story of a building than at street level because they are farther from the center of the earth, in a weaker gravitational field. And the sun’s strong gravity bends space enough for stars to appear out of place from certain vantage points during an eclipse.
One unconfirmed anecdote says that Einstein told his secretary to tell this to callers asking what the theory means:
“When you sit with a nice girl for two hours, you think it’s only a minute, but when you sit on a hot stove for a minute, you think it’s two hours. That’s relativity.”
But what Einstein really explained is that there is no fixed frame of reference in the universe and that everything is moving relative to everything else.
