Your Thursday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• A small but significant rise.
The Federal Reserve signaled its confidence in the U.S. economy by raising short-term interest rates, which had been near zero for seven years.
Rates on credit cards, home equity loans and credit lines will probably rise slightly. Here’s more on what the rate increase means for you.
Markets worldwide rose in response, and Wall Street stock futures are positive.
• Policing across the U.S.
A circuit court judge is to meet privately today with prosecutors and defense attorneys, a day after the trial of William G. Porter, one of six Baltimore police officers charged in the death of Freddie Gray, ended in a hung jury. The mistrial could complicate the prosecutions of the other officers charged.
Chicago’s mayor, Rahm Emanuel, is to meet today with federal investigators examining his city’s police department. An officer there was indicted by a grand jury on Wednesday over the shooting of a black teenager in 2014, The Chicago Tribune reported.
And Ferguson, Mo., is near a deal to overhaul its police force, which would head off a Justice Department lawsuit alleging years of unconstitutional law enforcement.
• Putin meets the press.
President Vladimir V. Putin of Russia held his year-end news conference in Moscow.
• On Capitol Hill.
The House is set to vote today on the first component of a huge, year-end spending and tax package.
The $1.1 trillion measure makes some tax breaks permanent, increasing the deficit by $500 billion, and it surpasses previous spending limits by $66 billion.
• Obama’s focus on security.
President Obama visits the National Counterterrorism Center, where he’ll speak at about 12:15 p.m. Eastern today.
• Visas under scrutiny.
The House Oversight Committee holds a hearing on visa-vetting programs today. Officials from the Department of Homeland Security and the State Departmfoent will testify.
Tashfeen Malik, one of the suspects in the San Bernardino, Calif., shooting, passed background checks required to move to the U.S. The State Department will hold a news briefing on the screening process for the K-1 visa, which Ms. Malik had received.
• Cruz versus Rubio.
Ted Cruz and Marco Rubio, conservative Cuban-American candidates who make a point of playing down their ethnic identity, are increasingly gunning for each other.
• A strike at terrorists’ funding.
The U.N. Security Council is expected today to adopt a resolution to strengthen sanctions against those who do business with terrorist groups, mainly the Islamic State.
• A fateful protest.
Five years ago today, Mohamed Bouazizi, a Tunisian, set himself on fire to protest the confiscation of fruit and vegetables he was selling. It began the Arab Spring, which later led to the Syrian civil war.
• Maiden names not allowed.
Japan’s highest court upheld a law that requires married couples to share the same surname.
• In memoriam.
Walter J. Leonard, a pioneer of affirmative action in Harvard admissions, died at 86.
His formula was emulated across the U.S., opening colleges and universities to more women and minorities.
BUSINESS
• European anti-fraud investigators are looking into whether Volkswagen misused hundreds of millions of dollars in low-interest loans — threatening a significant source of funding for the crisis-struck automaker.
• Sheldon Adelson, the billionaire casino magnate and Republican donor, is behind the $140 million purchase of The Las Vegas Review-Journal, two people familiar with the matter said.
• Internet start-ups are trying to put human intermediaries back at the center of how we make decisions, whether for travel, home services or shopping.
NOTEWORTHY
• The president tests his survival skills.
President Obama appears tonight on “Running Wild With Bear Grylls.” He and Mr. Grylls trek around a vast glacier in Alaska, observing the effects of climate change and discussing life at the White House (10 p.m. Eastern, NBC).
• At the movies.
“Star Wars: The Force Awakens” will be released in North America early Friday, but it’s already in theaters in some countries.
And relatives of the former N.F.L. player Dave Duerson accused the makers of the film “Concussion” of inventing encounters that portrayed him as a villain, but the director defended the movie, starring Will Smith, which opens Christmas Day.
• Classic acts.
Cheap Trick, N.W.A, Chicago, Steve Miller and Deep Purple are the 2016 inductees into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, it was announced today.
Meanwhile, Michael Jackson’s album “Thriller,” released in 1982, has become the first album to sell 30 million copies in the U.S.
• When the Wright brothers took flight.
Orville and Wilbur Wright made their historic flight near Kitty Hawk, N.C., on this day in 1903. The craft stayed aloft for about 12 seconds, and it was years before the brothers’ achievement was recognized.
• Sweet stuff.
Today is National Maple Syrup Day — here are some recipes for the occasion.
BACK STORY
It’s hard to imagine the holiday season without the cultural feast of glittering fairies, toys and sweets that come alive with the dancing and music of “The Nutcracker.”
The ballet, with a score by Tchaikovsky, premiered in St. Petersburg, Russia, 123 years ago on Friday.
It might never have happened. Tchaikovsky’s parents sent him to law school because they didn’t consider music an acceptable profession. Later, he quit a legal job to attend the St. Petersburg Conservatory, the foundation of an illustrious career that included “Swan Lake” and “Sleeping Beauty.”
The “Nutcracker” tale isn’t entirely Russian, though. It’s based on an 1816 story, “Nutcracker and Mouse King,” by the German writer E. T. A. Hoffmann, and it was adapted and popularized by the French writer Alexandre Dumas in 1844 as “The Tale of a Nutcracker.”
Decades later, the St. Petersburg Opera hired Tchaikovsky to write music for a ballet based on the later version. No performances today faithfully follow the staging first done in St. Petersburg.
Tchaikovsky’s scores are among the most famous in ballet, and they lend much of the magic to the holiday fairy tale that ends happily ever after.
