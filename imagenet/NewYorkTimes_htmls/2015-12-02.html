Your Wednesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• New vows to fight ISIS.
The British Parliament is expected to vote today on Prime Minister David Cameron’s request for the Royal Air Force to join airstrikes against Islamic State militants in Syria.
At the same time, the Pentagon will expand its Special Operations force against the extremists in both Syria and Iraq.
Separately, Iraqi forces, backed by U.S.-led airstrikes, are preparing to take back Ramadi, as the Islamic State’s promise to provide actual governance appears to be faltering.
• A disappearing land.
As the legal and financial terms of a major U.N. climate change accord are being negotiated in Paris, low-lying nations like the Marshall Islands, in the Pacific, are being inundated by rising sea levels.
The 70,000 residents of the Marshalls, because of their long military ties to Washington, are free to emigrate to the U.S., a prospect that grows more likely as their land vanishes.
• On Capitol Hill.
Congress passed two measures in an attempt to undercut promises that President Obama, who’s back in Washington today, made at the Paris climate conference. But he says he will veto the bills.
And congressional negotiators sealed a deal for a five-year, $300 billion transportation bill that would inject much-needed investments into the nation’s infrastructure. The legislation will be voted on this week by the House and Senate.
• Zuckerberg’s great giveaway.
Mark Zuckerberg, the co-founder and chief executive of Facebook, said that he and his wife would give 99 percent of their Facebook shares — holdings currently worth more than $45 billion — to charitable concerns “during our lives.”
It’s the latest sign of a growing interest in philanthropy among Silicon Valley’s billionaires, who, unlike previous generations of tycoons, want to spread their wealth while they are still young.
• Top Republicans’ growing worries.
Irritation is giving way to panic in the Republican establishment as it becomes increasingly possible that Donald J. Trump could be the party’s presidential nominee, perhaps imperiling the chances of Republicans running for other offices.
Many leading officials, strategists and donors now say they fear that his nomination would lead to an electoral wipeout in November.
A new national poll finds Mr. Trump and Hillary Clinton solidifying their positions in their respective races.
• A portrait of rage.
Robert L. Dear Jr., who is accused of killing three people and wounding nine others with a semiautomatic rifle at a Planned Parenthood clinic in Colorado Springs, was a man of religious conviction but also angry and sometimes violent, according to court documents and those who knew him well.
• Campus protests.
The tense atmosphere at some U.S. colleges could affect the legal dynamic when a major case, which could put an end to racial preferences in college admissions, is argued at the Supreme Court next week.
And Harvard Law School’s official seal is under scrutiny because it includes elements drawn from a slaveholding family’s crest.
BUSINESS
• The board of Yahoo will discuss potentially selling its core Internet business during meetings this week, people briefed on the plans say.
• Janet L. Yellen, the Federal Reserve chairwoman, addresses the Economic Club of Washington this afternoon, where she may again signal that the Fed is finally ready this month to start raising its benchmark interest rate, which it has held near zero since December 2008.
• Nokia shareholders are expected to approve today the $16.6 billion takeover of the company’s French-American rival Alcatel-Lucent.
• Wall Street stock futures are trending positive. European markets are higher, and Asian indexes finished mixed.
NOTEWORTHY
• American jailed in Peru heads home.
Lori Berenson, the American who has completed a 20-year sentence for aiding a rebel group that planned to take members of Peru’s Congress hostage, could be deported as early as today.
• Sports roundup.
David Price has reached an agreement with the Boston Red Sox on a seven-year, $217 million deal that would be the richest for a pitcher in major league history.
The Philadelphia 76ers won their first game of the season on Tuesday, beating the Los Angeles Lakers, 103-91, and avoiding a 19th straight loss, which would have been an N.B.A. record.
• A new edition of Hitler’s book.
As the copyright of “Mein Kampf” expires, a team of historians has prepared a heavily annotated version meant to put the notorious manifesto into historical context.
• Ebony’s honorees.
Ebony magazine celebrates its Power 100 list tonight in Los Angeles, ending with a special recognition of the publication’s 70th anniversary.
Among those honored: Lester Holt, Russell Westbrook, Loretta E. Lynch, Trevor Noah, Viola Davis and Drake.
• U2 heads back to Paris.
The band is looking to bring support to the city after the terrorist attacks.
• Twice a winner.
A California man won a $1,000 lottery prize and immediately bought more tickets — winning $10 million more.
BACK STORY
With climate change in focus this week and next, let’s take a moment to recognize someone who predicted human-induced climate change as early as 1800.
That was when Alexander von Humboldt, a Prussian explorer, naturalist and scientist, began to recognize a link between cutting down trees and the falling water level of Lake Valencia in Venezuela.
After he recorded the environmental effects of colonial plantations there, he warned of catastrophic consequences for mankind, Andrea Wulf writes in “The Invention of Nature.”
Humboldt also created isotherms, those lines you see on weather maps that connect points with the same temperature.
Isotherms became an easy way to spot global heating and cooling patterns that hadn’t been apparent. Scientists still use them to track climate change and global warming.
In 1829, Humboldt called for scientists around the world to record data on the effects of deforestation.
It prompted the first large-scale study of climate and was the earliest global effort to deal with climate change, nearly 200 years before today’s diplomatic efforts to stop global warming.
