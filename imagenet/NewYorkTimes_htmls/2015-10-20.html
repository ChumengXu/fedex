Your Friday Briefing: European Union, South China Sea, Lamar Odom
By VICTORIA SHANNON
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Candidates release fund-raising figures.
Establishment-backed candidates in both the Republican and Democratic presidential contests are flagging in the money-raising race, filings show.
On the G.O.P. side, the retired neurosurgeon Ben Carson raised the most cash, $20 million, in the three months that ended Sept. 30, far ahead of the $11.4 million brought in by former Gov. Jeb Bush of Florida. Senator Ted Cruz of Texas raised $12 million, and Donald J. Trump raised $4 million.
Among Democrats, Hillary Rodham Clinton, at $30 million, barely outraised Senator Bernie Sanders of Vermont, with $26 million. (See the complete list.)
• E.U. action plan on refugees.
European Union leaders, ending their summit meeting in Brussels, agreed to support a plan that offers Turkey up to $3.4 billion, faster action on visa-free travel for its citizens and other sweeteners to gain Turkish cooperation on the migration crisis.
Jean-Claude Juncker, the president of the European Commission, said that the aim of the proposed pact was to keep more than two million Syrian refugees in Turkey and to prevent them from trying to reach northern or Western Europe.
Separately, Hungary was expected to decide today whether to close its border with Croatia to stem the flow of migrants.
• U.N. meets on Jerusalem violence.
The U.N. Security Council meets today over the recent violence between Israel and the Palestinians, diplomats said.
An estimated 30 Palestinians and seven Israelis have been killed during two weeks of bloodshed, the most serious unrest in years.
• Nevada shuts down fantasy sports sites.
Regulators in Nevada, the only U.S. state with legal sports betting, ordered fantasy sports websites to stop operating immediately there until they obtain state gambling licenses.
Federal and state investigators are looking into whether employees of the unregulated industry, armed with inside information, exploited paying customers.
• South Korea’s president at the White House.
Before meeting with President Obama at the White House today, President Park Geun-hye said she was willing to meet with Japan’s prime minister, Shinzo Abe, next month, in what would be their first bilateral summit meeting.
Washington has repeatedly urged its two key allies in Northeast Asia to mend relations, but the South Korean leader had been cool to that idea until now.
• South China Sea tensions.
China hosts two international security forums starting today as expectations grow that the U.S. Navy will directly challenge Beijing’s claims to islands in the South China Sea.
• Benghazi committee takes testimony.
Despite Capitol Hill’s weeklong recess, a top aide to Hillary Rodham Clinton will be questioned today by the House Select Committee on Benghazi, Republicans said.
Mrs. Clinton is to testify before the panel investigating the attacks in Libya in a public hearing on Thursday.
BUSINESS
• Oil and gas companies will propose a global pricing system for carbon today, while urging governments to use less coal and more natural gas.
The move comes before diplomats gather in Bonn, Germany, next week for the last formal session before global climate talks start in Paris in November.
• The Greek Parliament votes today on the new government’s first austerity bill, which contains major economic overhauls, including tax increases and stricter pension rules, as agreed under its new bailout program.
• A computer crash at a web-hosting company on Thursday that took down popular websites including Netflix and Expedia was an internal problem and not the result of an attack by hackers, the company said.
• General Electric reports its quarterly earnings today.
• Wall Street stock futures indicate a flat market today. European and Asian shares are mostly higher.
NOTEWORTHY
• It’s scary-movie time.
“Goosebumps,” a PG-rated adaptation of the R. L. Stine books with Jack Black, and “Crimson Peak” (rated R), a thriller directed by Guillermo del Toro of “Hellboy” fame, open today.
Also debuting is a different kind of thriller, the Cold War drama “Bridge of Spies,” directed by Steven Spielberg and starring Tom Hanks. It is “a consummate entertainment that sweeps you up with pure cinema,” our reviewer says.
Here are other movies opening today.
• Twelve days until the World Series.
The Kansas City Royals open the American League playoff series with the Toronto Blue Jays today and Saturday.
In pro football, six teams are undefeated heading into this weekend’s games.
• Action against hunger.
Secretary of State John Kerry helps commemorate World Food Day, a U.N. call for action against hunger, at the official celebration in Milan today.
The U.N.’s Food and Agriculture Organization was founded 70 years ago today.
• U.S. combat troop morale.
After months of testing mixed-gender combat units, the Marine Corps reported morale equal to that of all-male groups and higher than in noncombat integrated groups.
The study also found that sexual assault levels were no higher than in the Marines as a whole.
• Eight hundred dogs and a lot of tennis balls.
Flyball is a relay race invented in California in the 1970s for teams of dogs, both purebreds and mutts, and the premiere flyball competition starts today in Indianapolis.
At the end of a 102-foot lane, each of the team’s four dogs has to fetch a tennis ball and bring it back. Here’s a nine-minute documentary explaining the sport.
• Lamar Odom’s decline.
Friends and family say they watched the former N.B.A. basketball star’s struggles mostly from a distance. He remained in a coma today in a Las Vegas hospital.
BACK STORY
It was the largest assembly of black Americans in Washington since the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. spoke of his dream in 1963.
But whether there were actually a million people at the Million Man March on the Mall 20 years ago today is not clear.
The National Park Service stopped issuing crowd estimates after a dispute with organizers of the march over the turnout.
The park service said there were around 400,000 attendees, while other estimates put the number at about 800,000.
Either way, millions watched it on television, 1.7 million black men were said to have registered to vote and organizers called the event a success.
Louis Farrakhan, the provocative leader of the Nation of Islam who led the 1995 march, appealed then for the regeneration of the black family and the need for African-American male role models.
By contrast, a smaller anniversary march in Washington last weekend heard from a broader array of speakers as well as Mr. Farrakhan: relatives of victims of police violence, Christian clergy members, Muslim ministers, Native American activists and Latinos defending unauthorized immigrants.
Their message was that the struggle of one oppressed group is intrinsically linked to the struggles of others, and that they should unite.
