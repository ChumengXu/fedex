Your Thursday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Live from Des Moines.
The Republican presidential candidates, minus Donald J. Trump, face off tonight in Iowa, where Mr. Trump and Ted Cruz are in a furious fight to place first in Monday’s caucuses (9 p.m. Eastern on Fox News).
Mr. Trump’s feud with the network, an influential force in Republican politics, could be one of the most consequential fights in the campaign.
And the campaign of Ben Carson, once an Iowa front-runner, is in disarray.
On the Democratic side, Bernie Sanders is wrestling with whether to use a tougher line of ads.
• Oregon standoff appears near its end.
Ammon Bundy, the leader of an antigovernment group that is protesting federal land-management policies, asked the few remaining people occupying a wildlife refuge to “please stand down.”
The F.B.I. says three protesters turned themselves in late Wednesday night.
• On Capitol Hill.
Senators are expected to start voting today on amendments to a comprehensive energy bill that focuses on protecting and improving the power grid and updating the oil and gas infrastructure.
• Charity for veterans under scrutiny.
Current and former workers at the Wounded Warrior Project, the largest and fastest-growing veterans charity in the U.S., are questioning whether self-promotion is superseding the group’s mission.
About 40 percent of 2014 donations were used for overhead costs. “People could spend money on the most ridiculous thing, and no one batted an eye,” one former worker said.
• The migrant crisis.
The European Union authorities are raising pressure on Greece to increase its border-control efforts.
And Sweden’s government is planning to expel up to 80,000 asylum-seekers.
• Push for gay rights.
The Italian Senate will begin voting today on legislation to legalize same-sex civil unions. The outcome is uncertain, as lawmakers take on an issue that challenges traditional social mores and the politics of the Roman Catholic Church.
And two men in China are going to court to press for the right to marry. It’s the first time a Chinese court has agreed to hear a case on same-sex marriage.
• In memoriam.
Concepcion Picciotto kept a peace vigil encampment behind the White House for more than 30 years, considered the longest political protest in U.S. history. She died on Monday and was believed to be about 80.
BUSINESS
• Microsoft and Amazon investors will be watching to see if there was strong growth in the companies’ cloud services businesses when quarterly earnings are announced today.
• Facebook, which is building a network for virtual reality, reported another quarter of soaring revenue.
• An investigation by the New York attorney general portrays the ticketing industry as a “fixed game” that denies ordinary fans access to events.
• Here are snapshots of the U.S. and world markets.
NOTEWORTHY
• Fighting cancer.
President Obama will create a White House task force on cancer today, the first step in what Vice President Joseph R. Biden Jr. has called a “moonshot” to cure the disease, administration officials said.
• NASA’s Day of Remembrance.
Today is the 30th anniversary of the space shuttle Challenger breaking apart 73 seconds into launch, killing all seven astronauts on board, including Christa McAuliffe, a teacher who had been selected to participate.
• Nearing a tennis milestone.
Serena Williams, the defending Australian Open champion, dominated Agnieszka Radwanska, 6-0, 6-4, today and needs one more victory to capture her seventh title there and to tie Steffi Graf’s Open-era record of 22 Grand Slam singles titles.
Novak Djokovic beat Roger Federer, 6-1, 6-2, 3-6, 6-3, today and advances to the final.
And the leaders of the sport’s multiple governing bodies are planning new measures to fight match fixing.
• New nonfiction.
“The Last Thousand,” about a coed school in the slums of Afghanistan’s capital, and “The Lovers,” about a young Afghan couple willing to risk their lives to be together, are out this week.
(Full disclosure: Rod Nordland, our Kabul bureau chief, wrote “The Lovers.”)
• White actor to play Michael Jackson.
Joseph Fiennes has been cast to play the king of pop in a comedy for British television, an announcement that quickly drew a negative reaction.
• What’s new on TV.
Rob Lowe, Jenna Fischer and Megan Mullally star in “You, Me, and the Apocalypse,” a comedy that sets an eight-mile-wide comet on a path for Earth and tracks the movements of eight people who will all end up in a bunker (8 p.m. Eastern, NBC).
• Back to the future.
DeLorean, the company that manufactured the sports car featured in the “Back to the Future” trilogy, will produce 300 replicas of the vehicle.
They’re expected to go on sale in 2017, and will cost close to $100,000.
BACK STORY
It’s easy to create new words by adding the suffix “-gate” or “-iness” to it. (See Stephen Colbert’s truthiness.)
What Horace Walpole, a British member of Parliament, did in a letter written on this day in 1754 was a bit more creative.
He wrote to a friend of a word he conceived to describe what happened to three princes he read about in a fairy tale. The brothers were constantly discovering things by accident.
The fairy tale’s name? “The Three Princes of Serendip.” The word he came up with, of course, was serendipity.
“Three Princes” was a Persian tale and “Serendip” was an old name for Sri Lanka, the island nation off India’s coast that served as the royals’ home.
In Walpole’s letter, he described as an aha moment while browsing a book: “This discovery, indeed, is almost of that kind which I call Serendipity, a very expressive word.”
Slinkys and chocolate chip cookies — both of which we’ve written about in recent Back Stories — are just two inventions that were discovered serendipitously.
