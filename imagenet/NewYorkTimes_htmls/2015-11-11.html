Your Wednesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Republicans square off.
The party’s eight leading presidential candidates splintered over immigration in a fourth debate on Tuesday night, and Donald J. Trump defended his plan to build a wall along the Mexican border and to deport about 11 million people.
Highlights | Fact Check | Transcript | Critic’s Notebook
And Chris Christie used an appearance in the undercard debate to seize center stage and project himself as above intraparty conflict.
• Gaming the U.S. visa system.
A program meant to help American companies hire foreigners with special skills is increasingly dominated by outsourcing companies that move jobs offshore.
• Veterans Day.
The Obama administration today will call on Congress to pass measures to help military veterans gain easier access to health care, disability and educational benefits.
President Obama will also lay a wreath at Arlington National Cemetery.
• Migrant boat sinks.
Several children are among the 14 migrants who drowned today when their boat sank off the Turkish coast.
European and African leaders meet in Malta today to discuss the refugee crisis.
The U.S. and Russia are trying new approaches to Syria, which many of the migrants are fleeing.
• Myanmar’s next step.
The leader of the democracy movement, Daw Aung San Suu Kyi, asked today to talk with the commander of the country’s military-backed leadership for “national reconciliation,” after her party’s apparent landslide victory.
• Court watch.
An 8-year-old boy has been charged with murder in Birmingham, Ala. The police say that he attacked a toddler because she wouldn’t stop crying.
And a judge has sentenced to death a white supremacist for the fatal shootings of three people at a Jewish community center in Kansas in April 2014. None of the victims were Jewish.
• Stop taking bets here.
The fantasy sports operators Fan Duel and DraftKings have been ordered to shut down in New York. The attorney general says the games constitute illegal gambling and are subject to criminal penalties.
It’s the first time fantasy-sports operators have been formally accused of criminal activity.
BUSINESS
• Federal prosecutors in Manhattan have announced charges against three men — two Israeli citizens and an American citizen — in a scheme to steal information on 100 million customers of financial companies in the U.S.
It is one of the biggest cybercrimes in history, prosecutors say.
• Anheuser-Busch InBev, the world’s largest brewer, said today that it had finalized an agreement to acquire its closest rival, SABMiller, for about $105 billion.
• Snapchat has been discounted 25 percent by Fidelity, one of its earliest investors, in the latest sign of growing unease over the valuations of some technology start-ups.
• Japan’s first commercial jet in half a century made its maiden flight today. The country is hoping to revive its aerospace industry for the first time since World War II.
• Wall Street stock futures are trending positive. European markets are broadly higher, and Asian indexes finished mixed.
NOTEWORTHY
• Treat yourself.
Chinese shoppers are flocking online in record numbers today to take advantage of the discounts offered on Singles Day, the world’s largest Internet shopping event.
• Bedazzling.
“The Blue Moon,” a 12.03-carat blue diamond, may fetch up to $35 million at an auction today in Geneva. A pink diamond took in $28.55 million on Tuesday.
And the buyer of a Modigliani painting that sold for $170.4 million this week is a former taxi driver, Liu Yiqian, who plans to bring the work back to Shanghai, where he and his wife have two private galleries.
• Problems for the N.F.L.
The domestic violence case of the Dallas Cowboys’ Greg Hardy is shining a spotlight on a league still struggling to find a consistent way of dealing with such charges.
On the field, the Indianapolis Colts quarterback Andrew Luck is expected to miss two to six weeks because of a lacerated kidney and a partly torn abdominal muscle.
• In memoriam.
Allen Toussaint, the versatile producer, songwriter, pianist and singer who was a fixture of New Orleans R&B, died after appearing at a concert in Madrid on Monday night. He was 77.
• A final wish, granted.
A “Star Wars” fan’s last wish was to see an early version of the newest film, “Star Wars: The Force Awakens,” which opens next month.
Disney executives and the film’s director, J. J. Abrams, let the fan, Daniel Fleetwood, see the film last week. He died from cancer this week.
BACK STORY
The 1918 truce that halted fighting in World War I went into effect at the 11th hour of the 11th day of the 11th month. Today, that is commemorated in the U.S. as Veterans Day.
Which gives us the opportunity to share a trivia question from a reader: “Who was the highest-ranking U.S. military officer in history and what was his rank?”
There’s a simple answer — George Washington — with a complicated explanation.
Washington, a major general in the Revolutionary War, was given an honorary promotion to lieutenant general after his presidency, giving him three stars. All the four- and five-star generals who followed officially outranked him.
So as part of the nation’s bicentennial in 1976, President Gerald Ford signed a resolution posthumously promoting Washington to the highest rank ever: general of the armies.
That was also the title given to John Pershing following his service in World War I.
But the measure Ford signed also stated that “no officer of the United States Army should outrank Lieutenant General George Washington.”
So no matter how many stars other generals are awarded, Washington is understood to have one more. Right now, that puts him at six.
