In this political season's ludicrously crowded field of Republican presidential hopefuls, a couple of accomplishments distinguish the Wisconsin governor Scott Walker. As Robert Draper wrote earlier this week, Walker has won two elections and beaten a recall attempt in a state that President Obama carried twice. And as Dan Kaufman writes in this week's cover story, he has managed one of the great conservative policy successes of recent years, hamstringing unions with right-to-work laws in a state that the labor movement has considered home turf for a half century. Kaufman traveled to Wisconsin to meet the members of private-sector unions - ironworkers, heavy-machinery operators - who once supported Walker but now find their unions threatened by his ambitions.
Elsewhere in the issue, Giles Harvey profiles the English author Jenny Diski, who, afflicted with inoperable lung cancer, is writing her way unflinchingly through the last years of her life. Samantha M. Shapiro travels to Brazil to meet the child preachers who minister in the country's Pentacostal churches; in an accompanying video, the filmmaker Nadia Sussman brings us into one of their faith-healing ceremonies. The Drinks columnist Rosie Schaap experiments with the Korean spirit soju; Sam Sifton tries his hand at the Taiwanese staple three-cup chicken; and Troy Patterson considers eyeglasses, an accessory "more intimate than underpants."
