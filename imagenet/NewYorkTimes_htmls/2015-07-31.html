Your Friday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Palestinian toddler dies in attack.
A year-and-a-half-old child was killed in an overnight arson attack in the West Bank that was apparently carried out by Jewish settlers. The Israeli prime minister is calling it an act of terrorism.
Graffiti in Hebrew reading “revenge” was scrawled on the wall outside. About 500,000 Jews live in more than 100 settlements built in the occupied West Bank and East Jerusalem.
• Beijing gets the Games.
The Chinese capital beat out a long-shot bid from Almaty, Kazakhstan, to host the Winter Olympics in 2022. It will be the first city to stage both the Summer and Winter Games.
And a new report shows that the waters where Olympians will compete in swimming and boating events next summer in Rio de Janeiro are rife with human sewage.
• It’s only a clue.
The piece of airplane debris found on a French island in the Indian Ocean is being flown to southern France for analysis. A finding on whether it is from Malaysia Airlines Flight 370 may take another week.
Even if that’s confirmed, experts say it may not be much help in solving the mystery of where the rest of the plane’s wreckage is.
• Tracking campaign finances.
Many super PACs, the political groups than can raise and spend money in unlimited amounts, must file their first financial reports of the 2016 campaign cycle at the Federal Election Commission by midnight.
Less than nine percent of the money given to candidate-specific super PACs so far will benefit Democratic presidential candidates, The A.P. reports.
• Videos change views of the police.
Videos of confrontations involving white officers and black civilians have begun to alter public views of the police’s use of force and race relations, experts and police officials are saying.
On Thursday, Ray Tensing, who was fired from his job as a University of Cincinnati police officer after killing a black motorist, Samuel DuBose, was released from jail on bond. And in Texas, a state official called Trooper Brian Encina’s behavior the “catalyst” for Sandra Bland’s death in her jail cell on July 13.
• Student aid for prisoners.
Officials will announce today a pilot program that makes some federal and state prisoners eligible for student aid to take college courses.
The aid would come in the form of Pell grants, which are for low-income people and do not have to be repaid.
• Church shooting suspect in court.
Dylann Roof is due in court today in Columbia, S.C., on dozens of federal charges, including some that could carry the death penalty.
MARKETS
• Google says it won’t follow an order from France’s privacy regulator to scrub search results worldwide when users invoke their “right to be forgotten” online.
• About 650 officials from 12 Pacific Rim nations are hoping to wrap up negotiations on the Trans-Pacific Partnership today.
• Puerto Rico, the U.S. territory that’s teetering on the edge of insolvency, has two critical debt payments due on Saturday.
• The United Arab Emirates will be the first country in the Persian Gulf to remove fuel subsidies.
• Wall Street stock futures are trending negative. European markets are mixed, and Asian indexes inched higher, though China suffered its biggest monthly drop in six years.
NOTEWORTHY
• A fifth “Mission: Impossible.”
A 53-year-old Tom Cruise returns to his death-defying ways, without having lost a step, in “Mission: Impossible — Rogue Nation,” opening in theaters today.
And Jason Segel plays David Foster Wallace, the novelist who committed suicide in 2008, in “The End of the Tour,” which is based on a five-day Rolling Stone interview with Mr. Wallace during his 1996 book tour for “Infinite Jest.”
• Surpassing “Mockingbird.”
“Go Set a Watchman” debuts on our hardcover best-seller fiction list at No. 1. Its sequel of sorts, “To Kill a Mockingbird,” spent 98 weeks on the list in the 1960s, but never rose higher than No. 2.
Ta-Nehisi Coates’s “Between the World and Me,” an essay about being black in America, enters our hardcover nonfiction best-sellers list at No. 1.
• They haven’t aged.
An all-star cast from the 2001 comedy “Wet Hot American Summer” — Amy Poehler, Bradley Cooper, Elizabeth Banks, Paul Rudd and more — returns for a prequel series “Wet Hot American Summer: First Day of Camp” (Netflix).
• Happy birthday, Harry.
Although the age of the fictional wizard Harry Potter is unknown, it’s the 50th birthday of his creator, J. K. Rowling, who shares his birthday.
• Dear mom, don’t throw those out.
A pre-Civil War baseball card fetched $179,250 at an auction on Thursday.
BACK STORY
“You play the cornet, don’t you?”
With that invitation 100 years ago at Henry Ponce’s honky-tonk in New Orleans, Louis Armstrong got his first professional gig.
He was 14 and had just emerged from a home for juvenile delinquents, where he learned the cornet, the trumpet’s shorter cousin.
The horn he received there, his first ever, is part of an exhibit at the Old U.S. Mint in New Orleans from today through January 2017. The opening is timed to coincide with the start of the annual Louis Armstrong music festival.
Bookending that battered instrument in the exhibit is a shiny trumpet, the last he owned before his death in 1971.
In between, Armstrong became a musical legend.
“He could have gone the other way, if not for a teacher at the Colored Waifs’ Home who worked with him on trumpet,” the music journalist John Swenson told The Times-Picayune.
His early life in New Orleans was rough-and-tumble, and he refused to play there for 10 years because of its segregationist laws.
But as he said in 1966, “Every time I close my eyes blowing that trumpet of mine, I look right in the heart of good old New Orleans.”
