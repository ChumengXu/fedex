Your Tuesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Markets: Unsteady, but moving ahead.
After a three-day rout that erased nearly $3 trillion in value from stocks globally, the free fall continued only in China today — so far — with the Shanghai index losing another 8 percent.
European shares bolted higher today, and Wall Street stock futures point to a strong gain when trading opens, after a volatile Monday chopped almost 4 percent from the main U.S. indexes.
Investors are trying to separate fact from fear as they digest how China’s problems will affect them. The tumult, which has engulfed oil and other commodities, has many analysts grasping for explanations.
• Bush vs. Trump, G.O.P. vs. China.
Jeb Bush is punching back after a steady stream of taunts from Donald J. Trump.
Other Republican presidential candidates are taking aim at China amid the global stock jitters.
• Next up for the Iran deal.
The International Atomic Energy Agency’s board of governors meets in Vienna today to discuss how the group will monitor the nuclear accord between Iran and world powers.
Supporters of the deal in the U.S. Congress are closing in on the numbers they need to ensure it goes into force. In Europe, it is hardly stirring political passions.
• Erasing ancient history.
The destruction of antiquities by Islamic State militants — including, most recently, a fifth-century monastery and a first-century temple in Syria — has reached staggering levels, archaeological experts say.
A new report also says the extremists used poison gas in Syria.
• Amnesty in Ferguson, Mo.
The city is withdrawing nearly 10,000 arrest warrants for minor crimes like traffic violations, and it is taking steps to prevent the jailing of people who can’t pay fines and fees.
The moves are a response to criticisms of the judicial system after the killing of Michael Brown a year ago. Activists say the region’s court system still needs an overhaul.
• Racial gaps in U.S.
Black students are suspended or expelled at rates overwhelmingly higher than white children, according to a new report on 13 Southern states.
And hospice use has been growing fast, except among African-Americans, as more people choose to avoid futile, often painful medical treatments in favor of palliative care.
• Koreas find agreement.
South Korea agreed today to switch off its propaganda loudspeakers on the border, while North Korea expressed “regrets” about land mines that maimed two soldiers from the South and agreed to stand down from a high level of military alert.
MARKETS
• Is the value of your home appreciating, even as your retirement investments are shrinking?
We get a look at U.S. home prices today when two measures come out — the private Case-Shiller index and the government’s Federal Housing Finance Agency index.
• Chipotle plans to hire 4,000 people in the U.S. in a single day, Sept. 9, seeking to counter a tightening labor market for restaurant workers.
The hiring would expand Chipotle’s 59,000-member work force by nearly 7 percent.
• Netflix is adding films and TV series focused on teenagers, as part of efforts to cater to the postmillennial generation.
• A study out today raises concerns about the wave of insurance company mergers, citing a lack of competition in the private Medicare market.
NOTEWORTHY
• IndyCar driver dies after crash.
Justin Wilson, a British racecar driver, was struck on the head by debris from another car during a race at Pocono Raceway in Pennsylvania on Sunday. He was 37.
• New books.
The award-winning political cartoonist Ted Ralls tells the story of the former N.S.A. contractor Edward J. Snowden in “Snowden,” an illustrated biography released today.
And David Vine’s “Base Nation: How U.S. Military Bases Abroad Harm America and the World” traces how the U.S. came to have 174 military bases in Germany, 113 in Japan and 83 in South Korea today, and hundreds more across dozens of other countries (plus more than 170 golf courses).
• A star on all stages.
Misty Copeland, who in June became the first African-American woman to be named a principal dancer at the American Ballet Theater, joins the cast of the Broadway musical “On the Town” for 12 performances.
The story follows three American sailors who have 24 hours of shore leave in New York during World War II.
• The ’60s are back.
Edward Burns writes, directs, produces and stars in “Public Morals,” a cop-and-gangster tale set in early 1960s New York (10 p.m. Eastern, TNT).
• “We are not splitting up.”
The British boy band One Direction took to Twitter to assure fans that it was only planning to take a break next year.
• Time to head outdoors?
It’s the 99th anniversary of the founding of the National Park Service, and there are no entrance fees today. Events are being held at more than 400 locations across the U.S.
BACK STORY
The start of the N.F.L. season is two weeks away, but today is the unofficial kickoff as the video game Madden NFL 16 is released for PlayStation 4, PS3, Xbox One and Xbox 360.
Madden NFL has sold 100 million copies since it started, averaging six million to seven million a year.
The creator, EA Sports, gets the same game footage that all 32 N.F.L. teams receive weekly during the season.
The developers also use stunt players in a motion-capture studio to act out thousands of moves so that star players don’t risk injury.
That is, unless you’re the player featured on the cover of the game. Most ended up with poor seasons and untimely injuries after their cover shots.
This year’s cover star is Odell Beckham Jr. of the New York Giants, who made a spectacular one-handed touchdown catch last season.
The first player to be featured on the cover, Garrison Hearst of the San Francisco 49ers, led his team to the playoffs and then broke his fibula. He didn’t play for two seasons.
That, some people say, started the so-called Madden curse.
