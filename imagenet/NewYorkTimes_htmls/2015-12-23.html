Your Wednesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• The battle for Ramadi.
Iraqi forces are fighting Islamic State militants for control of Ramadi, the crucial western provincial capital. For the first time in months, Iraq’s military has reached the city’s center.
Regaining control of Ramadi would deliver the biggest loss for Islamic State militants since they began their expansion across Iraq last year.
• The migrant crisis.
Turkey is moving to shore up its borders at land and sea to slow the flow of refugees into Europe and foreign fighters into Syria.
President Obama is planning a U.N. summit meeting on the global migrant crisis. The number of migrants and refugees who entered Europe this year passed the one million mark this week.
• Mall of America protest.
Black Lives Matter campaigners plan to hold a demonstration against police brutality at an enormous shopping center in Bloomington, Minn., today, one of the biggest shopping days of the year.
A judge, however, barred three of the organizers from participating.
• “Super PACs” switch tactics.
The groups, created to support candidates by raising and spending unlimited amounts of money on TV, are overseeing jobs like data-collection programs, digital advertising, email lists and voter registration efforts.
That’s because TV ad rates are soaring in early-primary states.
• At the United Nations.
A session intended to draw attention to the grim effects of the war in Yemen is raising questions about possible crimes committed by the Saudi-led offensive, which the Pentagon actively supports.
And a scandal over the U.N.’s failure to protect child victims of sexual abuse by peacekeepers has led to changes in how such accusations will be handled.
• Defending their faith.
American Muslims are publicly confronting the uncomfortable questions that non-Muslims have about Islam and violence, and are providing answers with their own words and examples of how they live their lives.
For many Muslims, it is no longer enough to denounce terrorism.
• Real estate scion faces murder charge.
Robert A. Durst, an estranged member of a prominent New York real estate family, has agreed to be extradited to Los Angeles from Louisiana to face a murder charge.
Mr. Durst made a possible confession about the 2000 death of his friend Susan Berman this year at the end of the HBO documentary “The Jinx.”
• 1950s U.S. nuclear hit list.
The U.S. cited Moscow and East Berlin as potential targets in the event of a war with the Soviet Union, a newly released government document shows.
BUSINESS
• A showdown between two drug companies, one that gives away a treatment for a rare disease and another looking to profit from it, illustrates growing tensions over the rise in drug prices.
• Martin Shkreli and other executives charged with crimes are turning to social media to proclaim their innocence.
• The editor of The Las Vegas Review-Journal announced his resignation, less than two weeks after the billionaire casino magnate Sheldon Adelson bought the newspaper.
• The U.S. Census Bureau and the Department of Housing and Urban Development release today the data on new-home sales in November.
• Here are snapshots of the U.S. and world markets.
NOTEWORTHY
• At the movies.
“The Big Short,” opening across the U.S. today, stars Brad Pitt, Ryan Gosling, Steve Carell and Christian Bale and is about Wall Street shenanigans that led to economic collapse in 2008. It opened in limited release two weeks ago.
In Michael Moore’s documentary “Where to Invade Next,” opening in New York and Los Angeles today, the director travels to three continents to identify good ideas from foreign lands and bring them back to the U.S.
• Football roundup.
A federal appeals court ruling could help the Washington Redskins keep the registered trademarks to their name.
And researchers were awarded about $16 million to find a way to diagnose, while victims are alive, chronic traumatic encephalopathy, a degenerative brain disease linked to repeated hits to the head in contact sports.
• Two-year flight delay.
NASA’s next Mars mission won’t take off until 2018, after tiny leaks were found in a vacuum sphere housing its seismic instrument.
• Madam Claude dies.
The brothel keeper in Paris had one of the most exclusive lists of clients. She was 92.
• Happy Festivus.
Festivus, a fictional holiday invented by George Costanza’s father, Frank, on the sitcom “Seinfeld” is celebrated today. Frank created it to protest the commercialism of Christmas.
There is a “Festivus Pole” on display today at the Oklahoma State Capitol. Seriously.
BACK STORY
Quick — what do the U.S., Liberia and Myanmar have in common?
They are the only nations that do not mandate the metric system as their official measurement standard.
It’s not for lack of trying, at least in the U.S. Forty years ago today, the Metric Conversion Act required the federal government to endorse, if not adopt, the system.
That law followed at least five other legal efforts to go metric since 1866, as well as numerous informal attempts since the time of Thomas Jefferson.
Officially, not much has happened since then. Many federal agencies now use the metric system exclusively, but many others — particularly those involving transportation and construction — do not.
Most recently, Lincoln Chafee of Rhode Island made converting to metric a centerpiece of his bid for the Democratic presidential nomination.
Apathy is most often cited as the cause of Americans’ attachment to inches and pounds.
Yet even without legal mandates, the metric system surrounds us.
Medicines are measured in milligrams, soda bottles in liters and dental floss in meters. Even marijuana and cocaine come by the gram.
Some experts estimate half of U.S. business is conducted in metric, leading them to predict its eventual adoption, by law or not.
