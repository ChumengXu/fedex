Your Monday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• High theater at the U.N.
World leaders gather today in New York for the 70th session of the United Nations General Assembly, where Syria, the refugee crisis, climate change and the Iran nuclear accord will be the top subjects.
President Obama; the Iranian leader, Hassan Rouhani; and President Vladimir V. Putin of Russia are among those speaking today. Mr. Obama and Mr. Putin will speak privately in a meeting that could have a wide-ranging impact.
• Russia’s leader on the world stage.
In his first address at the U.N. in 10 years, Mr. Putin will focus on Syria. Russia’s military buildup there and its agreement to share intelligence about ISIS show that Mr. Putin wants to be a leading player in solving the crisis.
What he might not mention: Ukraine, where nearly 8,000 people have been killed in the last 17 months, the U.N. says, in a conflict with Russian-backed rebels who control the eastern part of the country.
• The pope’s grand farewell.
Pope Francis is back at the Vatican today after a 10-day visit to Cuba and the U.S. and a Mass in Philadelphia on Sunday attended by one million people. He also met with victims of sexual abuse and vowed that those responsible would be held accountable.
See highlights of his speeches, top moments and the visit in pictures.
His next gathering — with 250 bishops from around the world at the Vatican next week — might not be as unifying. It’s the second part of the synod (pronounced SIN-id) on how the church should approach issues including divorce, homosexuality and contraception.
• Capitol Hill anxiety.
The new fiscal year starts Thursday, and Congress has not yet passed any of the spending bills needed to fund the government after Wednesday.
The hang-up, over funding for Planned Parenthood, contributed to Speaker John A. Boehner’s decision to resign and may embolden conservative hard-liners to further defy Democrats and Mr. Obama.
• On the campaign trail.
The leading Republican candidate for president, Donald J. Trump, will unveil what he calls a major tax reduction for almost all citizens and corporations.
A new poll shows gains for the non-establishment candidates: Ben Carson and Carly Fiorina, both Republicans; and Senator Bernie Sanders, who is seeking the Democratic nomination.
MARKETS
• Wall Street stock futures point to a slightly lower opening. European markets are lower, and Asian indexes finished mixed.
• More high-level firings could be announced at Volkswagen this week after the automaker’s emissions scandal.
And the E.P.A. announced that diesel-engine cars would be tested more rigorously in light of the deception, fanned by the company’s ambition to be No. 1.
• India is replacing China as the next big tech frontier for U.S. companies, as China’s president stays firm on tough Internet policies.
• Royal Dutch Shell said today that it would stop exploration off the coast of Alaska “for the foreseeable future.”
OVER THE WEEKEND
• A total lunar eclipse and a supermoon converged overnight for the first time in 33 years. The next one will be in 18 years.
• A new partnership between the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, the World Bank and the World Health Organization will work to improve health care in poor and middle-income countries.
• Nigerians celebrated the announcement that polio was no longer endemic in the country. Only Pakistan and Afghanistan have yet to wipe it out.
• Mark Zuckerberg announced that Facebook would help the U.N. bring Internet connections to refugee camps.
• At least 50,000 people marched through Mexico City to observe the one-year anniversary of the disappearance of 43 college students in southern Mexico.
• Former President Bill Clinton blamed Republicans for the furor surrounding Hillary Rodham Clinton’s use of a private email account and server while she was secretary of state.
• Separatists in Catalonia, Spain’s most prosperous region, won a majority of seats in local elections and vowed to achieve independence within 18 months.
• “Hotel Transylvania 2,” with a Dracula voiced by Adam Sandler, became the top September opener at the domestic box office.
• Catching up on TV: Episode recaps for “Fear the Walking Dead” and “Masters of Sex.”
NOTEWORTHY
• For laughs.
Trevor Noah takes the seat that Jon Stewart had for 16 years on “The Daily Show.” The comedian Kevin Hart is his first guest (11 p.m. Eastern, Comedy Central).
Michelle Obama will be on “The Late Show” (11:35 p.m., CBS).
• A museum like no other.
Exhibits at the American Museum of Tort Law, which opened Sunday in Connecticut, describe cases like a 1992 lawsuit against McDonald’s over hot coffee.
• Scoreboard.
The Pittsburgh Steelers won their game on Sunday against the Rams, but they lost their two-time Super Bowl-winning quarterback Ben Roethlisberger to an injury. All the scores and highlights are here.
Tonight’s game: Kansas City at Green Bay (8:30 p.m. Eastern, ESPN).
In baseball, the Chicago Cubs, New York Mets and Toronto Blue Jays clinched playoff berths.
• Funny ladies.
The Thurber Prize for American Humor will be awarded tonight for the first time to a woman.
The finalists are The New Yorker cartoonist Roz Chast, the actress and writer Annabelle Gurwitch and the novelist Julie Schumacher.
BACK STORY
One paradox of banning a book is that doing so often just draws more attention to it.
Banned Books Week drives home the point. The annual celebration of First Amendment rights led by the American Library Association began Sunday.
The first book banned and burned in the New World was published in London in 1650: William Pynchon’s “The Meritorious Price of Our Redemption,” a critique of Puritanism.
Mr. Pynchon, a Puritan living in Massachusetts Bay Colony, found little welcome for his views and was eventually forced to return to England.
Fast forward three centuries, and there were about 300 cases of attempted censorship reported in 1980, the library group says.
Coinciding with the election of Ronald Reagan and the rise of a conservative group called Moral Majority, that number tripled in 1981. (It has fallen to about 300 today.)
Banned Books Week began in 1982 to confront the rise in censorship challenges.
The list of modern and ancient classics that have been condemned is an illustrious one.
One of them, in fact, is about a world where books are illegal: Ray Bradbury’s “Fahrenheit 451” was once pulled from the reading list of a Mississippi high school, among others.
