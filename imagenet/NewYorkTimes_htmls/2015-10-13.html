Your Tuesday Briefing
By VICTORIA SHANNON
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• A debate of subtle divisions.
The four candidates for the Democratic nomination trailing Hillary Rodham Clinton in the polls — Bernie Sanders, Martin O’Malley, Jim Webb and Lincoln Chafee — have an opening to broaden their appeal in the party’s first debate tonight (8:30 p.m. Eastern, CNN).
Mrs. Clinton and the others will most likely present a calmer version of the scrappy G.O.P. debates, revealing how much they agree on the issues.
Instead, any tensions or potential attacks may involve how fervently and sincerely the candidates embrace the progressive cause.
Our live blog follows developments through the day.
• Meanwhile, on the Biden watch...
CNN has set aside an extra lectern for the debate in case the vice president throws his hat in the ring today.
But Joseph R. Biden Jr.’s public schedule shows him in Washington today, not in Las Vegas. Aides are researching filing deadlines to see if he can keep his options open into November.
• Juvenile sentences at issue.
The U.S. Supreme Court hears arguments today on whether a 2012 ruling banning life-without-parole sentences for juveniles offenders must be applied retroactively.
Louisiana and five other states — Alabama, Colorado, Michigan, Minnesota and Pennsylvania — have said no.
• A left-leaning Paul Ryan?
The far right of the Republican Party is now asking whether Representative Paul D. Ryan of Wisconsin is too far left to be their choice for speaker of the House.
In any event, he has not budged from saying he is not a candidate.
• The Afghan war.
Despite Taliban victories, the Islamic State has made major inroads in turf battles against Taliban commanders in Afghanistan.
The result, rather than weakening the overall insurgency, has mostly been more chaos and misery for Afghan civilians.
• Violence in Israel.
Palestinian groups called for a “Day of Rage” across the West Bank, Gaza and East Jerusalem today as stabbing attacks in Israel continue to spread.
• Malaysian crash report.
The Dutch Safety Board publishes its final report today on why Malaysia Airlines Flight 17 broke up over Ukraine in 2014, killing all 298 on board.
The West and Ukraine say that Russian-backed rebels brought down the Boeing 777 with a missile, while Russia blames Ukraine. In advance of the report, a Russian manufacturer of missiles sought to debunk it.
• Nuclear agreement passes a step.
Iran’s Parliament voted today to support the nuclear deal it struck with world powers, sending the measure to a council of senior clerics who will review the accord before its final approval.
BUSINESS
• The beer business is likely to have a huge industry leader after SABMiller finally agreed to a takeover offer today from Anheuser-Busch InBev.
If it goes through, the $104 billion merger would create a behemoth with annual revenue of $64 billion that commands 30 percent of global beer sales.
• Peter D. Barbey, the new owner of The Village Voice, the storied alternative weekly newspaper created 60 years ago, says he plans to invest in the Voice and make it relevant again in New York’s cultural life.
• Wall Street stock futures are falling this morning, after a retreat on global stock markets.
NOTEWORTHY
• A PG-rated Playboy.
Playboy’s print magazine will stop publishing photos of nude women in a redesign next year, capitulating to the presence of plenty of free pornography on the web.
There will still be a Playmate of the Month, but the pictures will be “PG-13.” Last year, the magazine’s website dispensed with nudity. Playboy’s circulation has dropped to about 800,000 from 5.6 million in 1975.
• On the diamond.
The Mets cruised to a 13-7 win over the Los Angeles Dodgers to take a two-games-to-one lead in the best-of-five series, while the Chicago Cubs took the lead in their series.
The Kansas City Royals and the Toronto Blue Jays each won to send their separate series into a deciding Game 5.
• Literary plaudits.
The Man Booker Prize, a prestigious annual honor for books written in English, is awarded today.
This year’s shortlist features two authors from Britain, two Americans and one each from Jamaica and Nigeria
• Compulsive texting.
A study of more than 400 eighth and 11th graders published this month has found that many teenage texters had a lot in common with compulsive gamblers, including losing sleep because of texting, problems cutting back on texting and lying to cover up the amount of time they spent sending messages.
• A sweet spot.
Preliminary data in Mexico finds that a tax on soda resulted in a drop in sales of drinks sweetened with sugar, particularly among the lowest-income consumers.
The long-term effects of the policy remain uncertain, but advocates say it could translate to the United States.

BACK STORY
Augusta Ada King, countess of Lovelace, managed to squeeze all the elements of a graphic novel into her 36 years two centuries ago: fame and fortune, beauty and brains, love and loss (also gambling, drugs, alcohol and adultery).
But it is as a technology visionary and possibly the world’s first computer programmer that she is most remembered today, Ada Lovelace Day.
She was the daughter of the enigmatic poet Lord Byron, but he left his wife a month after Ada was born. Her mother, desperate to isolate her from the madness of poetry, steered her into an education in numbers and science.
Through a tutor, she met Charles Babbage when she was a teenager, just as he was developing what became the progenitors of today’s computers.
He became her mentor, and she ended up translating a lecture on his Analytical Engine.
Her accompanying notes, which turned out to be longer than the lecture, included what some say is the first computer program.
But while Babbage thought he was designing a calculator, Lovelace also wrote of her vision for the machine to handle endless formulations, perhaps even composing “music of any degree of complexity or extent.”
An exhibition about her life opens today at the Science Museum in London. Oh, and a graphic novel inspired by her was published this year: “The Thrilling Adventures of Lovelace and Babbage” by Sydney Padua.
