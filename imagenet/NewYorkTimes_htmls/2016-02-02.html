Your Tuesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Cruz wins; virtual tie for Democrats.
Ted Cruz eclipsed Donald J. Trump in the Iowa caucuses, but Marco Rubio’s strong third-place showing bolsters his case to Republican donors uneasy about Mr. Cruz and Mr. Trump. Voters’ fury helped determine the results.
The close vote between Hillary Clinton and Bernie Sanders means that they are likely to split Iowa’s share of delegates to the national convention. The tie is an ominous result for Mr. Sanders, our writer says.
Here are the full results, and videos of the candidates’ postvoting remarks. The caravan of candidates and news media are in New Hampshire today.
• Republican leaders at the White House.
Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell and House Speaker Paul Ryan are discussing legislative priorities with President Obama today.
• In search of a settlement.
Secretary of State John Kerry attends a meeting today in Rome of the anti-Islamic State coalition.
Mr. Kerry is urging the rival factions in Syria not to squander the ongoing diplomatic talks in Geneva. The five-year civil war has killed more than 250,000 and driven over 10 million from their homes.
• Massacre in Nigeria.
Government officials have raised the death toll of the weekend rampage by Boko Haram militants to 65 people, though villagers who were attacked say up to 100 died.
The attack adds to the 2.5 million people who have been displaced from their homes across four countries because of violence by the group.
• Fortifying Europe’s defense.
The U.S. plans to send more heavy weapons to NATO countries in Central and Eastern Europe in an effort to deter Russia from further aggression in the region.
• Storm alert.
Heavy snow is predicted today from northern New Mexico to Michigan’s Upper Peninsula, the National Weather Service says.
The same storm is bringing severe thunderstorms, capable of damaging winds and a few tornadoes, to Mississippi and Alabama.
Travelers should check their flight status, and here’s what to do if your flight is canceled.
• Bill Cosby on trial.
An ex-prosecutor is expected to testify today in Philadelphia that he promised Bill Cosby would never be charged over a 2005 sex-assault complaint.
Mr. Cosby’s lawyers argue that prosecutors who charged Mr. Cosby last month unfairly used his deposition testimony from that civil lawsuit against him. A judge will decide whether the promise amounts to an immunity deal.
BUSINESS
• Alphabet, Google’s new parent company, could surpass Apple as the world’s richest company when trading begins today.
Here are snapshots of the U.S. and world markets.
• Yahoo is scheduled to report earnings today. More job cuts may be on the way, while a lawsuit filed by a fired manager takes a peek inside Yahoo’s quarterly performance review system.
• BP reported a sharp fourth-quarter loss today. Earnings announcements from Exxon Mobil and Royal Dutch Shell are up next, and they could be equally ugly. Oil prices dropped nearly 20 percent in the fourth quarter.
• Iraq faces crippling budget cuts because it depends on oil for 90 percent of its revenue.
NOTEWORTHY
• New nonfiction.
Among the releases today: “The Black Calhouns,” a chronicle of one black family from the Civil War to civil rights; and “Sing to Me,” L.A. Reid’s memoir of making music.
Friendships form the basis of “The Firebrand and the First Lady” — between Pauli Murray, a young black woman, and Eleanor Roosevelt; as well as “Blood Brothers” — between Muhammad Ali and Malcolm X.
• All quiet at the Supreme Court.
The justices return to hear arguments on Feb. 22. And all of them are likely to have something to say, except for Clarence Thomas, who hasn’t asked a question from the bench in 10 years.
• What’s to watch.
A 10-hour mini-series on O.J. Simpson, with Cuba Gooding Jr. in the lead role, begins (10 p.m. Eastern, FX). The cast includes John Travolta, Courtney B. Vance, Sarah Paulson, Nathan Lane and David Schwimmer.
And you may find some insights for the 2016 election “American Experience: Murder of a President.” It’s about James A. Garfield, who became the surprise nominee at the 1880 Republican convention (9 p.m. Eastern, PBS, but check local listings).

Rihanna’s new album “Anti” entered the Billboard chart at No. 27, her lowest debut position. One streaming service — in which she is a partner — is still calling it a hit.
Adele’s “25” remains a smash; it’s No. 1 for an eighth week.

BACK STORY
We’re on to New Hampshire, home of the nation’s first presidential primary, one week from today.
New Hampshire’s first primary dates back 100 years, to 1916. To save money, it was held on the same day as the traditional town meeting day — the second Tuesday of March — when local legislative issues were hammered out.
Up until 1948, voters picked only delegates to the national conventions. To increase voter interest, presidential candidates were added to the 1952 ballots.
That was enough incentive for two senators and White House hopefuls — Robert Taft, Republican of Ohio, and Estes Kefauver, Democrat of Tennessee — to travel across the state canvassing for votes.
Taft lost to Gen. Dwight D. Eisenhower, but Kefauver scored a double-digit win over President Harry Truman, who later dropped out of the race. The trek across the state became a quadrennial winter ritual.
In 1975, the nominating contest was moved earlier after neighboring states tried to hold a regionwide primary a week earlier than New Hampshire.
Today, state law ensures the primary will be held “on the Tuesday at least seven days immediately preceding the date on which any other state shall hold a similar election.”
