Your Wednesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Fatal campus attack in Pakistan.
A group of militants stormed Bacha Khan University in northwestern Pakistan today, leaving at least 19 dead and several wounded.
It’s the latest civilian target chosen by extremists determined to spread terror around the world. We have live updates.
• Trump and Sanders get lifts.
Sarah Palin, the 2008 vice-presidential nominee, has endorsed Donald J. Trump in the Republican primary, providing him with a potential boost among Tea Party loyalists two weeks before the Iowa caucuses.
Scandals from the ’90s are threatening to erode Hillary Clinton’s support among women as she trails Bernie Sanders by double digits in New Hampshire.
Latinos are becoming more distant from the U.S. political process, according to a new report. And Chris Christie opens up about marital troubles in a new book.
• Combating “dark money.”
President Obama is seriously considering an executive order that would require companies doing business with the federal government to disclose their political contributions.
It’s an attempt to stem the tide of corporate donations that began with the Supreme Court’s Citizens United ruling in 2010, which allowed companies and labor unions to give unlimited sums.
• More than “top secret.”
Intelligence officials say they have found emails on former Secretary of State Hillary Clinton’s private computer server that they consider to be of a higher level of classification than “top secret.”
It’s not clear whether she sent or received the dozens of emails.
• President Obama visits Detroit.
Mr. Obama will be at the at the North American International Auto Show, showcasing the recovery of the auto industry since his bailout of General Motors and Chrysler during his first year in office.
On Tuesday, he met the mayor of Flint, Mich., to discuss the crisis that has erupted over the city’s contaminated drinking water.
• Star-powered gathering.
The annual World Economic Forum, attended by thousands of political and financial luminaries, begins today in Davos, Switzerland.
At least one person — North Korea’s foreign minister — accepted an invitation that was later revoked.
• A Navy SEAL’s suicide.
A Times special report details how a final deployment to Afghanistan led to the suicide of a high-achieving SEAL officer in 2012.
It’s a cautionary tale of how men on the team were ground down by years of fighting and of losing comrades.
• U.S. storm tracking.
The National Weather Service is monitoring a potential weekend storm that could bring as much as one to two feet of snow from Washington to Boston.
BUSINESS
• Treasury Secretary Jacob J. Lew is in Puerto Rico today to discuss the Obama administration’s proposal for addressing the island’s huge debt.
• Netflix’s fourth-quarter results show a company achieving record global growth, but one that also faces a slowdown in the U.S.
• Stocks in Japan closed in bear-market territory. Here are snapshots of the U.S. and world markets.
• Falling oil prices are posing one of the first big tests to the U.S. banking system since the financial crisis.
NOTEWORTHY
• Planetary parade.
Five planets — Mercury, Venus, Mars, Saturn and Jupiter — will arrange on an arc across the sky early today, in a rare spectacle that will repeat every morning until late next month.
It’s the first time in more than a decade that the five will be simultaneously visible to the naked eye, and stargazers in the Northern Hemisphere should plan to get up about 45 minutes before sunrise to catch the show.
• Health warning.
Pregnant women who feel sick and have visited countries in Latin America in which the Zika virus is spreading should quickly see a doctor, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention says.
• New guidelines on medical records.
The Obama administration says doctors and hospitals cannot require patients to state a reason for requesting their records, and cannot deny access to those records merely because they are concerned patients might be upset by the information.
• In case you missed it …
Our most popular articles included a look at Canada’s sudden hipness; a dissection of Mr. Trump’s 1988 purchase of the Plaza Hotel; how biologists are playing matchmaker to save salmon; and our restaurant critic’s downgrading of Per Se.
• At the Australian Open.
The No. 1 seed Serena Williams and the No. 5 seed Maria Sharapova were among those who reached the third round.
• A $1 billion donation.
Microsoft hopes to give away its cloud services to 70,000 nonprofits.
BACK STORY
No one invented the scream, but one American received a patent on this day in 1885 for his creation of the ultimate scream machine, the roller coaster.
LaMarcus Thompson’s Switchback Gravity Pleasure Railway at Coney Island wasn’t exactly the hair-raiser we’re familiar with today.
For a nickel, riders reached a top speed of six miles an hour as they moved down a hill. The ride was 600 feet long.
Thompson’s idea was based on a converted coal train in Pennsylvania that carried people down a hill as a tourist attraction.
But many historians believe the origin of the roller coaster dates further back. In the 1600s, Russian sled riders hurtled down artificial mountains of ice and snow that were supported by wood frames. Catherine the Great was said to enjoy the rides.
The French may have adapted the idea for a warmer climate by adding wheels to the sled-like car and locking it into a track. In the 1800s, France had a couple of roller coasters, including the first with a loop.
Today, ride designers use high-tech parts that create a much more terrifying rush of twists and turns. The fastest coaster goes 150 m.p.h.
