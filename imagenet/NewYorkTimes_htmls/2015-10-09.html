Your Friday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Tunisians win Nobel Peace Prize.
The National Dialogue Quartet in Tunisia won the award today “for its decisive contribution to the building of a pluralistic democracy” in Tunisia, birthplace of the Arab Spring of 2011.
Tunisia’s transition to democracy has been a glimmer of hope in the Middle East and North Africa, and the group of four organizations helped pave the way for a peaceful political dialogue, the Nobel Committee said.
Did you know there’s a legal battle over the Rev. Martin Luther King Jr.’s Nobel Peace Prize? Test your knowledge of the prize with our quiz.
• Republicans regroup.
House Republicans meet this morning at the Capitol to try to move forward after Kevin McCarthy’s withdrawal from the race for speaker ended expectations of a tidy succession and sank Congress deeper into turmoil.
He was undone by the same hard-line conservative faction that pushed John A. Boehner out. There are at least seven possible candidates for speaker.
• Consoler in chief.
President Obama will be in Roseburg, Ore., today to meet privately with the families of the victims of the mass shooting last week.
But such rampage killings are not the typical face of gun violence in the U.S. Each day, about 30 people are victims of gun homicides, and another 60 people a day kill themselves with guns.
• U.S. waits for Russia.
Russian intervention in Syria is closing off Western options that might have threatened Syria’s government, and could eventually force the West into a choice between President Bashar al-Assad or the Islamic State militant group.
President Obama fears risking the escalation of the conflict and does not have a new strategy to resolve it or defeat ISIS.
• Holy site of contention.
Israel is barring its ministers and Parliament members as well as Muslim men under the age of 50 from the Jerusalem site revered by Jews as the Temple Mount and Muslims as the Noble Sanctuary, in an attempt to calm several days of violence in the Old City.
The competing claims to what may be the world’s most contested piece of real estate is a perennial focal point for flare-ups in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.
• Police shooting case is settled.
The family of Walter L. Scott, the black man who was fatally shot by a white police officer in North Charleston, S.C., reached a $6.5 million settlement with the city.
MARKETS
• Federal and California regulators are investigating a second computer program in Volkswagen’s diesel cars that affects the operation of the cars’ emission controls.
With VW cars under recall, diesel fans have few comparable options among other carmakers.
• Federal Reserve members held off on an interest-rate increase last month over global inflation worries, minutes of the meeting show.
The policy-making committee meets again Oct. 27-28 and then Dec. 15-16.
• Some Starbucks stores will accept Apple Pay, a mobile payments system, this year as part of a pilot program. KFC and Chili’s also signed up.
• California today joins at least six other states in banning plastic microbeads, tiny abrasives used in exfoliators and other products.
• Wall Street stock futures trading hints at a slightly lower opening this morning. European and Asian shares are broadly higher.
NOTEWORTHY
• A portrait of Apple’s co-founder.
Michael Fassbender has the title role in “Steve Jobs,” a biographical drama directed by Danny Boyle (“Slumdog Millionaire”) and written by Aaron Sorkin (“The Social Network”).
Here’s what else is coming to theaters today.
• Book best-sellers.
Jenny Lawson writes about mental illness in her memoir “Furiously Happy,” new at No. 3 on our weekly list of best-selling hardcover nonfiction.
Get an early look at all our best-seller lists from the Sunday Book Review.
• Lone Star sweep.
The Texas Rangers defeated the Toronto Blue Jays, 5-3, and the Houston Astros beat the Kansas City Royals, 5-2, in their division series openers.
Today’s schedule: Rangers at Blue Jays (12:30 p.m. Eastern, MLBN); Astros at Royals (3:30 p.m., FS1); Cubs at Cardinals (6:30 p.m., TBS); and Mets at Dodgers (9:30 p.m., TBS).
• Even more zombies.
AMC’s post-apocalyptic zombie fantasy, “The Walking Dead,” has its 90-minute sixth-season premiere on Sunday. Our critic says it “reaches for a new level of cinematic wallop.”
And a group interview with 27 of Bill Cosby’s accusers airs tonight on “Dateline” (9 p.m. Eastern, NBC).
• New sounds.
The retired Canadian astronaut Chris Hadfield releases today “Space Sessions: Songs From a Tin Can.” It was recorded at the International Space Station in 2013, the same year his cover of David Bowie’s “Space Oddity” went viral.
Other new releases: Selena Gomez’s “Revival”; Toby Keith’s “35 mph Town”; Jana Kramer’s “thirty one”; and Duncan Sheik’s “Legerdemain.”
• An “unconventional convention.”
Politicon, which opens today in Los Angeles, is for fans of both politics and entertainment. Trevor Noah of “The Daily Show,” James Carville, Ann Coulter, Newt Gingrich and David Axelrod are among the speakers.
• “Star Wars” composer scores.
For the first time in its history, the American Film Institute will give its highest honor to a composer: John Williams, responsible for the music in more than 150 movies and television shows over seven decades.
• In memoriam.
Paul Prudhomme, the chef who put the Cajun cooking of Louisiana on the American culinary map, died on Thursday in New Orleans. He was 75.

BACK STORY
Thirty-three men had been trapped half a mile beneath the earth for more than two months, the longest known time that miners have survived a collapse, when a powerful drill finally broke through the hardest rock.
That critical breakthrough, which happened five years ago today in Chile, raised hopes that there might be a happy ending to the cave-in, after all.
A few days later, the first miners ascended to the surface, and all were out 69 days after they arrived for work, redefining what a bad day truly is.
The rescue drill not only carved their exit, but its sound also helped give them the spirit to carry on. “The silence just destroyed us,” one miner told the journalist Héctor Tobar. “Without a positive sign, your faith collapses. Because faith isn’t totally blind.”
Mr. Tobar’s book about the rescue, “Deep Down Dark,” is released as a film, “The 33,” on Nov. 13.
The miners’ primary nourishment for the first 17 days came from cookies, tins of tuna and dirty water that was for cooling off the drill machines. Once, they had a slice of peach — split 33 ways — for dessert. Each share was about the size of a fingernail.
For the last 52 days, rescuers sent food and hydration gels down a narrow borehole carved by the first, much smaller, rescue drill. Ventilation shafts and daily prayer sessions kept them alive.
It’s the universal story of human struggle — of the ordinary becoming extraordinary.
