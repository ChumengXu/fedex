Your Friday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• The migrants’ plight.
Scores of African migrants trying to reach Europe are feared dead today after two boats capsized off the coast of Libya. Separately, more than 70 corpses, assumed to be those of migrants, were found in a truck outside Vienna on Thursday.
European leaders have not been able to find a consensus on how to deal with the biggest wave of migrants in the region since World War II. A team of our reporters documented some of their journeys.
• Renewed push on gun laws.
The killing on Wednesday of two journalists in Virginia gives new intensity to the campaign for gun control. Andy Parker, the father of Alison Parker, one of the victims, vowed to join the fight.
The shootings also point to how difficult it can be for employers to deal with a volatile, aggrieved worker who has committed no crime. The gunman is someone who felt demeaned as a black man, a gay man and a son of a successful father.
• Studying the studies.
A long effort to reproduce 100 studies published in three leading psychology journals found that more than half of the findings did not hold up when retested.
The findings come as the number of retractions of published papers is rising sharply.
• Selling the nuclear deal.
President Obama will participate in a live webcast at 2:10 p.m. Eastern with the Jewish Federation of North America. The recent nuclear agreement with Iran will be among the topics discussed.
And Mr. Obama’s sanctions chief arrives in Israel today to defend the nuclear containment deal.
• On the campaign trail.
Donald J. Trump is trying to decide whether to sign a pledge to support whoever is selected as the Republican presidential nominee, and Carly Fiorina is gaining in the polls.
And Democratic leaders are growing concerned that Hillary Rodham Clinton is failing to seriously address questions about her email practices while secretary of state.
• Court watch.
A jury in New Hampshire is deliberating the fate of Owen Labrie, the 19-year-old who is accused of raping a girl at St. Paul’s School in Concord.
And lawyers for Robin Williams’s wife and children are due back in court today in the continuing fight over the actor’s estate. Mr. Williams died last August.
MARKETS
• Wall Street stock futures are trending slightly lower. European markets are down, and Asian markets rallied.
• A National Labor Relations Board ruling makes it substantially easier for American unions to bargain for higher wages and benefits.
• The Food and Drug Administration has approved the second drug in a new class of powerful cholesterol-lowering medications. It also warned tobacco makers against describing their products as “additive-free” or “natural.”
• Consumer Reports has given the new Tesla its highest-ever score for a car.
NOTEWORTHY
• Emmett Till’s legacy.
Sixty years ago today, a black 14-year-old named Emmett Till was beaten and murdered by two white men in Money, Miss., who accused him of whistling at a white woman.
The killers’ acquittal helped accelerate the civil rights movement. Relatives and activists are holding church services and film screenings to honor Till today.
• Popular reads.
Alice Hoffman’s “The Marriage of Opposites,” the fictionalized life of Rachel Pomié, a 19th-century Jewish woman on the island of St. Thomas whose son Camille Pissaro became a leading Impressionist painter, is in its second week on our hardcover best-seller list.
In nonfiction, Felicia Day’s “You’re Never Weird on the Internet,” a memoir about rising to stardom in the web video world, debuts at No. 3.
• For laughs.
The Oddball Comedy and Curiosity Festival tour returns for a third year, headlined by Aziz Ansari and Amy Schumer.
It kicks off tonight in Kansas City, Mo., and travels to 18 cities, ending on Oct. 18 in Dallas.
• What’s on this weekend.
Friday: The new series “Narcos” tells the story of the ruthless Medellín cartel in Colombia, which ruled the international cocaine trade in the 1980s (streaming, Netflix).
Saturday: The series finale of “Hannibal” (10 p.m. Eastern, NBC).
Sunday: “Warlords of Ivory” is a disturbing look at the extreme nature of elephant hunting in Africa (National Geographic, 8 p.m. Eastern), and “Elephant Queen” tracks a herd as it tries to survive dry season (9 p.m. Eastern, Nat Geo Wild).
• Golf’s postseason begins.
The Barclays golf tournament continues today at Plainfield Country Club in Edison, N.J., (livestream at 8 a.m. Eastern, and 2 p.m., Golf Channel).
Though it’s the first of four events that serve as the PGA Tour’s version of the playoffs, called the FedEx Cup, there are a few notable no-shows.
• Start school right.
Whether back-to-school means college or high school, apps can help students stay organized and productive.
And new federal data shows that U.S. school lunches have become healthier.
BACK STORY
It started with one shop 50 years ago today, in Bridgeport, Conn. Today, there are about 27,000 Subway locations across the U.S. — nearly double the 14,000 McDonald’s.
Just a couple of months out of high school, Fred DeLuca teamed up with a family friend, Dr. Peter Buck, to open a submarine sandwich shop called Pete’s Super Submarines. On the first day, they sold 312 sandwiches, priced at 49 cents to 69 cents.
The store name Subway debuted in 1968 and spread quickly once the company started franchising in 1974. In 1984, the chain opened its first store outside the U.S., in Bahrain.
Subway, of course, didn’t invent the sub, and in fact, the first Blimpie shop opened a year earlier.
The origins of what you might call a bomber, hero, grinder, hoagie, po’ boy, spuckie, wedge, torpedo or zeppelin are murky, but the common roots lie mostly in Italian-American communities in the Northeast.
What’s clear is that the sandwiches’ popularity was enough for Subway to build an empire of 44,187 restaurants in more than 110 countries.
