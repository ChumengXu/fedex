Postpartum depression is a well-known condition, but we often shy away from discussing its sibling, antenatal depression - depression during pregnancy. The dilemma it poses is an agonizing one: Pregnant women often fear taking antidepressants, because pregnancy is treated as a "universal Lent in which a thousand talismanic things must be forsaken for the health of the child," as Andrew Solomon writes in this week's cover story. But not treating their mental illness, he argues, can be much more dangerous.
Daniel Alarcón writes about the shaky evidence behind the widespread laws that punish criminals for gang affiliation, and about the young men who are targeted for simply wearing the wrong color shirt. Carlo Rotella profiles Matthew Aucoin, the 25-year-old composer, conductor, poet, pianist and critic who may turn out to be opera's most promising talent in a generation, if he can handle the spotlight. Bruce Schoenfeld examines the group of California winemakers trying to redefine what wine should taste like, Francis Lam praises the "terrific black hole of meats" that is feijoada and Adam Davidson ponders the impossible conundrum of regulating banks.
