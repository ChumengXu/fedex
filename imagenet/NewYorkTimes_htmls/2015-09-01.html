Your Tuesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• The global race in the Arctic.
President Obama, in Alaska today, will propose investing in new Coast Guard icebreakers in the nation’s polar regions, to try to catch up with other countries, especially Russia, in gaining a foothold at the top of the world.
Icebreakers allow teams to navigate Arctic waters for longer portions of the year, which can enhance a country’s claims to territory.
On Monday, Mr. Obama called for urgent action to address climate change, and the White House quashed speculation about his post-presidency plans.
• Rise in murders.
At least 35 U.S. cities are reporting a startling rise in murders or other violent crimes this year, ending decades of declines in most cases.
Experts cite disparate reasons for the increase over last year, from rivalries among organized street gangs to a higher scrutiny of the use of police force.
Separately, video of the killing of a man by sheriff’s deputies in San Antonio last week appears to show that he had his hands up when the officers fired.
• The migration crisis.
The rush of migrants and asylum seekers in Europe, and the Continent’s inability so far to assist them in an organized way, may be starting to fray its commitment to erase old borders.
Our colleagues were with one group as they dashed across the border into Hungary. We also have answers to four central questions about the crisis.
• Insights into Clinton.
Our reporters are still poring over the 7,121 pages of Hillary Rodham Clinton’s emails released Monday night by the State Department by court order, after the revelation that she used a private server while she was secretary of state.
The emails seem to highlight that Mrs. Clinton was acutely interested in remaining current on political developments and on any reports of upheaval at the White House.
• Marriage licenses for all.
A Kentucky county clerk who has refused to issue marriage licenses to same-sex couples faces penalties today if she continues to balk.
The Supreme Court on Monday refused to hear the case of the clerk, who objects on religious grounds.
• Cultural destruction.
Satellite images confirm that the Temple of Baal in the ancient city of Palmyra, Syria, has been largely destroyed by Islamic State militants, the U.N. said.
• Decision in Tom Brady case?
A federal judge could rule as early as today on the N.F.L.’s four-game suspension of the star quarterback Tom Brady for the use of underinflated balls in a conference championship game.
His team, the Patriots, who went on to win the Super Bowl, host the league’s season opener against the Pittsburgh Steelers on Sept. 11.
MARKETS
• Wall Street stock futures are down more than 2 percent, indicating rough sledding today. European shares are down almost 3 percent.
An index today showed activity in China’s manufacturing and service sectors slowed in August. But the main Shanghai stock index fell only 1.3 percent.
• Oil futures dropped 3 percent today after huge gains over three days as bearish traders took profits.
The price of a barrel of the benchmark U.S. crude is around $47.63.
NOTEWORTHY
• New novels.
Jonathan Franzen’s fifth novel, “Purity,” is published today. It tells the story of a young Californian named Pip who is looking for her father, and a Julian Assange-like figure who is eager to get Pip to work for him.
And “The Story of the Last Child” is the fourth and final installment of the Neapolitan series, about female friendship, by the famously anonymous Italian novelist who writes under y the name Elena Ferrante.
• At the U.S. Open.
Roger Federer, Andy Murray and Stan Wawrinka take to the hardcourts today for their opening-round matches, as do Caroline Wozniacki, Petra Kvitova, Victoria Azarenka and Simona Halep (1 to 11 p.m. Eastern, ESPN, and we’ll be following the action live).
Serena Williams won her first match on Monday, but other top players were knocked out.
• Fewer smokers.
A government report released today says the smoking rate for American adults dropped to 17 percent last year, down from about 18 percent in 2013.
• Escape from society.
It’s a bumper year for spandex and faux fur at Burning Man, the counterculture festival where participants build art in the Nevada desert, only to burn much of it down.
• For your watch list?
The film “Up in the Air,” starring George Clooney and Anna Kendrick, is one of many movies and shows coming to Netflix this month. They include a new series, “Puffin Rock,” aimed at preschoolers.
• In memoriam.
Dr. Wayne W. Dyer, author of more than two dozen self-help and how-to-live books that sold millions of copies, died in Hawaii. He was 75.
BACK STORY
It’s Proms season.
No, not that capstone of American high schools, where one-hit wonders and pop ballads populate the playlist.
The Proms with a capital P are an eight-week festival of daily concerts in London. They’ve been broadcast worldwide on the BBC since 1927, making them perhaps the highest-profile concert program on the planet.
The Proms began 120 years ago as a series of outdoor concerts with people milling about, or promenading. The organizers aimed to make classical music widely available at an affordable price. Even today, tickets start at less than $8.
Through Sept. 12, orchestras and ensembles from around the world perform a nightly program of mostly classical music.
For each performance at the almost 6,000-capacity Royal Albert Hall, 1,000 arena seats are removed to make room for people, or “prommers,” to stand. Many other prommers stand in the gallery.
They can be a boisterous bunch. The prommers in the arena shout “heave” when a piano lid is lifted onstage, and they are always meet with a “ho” from the gallery prommers.
Prommers have also been known to chant crude phrases to visiting orchestras in their own languages. It’s all part of what makes the Proms unique.
