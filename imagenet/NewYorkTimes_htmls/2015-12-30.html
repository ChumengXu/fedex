Your Wednesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Double blow for extremists.
Iraq’s prime minister has his sights on flushing out the Islamic State terrorist group from the rest of the country after his military clears pockets of resistance in the crucial western city of Ramadi.
But that could take months or even years, despite reports that 10 leaders of the group were killed in recent airstrikes.
• On the campaign trail.
Donald J. Trump says he will spend heavily on TV ads in Iowa, New Hampshire and South Carolina; and it’s safe to say that the Trumps and the Clintons are no longer friends.
George E. Pataki, the former governor of New York who failed to make an impact in the race for the Republican presidential nomination, dropped out.
And — oops — there have been some big social media blunders by campaigns.
• Lifestyles of the rich.
Our special report shows how the wealthiest families in the U.S. are able to shape a tax policy that will allow them to shield their income. Some of them are giving much of the cash for presidential campaigns.
And Ethan Couch, a teenage fugitive caught in Mexico, could return to the U.S. today. A witness had testified that Mr. Couch suffers from “affluenza,” or psychological problems stemming from excessive privilege, and he was sentenced to probation for his role in a deadly drunken-driving crash in Texas.
• Policing in the U.S.
More police officers are facing criminal charges in the deaths of civilians, but the law gives the police the benefit of the doubt, and few are jailed.
In Chicago, Jason Van Dyke, the police officer charged with murder for the shooting death of Laquan McDonald, has pleaded not guilty, putting the case on a course toward trial.
• Flooding threatens Midwest.
The flooding in central Missouri and Illinois that has killed more than a dozen people is getting worse, with some residents evacuated and two interstate highways and hundreds of other roads shut.
The Mississippi River is rising so fast that it is on track to break the records of the Midwest flood of 1993, when 800 levees in Missouri were breached.
• The race for the bottom of the world.
Antarctica is supposed to be protected as a scientific preserve, but nations are rushing to stake their claim there.
They’re preparing for when the protective treaties expire, and for the strategic and commercial opportunities that exist right now.
BUSINESS
• The price of oil is down 2 percent today, reaching an annual low of $36.85 for a barrel of the U.S. benchmark crude.
After a 46 percent drop in 2014, the price is off 20 percent this year.
• Martin Shkreli’s former biotech company KaloBios Pharmaceuticals filed for bankruptcy. He was charged with securities fraud two weeks ago.
• After a 1 percent rise on Tuesday put Wall Street’s indexes back in positive territory for the year, stock futures forecast a decline today.
European stocks are down today, and Asian indexes ended mixed.
NOTEWORTHY
• Just tooling around.
Jerry Seinfeld and President Obama take turns driving a blue 1963 Corvette Sting Ray coupe around the South Lawn on the Season 7 debut of “Comedians in Cars Getting Coffee” (11:30 p.m. Eastern, streaming, Crackle).
• So, that’s a no?
The hoverboard — a two-wheeled, gliding motorized device that some New York City officials want to legalize — isn’t popular with the police there.
“I think anybody who rides these things is out of their minds,” the police commissioner said on Tuesday.
• On and off the football field.
A new report is calling attention to the link between head injuries and poorly maintained fields, especially those made of synthetic turf.
And the Philadelphia Eagles coach, Chip Kelly, was fired with one game to go in the season.
• For music lovers.
Discogs has become an online hub for record collectors, with a database of more than 6.5 million releases and nearly $100 million in sales of records in 2015.
And the digital music service Spotify is being sued for copyright infringement in a case that highlights an escalating fight over the complex system of royalties for online music.
• Visualizing 2015.
Our year in graphics is worth a quick tour.
• That’s “sir” to you.
Britain announces its New Year’s honors, including knighthoods and British Empire Medals, today.
• You have to see this.
A 12-foot giant squid was recently spotted in Toyama Bay in Japan.
BACK STORY
It’s fitting that the guitarist and singer Bo Diddley was one of the first inductees into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame. It was his rhythmic innovations that helped turn rhythm and blues into rock ’n’ roll.
When he began recording in the mid-1950s, his blend of children’s songs, blues, Mississippi Delta rhythms, chants and humor was one of a kind.
In 1955, he wrote and recorded a song called “Bo Diddley,” featuring his hypnotic signature “Bo Diddley beat.” It was one of rock ’n’ roll’s first hits.
Diddley was born Otha Ellas Bates on this day in 1928 in McComb, Miss., and died in 2008.
He may have taken his stage name from the diddley bow, a traditional African one-stringed guitar. Diddley, though, said he never played one.
The “Bo Diddley beat” has been used in hundreds of songs — including Bruce Springsteen’s “She’s the One” and U2’s “Desire” — and across generations. The Beatles and the Rolling Stones revered him.
At the Beatles’ first United States news conference in 1964, John Lennon was asked what he was most looking forward to seeing. “Bo Diddley,” he replied.
