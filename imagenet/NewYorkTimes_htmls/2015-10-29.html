Your Thursday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Finding their voices.
Senator Marco Rubio of Florida brushed off attacks from his former mentor Jeb Bush, while Senator Ted Cruz of Texas emerged as a champion of social conservatives at the Republican presidential debate Wednesday night.
Their performances overshadowed those of Ben Carson and Donald J. Trump, who have been in the campaign spotlight lately. (Highlights and fact-checking.)
Candidates and political strategists alike criticized the performance of the CNBC moderators, saying they offered “one gotcha question, one personal low blow after the other.”
• Passing the gavel.
In a formal vote today, Representative Paul D. Ryan of Wisconsin is set to be installed as House speaker, which is often called the second most powerful job in Washington.
The departing speaker, John A. Boehner, said, “It’s been a good run” as his 25 years in Congress draw to a close.
• The case for the Bin Laden raid.
Before President Obama ordered the raid on Osama bin Laden’s compound in May 2011, four administration lawyers developed rationales intended to overcome any legal obstacles.
Their justifications made it all but inevitable that a team of Navy SEALs would kill the fugitive Qaeda leader, not capture him, our reporting shows.
• Migrants trying to beat winter’s start.
The Greek Coast Guard saved about 240 people in one of its biggest rescue operations in the Aegean Sea this year, but three drowned.
More than 500,000 have entered Greece through its outlying islands this year, in what has become the biggest humanitarian crisis in Europe in decades. Some are boys ages 14 to 17, arriving alone and scared.
• Officer fired for tossing student.
The white sheriff’s deputy who is under federal investigation after flipping a black high school student out of her chair in South Carolina has been fired.
We look at the growing number of physical confrontations between officers and students in schools.
• South Sudan’s horrors.
Gang rape, the massacre of civilians and even cannibalism are among a long list of atrocities committed during the country’s continuing civil war, a new report from the African Union says.
BUSINESS
• U.S. economic growth probably slowed to an annual rate of about 1.6 percent in the third quarter from 3.9 percent in the second, a survey of economists forecast.
The Commerce Department releases its first estimate of the quarter’s gross domestic product this morning.
• Pfizer and Allergan are in talks that could lead to the biggest merger deal of the year, The Wall Street Journal reported.
Allergan, based in Dublin, has a market value of $113 billion. The pharmaceutical giant Pfizer, based in New York, was rebuffed by AstraZeneca in a merger offer last year.
• Deutsche Bank said today it would slash as many as 35,000 jobs over the next two years through sales of businesses and internal cuts.
The overhaul comes as the German company reported a loss of $6.6 billion in the quarter, driven by write-downs and litigation charges.
• Shell today reported a $7.4 billion loss for the third quarter as lower petroleum prices took a toll.
• Wall Street stock futures trading indicate a lower opening today. European and Asian shares are mixed.
NOTEWORTHY
• It’s Kansas City, again.
Johnny Cueto gave up only two hits to New York, as the Royals routed the Mets, 7-1, in Game 2 of the World Series on Wednesday night.
The Mets are down two games to none, but they return home for Game 3 on Friday night.
In pro football tonight, the New England Patriots, the N.F.L.’s highest-scoring team, host the Miami Dolphins, who have scored 82 points in their last two games (8:30 p.m. Eastern, CBS and N.F.L. Network).
• What to watch ...
An old favorite, “It’s the Great Pumpkin, Charlie Brown,” is back (8 p.m. Eastern, ABC).
• ... and how to watch.
Our tech columnist Brian X. Chen writes that the new Apple TV is the best streaming device for many people, but other video boxes may be better for some.
• A wild claim.
Salmon sold as “wild” in restaurants and grocery stores was actually not wild 43 percent of the time, according to a report based on DNA testing.
In most of the cases of mislabeling, the salmon was really farm-raised fish.
• Happy birthday, Internet.
The first host-to-host connection was made on the Arpanet — an experimental military computer network from which the Internet evolved — on this day in 1969.
BACK STORY
It’s not the World Cup final or the Super Bowl, but the ticket prices are in the same stratosphere.
The cheapest single ticket we found on StubHub for Saturday’s Rugby World Cup final between Australia and New Zealand in London was over $800 this morning.
The roots of both rugby and American football include the ancient game of harpastum. Teams were awarded points when players either kicked, ran or passed the ball across a goal line. Sound familiar?
Over the centuries, ball sports in Europe evolved into two popular games: one that primarily used feet (soccer) and one that mostly used hands (rugby).
British civil servants working in Canada brought rugby to North America. Students from McGill University in Montreal modified it and in 1874 took it to Harvard, which was playing a game that was a cross between soccer and rugby.
The Harvard players liked McGill’s version enough to incorporate its rules, and the composite game of American football was born.
So football, not rugby, went on to become the king of American sports. But the U.S. is the reigning Olympics rugby champion, having won the gold in 1920 and 1924, the last time the sport was played in the competition.
Next year, rugby returns to the Summer Games in Rio de Janeiro.
