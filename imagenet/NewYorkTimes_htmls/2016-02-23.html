Your Tuesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Decision day for Nevada Republicans.
Marco Rubio is betting that a strong finish in today’s presidential caucuses in Nevada will bolster his status as the best candidate to unite his fractured party.
His rival Ted Cruz hopes to regain his footing after firing his communications director who misrepresented comments by Mr. Rubio about the Bible.
Mr. Cruz and Donald J. Trump head toward Super Tuesday next week with the party’s biggest war chests. But Jeb Bush spent $130 million and has nothing to show for it.
• Our rising seas.
The oceans are rising faster than at any point in the last 28 centuries and humans are largely to blame, scientists report.
Their study forecast a rise of three to four feet by 2100, creating a need to possibly abandon the world’s coastal cities in the 22nd century. Greenhouse gas emissions from the burning of fossil fuels are seen as the main cause.
• Advantage F.B.I.?
The narrow focus the F.B.I. has on unlocking a single iPhone could help the agency in its case against Apple. When it comes to court orders for access to online services like iCloud, though, Apple repeatedly cooperates.
Our DealBook columnist asks whether Apple has a moral obligation to help the government, or its customers.
• Russia wants a closer look.
Moscow is requesting permission to upgrade the cameras it uses on surveillance flights over the U.S., which are permitted by the Open Skies Treaty.
The Obama administration must now decide whether to object to the Russian request. The treaty was signed to reduce the risk of war, primarily in Europe.
• Closing Guantánamo?
The Pentagon is expected to send a plan to Congress today for the closing of the U.S. prison at Guantánamo Bay, Cuba, which President Obama wants shut before he leaves office.
There are 91 prisoners there, but Congress has shown little interest in taking any action.
• Michigan shooting suspect denied bail.
Jason Dalton, a 45-year-old Uber driver in Kalamazoo with no known criminal history, was charged with six counts of murder and two counts of assault with intent to murder on Monday. He offered no clues about a possible motive.

Business

• Before the U.S. housing crisis, low-income buyers got too much of a house that they couldn’t afford. They’re now getting too little of a house that they can’t afford to repair, and deep-pocketed investors are profiting.
• Coders with a Ph.D. are a new breed of trader on Wall Street.
• U.S. stocks indexes rose 1.5 percent on Monday. Here’s a snapshot of world markets.

Noteworthy

• Surprise us, please.
Today is the deadline for members of the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences to vote for the Oscars, where the unexpected rarely seems to happen anymore.
The academy is looking to the host of Sunday’s ceremony, Chris Rock, to lift viewership as it deals with a deeper problem of diversity.
We watched every film in which blacks have been nominated for best actress or actor to glean what the academy values in their performances.
• Financial tips.
Most people don’t think about life insurance until they absolutely have to, but why wait? We offer you a back-to-basics buyer’s guide.
We also take a fresh look at credit card offers, as competition for customers means signing bonuses, like increased cash-back offers or rewards points.
• What to watch.
“Frontline” has a two-hour investigation into the heroin epidemic in the U.S., focusing on the experiences of four addicts in Seattle (9 p.m. Eastern, PBS, but check local listings).
Separately, PBS is starting a 24-hour channel dedicated to children’s programming.
And we recap the season finale of “The X-Files” and interview the creator of the show.
• New reads.
Among today’s nonfiction releases: “American Girls,” about teenagers and social media; “Rise and Fire,” on how the jump shot changed basketball; and “Sunny’s Bar,” which explores Red Hook, in Brooklyn, through its last working-class pub.
• You’re telling me!
One out of three Americans don’t get enough sleep, a federal study says.
• What to eat.
We have a recipe for deviled chicken thighs, and it takes just 20 minutes.
But if you’re stuck at work, there are some digital tools that can help steer you toward healthier eating.

Back Story

As schoolchildren in the U.S. learn, President Lincoln’s assassination came toward the end of the Civil War. He was shot on April 14, 1865, and died the following morning.
Some historians believe that there was also a secret plot to kill him before the war began, even before he took the oath of office.
Lincoln was traveling by train from Springfield, Ill., to Washington for his March 4, 1861, inauguration, and did so without a military escort because he disliked pomp.
He was planning to stop in about 70 cities and towns along the way. A schedule was announced, so that crowds could greet him along the way; but it could also make it easier for him to be ambushed.
Crowds in Baltimore gathered to greet him on this day in 1861, but all they saw were his wife, their sons and Lincoln’s private secretary.
Lincoln had traveled secretly through the city overnight, after his travel plans were changed — and not announced — when the detective Allan Pinkerton discovered a plot to kill the president-elect.
It centered around a barber named Cipriano Ferrandini, who led a network of Lincoln haters. (He was never indicted.)
By the time the plotters arrived at the appointed site in Baltimore, Lincoln was already in Washington, where he made a surprise early visit to the White House.
