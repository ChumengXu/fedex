Your Thursday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Aiming to recapture an Iraqi town.
A ground offensive, backed by American air power, to retake the western Iraqi town of Sinjar from Islamic State fighters began early today. One objective is to cut off a strategic supply line used by the militant group between Syria and the Iraqi city of Mosul.
One of our correspondents spent 10 days in northeastern Syria, and tells us of people “scrambling to fill the void left by the collapse of the Syrian state.”
• Racial tensions on campus.
The passion that ousted University of Missouri leaders on Monday, after protests over racial discrimination on campus, is spreading to other colleges across the country.
Many black students on the Columbia campus say that racial tensions were already woven into the fabric of everyday life there well before recent events.
• Republican race shifts.
Tuesday night’s debate opened a new season of the campaign shaped by glaring policy differences, from immigration and bank regulation to taxes and national security.
It’s a struggle over what defines a conservative.
• Narendra Modi in Britain.
India’s prime minister arrived in London today for a three-day visit during an unsettling time.
Mr. Modi’s party suffered a humiliating defeat on Sunday in the country’s third most populous state, and religious intolerance is rising.
• A young boy’s murder charge.
The charging of an 8-year-old in the killing of a 1-year-old girl in Alabama is leading to questions today about juvenile justice and how the authorities should approach these rare cases.
The case will be heard in family court and not by a jury. Prosecutors in other states have pursued murder charges against children not yet in their teenage years.
• At the White House.
President Obama today awards Capt. Florent A. Groberg of the U.S. Army the Medal of Honor for gallantry in Afghanistan.
And Ray LaHood, a Republican who served as transportation secretary, says in a new book that Mr. Obama gave up on his promise of bipartisanship.
BUSINESS
• Apple is working on a person-to-person payments service that would let people use their smartphones to send money to one another as easily as they send messages, according to a source.
• Greece’s public services are shut today, the result of the first general strike since the country’s leftist government came to power in January. Workers are protesting austerity measures imposed as part of Greece’s third bailout.
• T-Mobile’s new data plan for unlimited video streaming could challenge the Federal Communications Commission’s new net neutrality policy, experts say.
• The owner of About.com and Vimeo is offering to buy Angie’s List, the money-losing online review site for home repairs and other services, for about $512 million.
• Wall Street stock futures are little changed. European shares are down, and Asian indexes were mixed.
NOTEWORTHY
• A new find at the Great Pyramid?
Researchers in Egypt say they have detected “thermal anomalies” within the Great Pyramid of Giza. It may mean there is an undiscovered tomb there.
• More Americans are obese.
Federal health officials report today that the share of Americans who are obese has edged up to 38 percent, from 35 percent in 2011 and 12.
• Funds for Srebrenica.
Calling it a gesture of reconciliation, Serbia is donating $5.4 million to the Bosnian town of Srebrenica, where about 8,000 Muslim men and boys were massacred by Bosnian Serb forces in 1995.
• Actions speak louder than words.
Surveys show that men today are much more committed to sharing chores at home with women, but that in reality they do less housework and child care than they think they do.
• FIFA leadership battle.
The world soccer governing body approved five candidates to run for president in the February election to replace Sepp Blatter, who was suspended last month during a continuing corruption investigation. Two — Musa Bility and Michel Platini — were not admitted.
Mr. Blatter, 79, is in the hospital this week after a stress-related “breakdown.”
• On the gridiron.
The Buffalo Bills head coach Rex Ryan faces the New York Jets for the first time since they fired him in December. Here’s our preview (8:25 p.m. Eastern, NFL Network).
BACK STORY
Neil Young, the singer-songwriter, environmentalist and foreigner, turns 70 today.
Foreigner? Yes, he’s from north of the border. A native of Toronto, Mr. Young is one of many Canuck celebrities frequently taken for Americans.
Justin Bieber is fairly well known for being Canadian, but here are others you might not associate with the north of North America: Jim Carrey, Keanu Reeves, Rachel McAdams, Donald Sutherland, Michael J. Fox, William Shatner and Mike Myers.
It’s not just movie stars but also movie studios that have traces of Canada in them. Both Jack L. Warner of Warner Brothers and Louis B. Mayer of MGM were native Canadians.
Famous Canadians from other walks of life include Joni Mitchell, Glenn Gould, Drake, Shania Twain, Marshall McLuhan, Pamela Anderson, the Weeknd and Bat Masterson.
So what’s a Canuck, besides a hockey player in Vancouver, British Columbia? (For Canada, that’s akin to asking “What’s a Yankee?” in America.)
The origin of the word, which frequently refers not just to a Canadian but to a French Canadian, is a mystery. The term became popular in the mid-1800s and may come from the Hawaiian word for man, kanaka.
The Hawaiian connection could be because both Hawaiians and French Canadians worked in the fur trade in the Pacific Northwest.
