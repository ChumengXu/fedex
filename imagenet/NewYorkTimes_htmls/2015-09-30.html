Your Wednesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Heavy fighting in Afghanistan.
The struggle for control of the city of Kunduz, the hub for the country’s north, continues today, and it’s unclear whether the Taliban, which maintained control on Tuesday, or government forces have the upper hand.
Two U.S. airstrikes are the earliest signs of increased involvement. The Pentagon is calling the Taliban’s major gain “a setback,” though Afghan officials began warning of the militants’ advances on the city a year ago.
• Deadline looms over Congress.
Today is the final day of the U.S. fiscal year, and the House and Senate are expected to pass a stopgap spending measure to keep the government running through Dec. 11. If they don’t, the government will shut down at midnight.
Conservative Republicans don’t have enough votes to stop the short-term bill that extends government funding at current levels and that doesn’t include language to end federal funding for Planned Parenthood.
• Executions in the U.S.
Early this morning, Kelly Renee Gissendaner became the first woman put to death in Georgia in 70 years, after a state panel turned down an application for clemency that drew Pope Francis’ support. She was convicted of arranging her husband’s murder in 1997.
And an Oklahoma death row inmate is scheduled to be executed today for his role in a 1997 killing. His lawyers are calling for a hearing on new evidence that they say proves a co-defendant acted alone.
Francis, in his U.S. visit, urged Congress to abolish the death penalty.
• An audience with the pope.
Francis is also said to have met last week with Kim Davis, the county clerk in Kentucky who defied a court order to issue marriage licenses to gay couples. He gave her a rosary and told her to “stay strong,” her lawyer says.
• At the United Nations.
The Palestinian flag will be raised for the first time outside U.N. headquarters, where Mahmoud Abbas, the Palestinian president, will speak today. A new poll shows that a majority of Palestinians want him to resign.
President Obama is back at the White House, but Secretary of State John Kerry will remain at the U.N. for meetings all week.
MARKETS
• Wall Street stock futures are trending upward. European indexes are sharply higher, and Asian markets bounced back.
• Ralph Lauren is leaving the daily leadership of his fashion empire. His replacement, Stefan Larsson, is credited with reviving Old Navy and expanding H&M.
• The Keystone XL pipeline’s developer is withdrawing lawsuits seeking to gain access to the property of Nebraska landowners who oppose the project.
• Bank of America has until today to resubmit its capital plan to the Federal Reserve. The lender, the nation’s second largest, has to prove it has a better grasp on risk than in its first plan for the Fed’s stress test.
• Two Uber executives in France went on trial today on charges of running an illegal taxi organization.
• Amazon’s new $50 Fire tablet begins shipping today, as do new Fire HD and Fire Kids Edition tablets.
NOTEWORTHY
• Inside the Senate.
Nineteen interviews with Edward M. Kennedy, and 170 more with colleagues, aides and others about his 46-year career, are released today and offer detailed accounts of how legislative fights were won or lost.
Mr. Kennedy said he had to down scotch and sodas with a senior senator to earn spots on three committees.
• When a dollar isn’t a dollar.
Tonight’s auction of about 100 American coins minted in the late 1700s and early 1800s is expected to bring in millions of dollars.
One of the biggest attractions is a 1794 Flowing Hair Silver Dollar, with an estimated sale price between $3 million and $5 million.
• Star-crossed basketball star.
The Chicago Bulls’ Derrick Rose, the 2011 M.V.P. of the N.B.A., will have eye surgery today, after being elbowed during practice on Tuesday. It’s not yet clear when he’ll return to the court.
Rose missed all of the 2012-13 season and played only 10 games of the 2013-14 season because of injuries.
• Snowden stirs.
Edward J. Snowden, the exiled former National Security Agency contractor who disclosed the far-reaching scope of N.S.A. surveillance, joined Twitter, and the world noticed that he is “following” only one account: @NSAGov.
• Take a stroll in the sky.
In “The Walk,” a 3-D biographical drama opening at IMAX theaters today, Joseph Gordon-Levitt stars as Philippe Petit, a Frenchman who walked across a high wire strung between the World Trade Center towers in 1974.
Ben Kingsley plays his mentor, Robert Zemeckis (“Forrest Gump,” “Flight” and “Back to the Future”) directed, and it’s an NYT Critics’ pick.
• Season premiere.
The Academy Award winner Marcia Gay Harden stars in “Code Black” — another in a long line of emergency-room dramas (10 p.m. Eastern, CBS).
BACK STORY
Today is the deadline set by Visa, MasterCard, American Express and other major card networks to switch in the U.S. to a more secure payment technology, one that is common in most of the world.
Retailers that don’t upgrade to systems that read the E.M.V. microchips on the front of more and more U.S. cards will be financially responsible for some fraudulent charges.
We have credit cards today because a man realized after eating at a Manhattan restaurant in 1949 that he had forgotten his wallet. His wife drove in from the suburbs to pay the bill.
That episode gave the man’s lawyer an idea for a new payment method: the Diners Club charge card, which came to be used for much more than dining. American Express, which debuted in 1958, asked its users to pay their monthly balances in full.
Other cards, called “credit” cards, did not. Bank of America offered the first one, also in 1958. That same year, Hilton Hotels expanded its affinity card into the Carte Blanche credit card.
And that very first Diners Club charge? It was lunch at a steakhouse.
