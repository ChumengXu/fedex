Your Friday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Seeking an end to Syria’s war.
Secretary of State John Kerry’s biggest challenge at international talks in Vienna today is to try to make peace between Saudi Arabia and Iran, rivals who are using Syria as a proxy war for Mideast dominance.
No one from the Syrian government will join the talks, but the inclusion of Iran means that all the major foreign players will sit together for the first time to consider a resolution to the war that has left more than 250,000 people dead.
Separately, an Iranian-American scholar is reported to have been arrested in Tehran, signaling increased risks for dual citizens from the U.S.
• On Capitol Hill.
The Senate voted early this morning, after an all-night session, to approve the bipartisan budget deal that was reached this week between congressional leaders and the White House.
The bill now heads to President Obama’s desk for his signature.
• A grim breast cancer milestone.
For the first time, the incidence of breast cancer among black women has risen to equal that of white women, the American Cancer Society is reporting.
The finding is worrisome because breast cancer has been more deadly in black women.
And some women with cancer and others are questioning the worth of all that pink merchandise for Breast Cancer Awareness Month.
• What China needs to grow.
While China is abandoning its one-child policy, experts say that economic overhauls and investments in technology, health care and education are more important to the country’s future.
Even so, the expense of raising a second child is overwhelming for most Chinese families.
• Roman Polanski decision.
A court in Poland is expected to decide today on a U.S. extradition request for the filmmaker Roman Polanski, who was convicted in California in 1977 for unlawful sex with a 13-year-old girl.
Mr. Polanski lives in France, but he’s making a film in Poland, where he was born.
• Prep-school sentencing.
Owen Labrie, once a star student at the elite St. Paul’s School in New Hampshire, was sentenced on Thursday to a year in jail for misdemeanors in connection with an assault on a 15-year-old.
He will also be on probation for five years and must register as a sex offender.
Many U.S. states are struggling with what to do with sex offenders after prison.
BUSINESS
• Retailers are priming the pump for holiday sales.
Target, Walmart and Macy’s say they will open again this year on Thanksgiving, while some smaller chains are taking a stand against holiday sales creep.
• The S.E.C. issues rules today to let small companies crowdfund from friends, family or any interested investors.
Since the 1930s, only those with $1 million in net worth or annual income of $200,000 were allowed to invest in start-ups.
• Google appears to be favoring its Android operating system over its Chrome OS, working to allow its low-cost Chromebook computing devices to work on Android.
• Wall Street stock futures show a slight rise. European shares are up, and Asian indexes were mostly lower.

In “Burnt,” a bad-boy chef (Bradley Cooper) who is recovering from a drug addiction heads to London to take over a top restaurant.

Garth Risk Hallberg’s “City on Fire,” about sex, drugs and rock ’n’ roll in New York City in the 1970s, debuts at No. 5 on the hardcover fiction list.
And Brandon Stanton, the blogger behind “Humans of New York: Stories,” sits atop the nonfiction list with his new book, which takes a “photographic census” of the city.

Citi Field in Queens hosts its first World Series game tonight as the Mets look for their first win against Kansas City (8 p.m. Eastern, Fox). Game 4 is Saturday and Game 5, if necessary, is Sunday.
And the Triple Crown champion American Pharoah’s final race is in Lexington, Ky., in the $5 million Breeders’ Cup Classic. He races Saturday at 4 p.m. Eastern (NBCSN or live stream).

BACK STORY
Anheuser-Busch InBev and SABMiller are negotiating terms for a blockbuster beer merger worth over $100 billion.
If it winds up happening next week, will the storied names of Anheuser and Busch live on?
The names will live on in St. Louis, where the names were first united. At the corporate level, they survived one megamerger, after a Brazilian company acquired Anheuser-Busch in 2008, but it’s unclear what’s next. (The company posted $4.4 billion in quarterly profit today).
Adolphus Busch left Germany in 1857 at age 18 to join his brothers in St. Louis and ended up in the brewing-supplies business, where he came to know Eberhard Anheuser, a soap maker who had invested in a struggling brewery.
Adolphus also came to know Eberhard’s daughter Lilly at about the same time that his brother was dating Lilly’s sister. A double wedding resulted, in 1861.
It was Mr. Busch who turned around his father-in-law’s brewery. But amid intense competition locally, Mr. Busch wanted to expand outside the area, even nationally.
He did so by investing in two technologies: pasteurization, which cut down spoilage; and refrigerated railroad cars, which let him sell far and wide. A beer empire was born.
Mr. Busch wasn’t much of a beer drinker himself. He preferred wine.
