Your Thursday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• A Russian spy’s death.
A British report into the 2006 poisoning death of Alexander V. Litvinenko, a former K.G.B. officer who turned against the Kremlin, concluded today that his killing was “probably” approved by President Vladimir V. Putin.
Mr. Litvinenko, 43, died in London 22 days after ingesting green tea laced with polonium 210 — a rare and highly toxic isotope.
His death prompted a chill in relations between Moscow and the West.
• Flint’s public health emergency.
Nearly 300 pages of emails show that the administration of Gov. Rick Snyder was initially dismissive of the water crisis in the poverty-stricken, black-majority city of Flint, Mich.
Residents complained for months about the water’s foul odor, odd color and strange health effects before state officials conceded that the tap water was contaminated with lead.
• Jittery global markets.
The European Central Bank is expected to leave its stimulus program unchanged today, despite the fall in eurozone stocks and plummeting oil prices. The region has dangerously low inflation.
Wall Street stocks sank on Wednesday, but may be ripe for a turnaround. European indexes are higher, and markets in Asia sank today.
Here are six tips for when the markets are skidding.
• Republican split.
Ted Cruz’s attention appears focused on Donald J. Trump, the man he spent much of last year praising, but dampening Mr. Trump’s popularity is proving complicated.
Bob Dole, the 1996 Republican presidential nominee, is warning members of his party not to vote for Mr. Cruz. And the Republican establishment is on the sidelines as outsiders thrive.
Our profile of Mr. Cruz’s time as a Supreme Court clerk shows that he was an ardent advocate of the death penalty.
• Diversity in the spotlight.
The Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences is expected to announce measures aimed at making its Oscar nominations more diverse. One change might be an immediate return to a 10-film field of best picture nominees.
And in sports, the N.F.L. has six minority coaches, a stagnant number that research suggests may stem from a lack of minority assistants.
• Weather worries.
New York and New England could be spared the worst of a snowstorm that meteorologists say is likely to reach the mid-Atlantic on Friday.
The Washington area could get one to two feet of snow, but details about where the storm will hit hardest are still unclear.
BUSINESS
• Amazon, Apple, Facebook, Google and Microsoft are gaining dominance in their sectors, more power in new sectors and are better protected from competition.
• Two research scientists are among five people indicted on charges of stealing trade secrets about drugs to treat cancer and other diseases from GlaxoSmithKline, the British pharmaceuticals giant.
• The World Economic Forum, a gathering for the world’s most powerful political and business leaders, has plenty of glitz and glamour. But women make up less than 20 percent of attendees.
NOTEWORTHY
• New reads.
Among this week’s nonfiction releases: Jane Mayer’s “Dark Money,” about the brothers Charles G. and David H. Koch; Ian Buruma’s “Their Promised Land,” about his grandparents’ marriage and their survival through two world wars; and Jo Merchant’s “Cure: A Journey Into the Science of Mind Over Body.”
And in fiction: Bill Bryson’s “The Road to Little Dribbling;” Garth Greenwell’s “What Belongs to You;” and Elizabeth McKenzie’s “The Portable Veblen.”
• A prehistoric massacre.
A dig in Kenya has uncovered the remains of a slaughter about 10,000 years ago, suggesting that warfare may have existed even before humans became farmers.
• College admission overhauls?
About 80 educators, including top admissions officials at major U.S. universities, have endorsed a proposal to rely less on standardized tests and to focus more on applicants who show a passion for learning and for volunteer projects.
• Sundance joins gun debate.
The annual Sundance Film Festival opens today in Park City, Utah, where at least four gun-themed films will have their world premieres; they all advocate tighter gun control in the U.S.
• Court watch.
A Pennsylvania prosecutor says that a former district attorney’s decade-old promise not to prosecute Bill Cosby has no merit.
And Track Palin, the eldest child of Sarah Palin, has been charged in a domestic violence case after his girlfriend said he punched her in the head and threatened to fire a rifle.
• Heroics.
Darius Fleming, a New England Patriots player, rescued a woman trapped in a burning car last week and injured his leg in the process. He tried to keep it quiet, but the news trickled out. Despite his injury, he played in last weekend’s playoff victory.
And the actor Jamie Foxx helped pull a man from a burning truck this week.
BACK STORY
A style called the “New Look” arrived in 1947, just two years after the end of World War II, when fabric was considered a luxury that was rationed.
Christian Dior, who was born in France on this day in 1905, was the designer behind those luxurious feminine fashions (cinched waist, long hemline and full skirt).
Their strong rebuke of war austerity made them instantly popular. Paris became the world’s fashion capital anew, and a grateful French government rewarded Dior with the Legion of Honor.
Dior’s artistic leanings first became apparent at age 23, when he opened a small art gallery, after having attended Sciences Po in Paris.
After his family lost much of its wealth in the tumult of the 1930s, Dior began sketching designs for fashion houses.
During the war, he dressed the wives of Nazi officers and French collaborators in order to survive.
His younger sister Catherine joined the French Resistance, and was later sent to a concentration camp, from which she was freed in 1945.
In 1947, Dior named his first fragrance Miss Dior as a tribute to her. He died in 1957 at the pinnacle of the fashion world.
