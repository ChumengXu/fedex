Your Wednesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• The president’s magic numbers.
President Obama is one vote away from the 34 needed in the Senate to uphold a possible White House veto of a Republican resolution disapproving the Iran nuclear deal. Next, he’ll aim to secure the 41 votes needed to filibuster the resolution.
Secretary of State John Kerry, in a speech today in Philadelphia, defends the agreement he negotiated.
• Migrants’ journeys.
Thousands of migrants arrived in Greece today, as the government there holds talks on how to cope with the waves of people reaching its shores.
And thousands more arrived in Germany. They were met by the police, who escorted them on their first step — registration with the bureaucracy.
• Washington’s climate aid.
President Obama today announces federal grant programs for Arctic communities whose shorelines and infrastructure are crumbling as warming seas melt their foundations.
He’ll venture north of the Arctic Circle, the first sitting president to do so, visiting Kotzebue, which built a sea wall in 2012 to deal with storm surges brought on by the loss of sea ice.
• Rising Republicans.
Like Donald J. Trump, the retired neurosurgeon Ben Carson has never held elected office, but he is starting to rival Mr. Trump in the polls in Iowa.
And Carly Fiorina, the former Hewlett-Packard chief executive, will benefit from CNN’s change in criteria for picking candidates for the next debate, scheduled for two weeks from today.
• Gloom in Mexico.
Amid widespread economic and political pessimism, President Enrique Peña Nieto gives his annual state of the nation address in Mexico City today.
And Guatemala’s president is barred by a judge’s ruling on Tuesday from leaving the country. Congress there also stripped him of immunity from prosecution, five months after revelations about a customs fraud ring.
• Football movie altered.
Sony Pictures softened parts of the film “Concussion,” about the death and dementia professional football players endure, and starring Will Smith, to avoid antagonizing the N.F.L., dozens of emails exposed by hackers show.
• Freddie Gray case moves ahead.
Baltimore is prepared for protests today as a pretrial hearing gets underway in the case of six police officers charged in the April death of Freddie Gray.
• Illinois manhunt.
The authorities in northern Illinois are continuing a sprawling search today for three people suspected in the fatal shooting of a police lieutenant, the latest slaying of a member of law enforcement in the U.S.
MARKETS
• Global stock markets are choppy so far today, but Wall Street futures indicate a positive opening after a nearly 3 percent plunge on Tuesday.
• Federal Reserve banks give policy makers anecdotal evidence of the health of the U.S. economy in their “beige book” this afternoon.
• India is the latest country to have Google in its sights.
An arm of India’s antitrust agency says the company is abusing its dominant position in search and online advertising there, according to people familiar with a report that has not been made public.
• McDonald’s franchises start offering all-day breakfast next month in an effort to reverse slumping sales and traffic.
And 7-Eleven is expanding its home delivery service to Chicago, Los Angeles and parts of New York. Washington and Boston are next. No Slurpees, though.
• The world’s biggest toymaker, Lego, just got bigger. The Danish firm today posted increases in profit (31 percent) and sales (23 percent) for the first half of the year, led by a robust Asia business.
NOTEWORTHY
• Film kickoff.
The 11-day Venice International Film Festival opens today with a mountain-climbing disaster drama, “Everest,” starring Jake Gyllenhaal.
Opening in the U.S. today are “A Walk in the Woods,” the horror film “Creep” and a documentary about the rise and fall of the Black Panther Party.
• Hello and farewell, Maria.
You most likely spent part of your childhood with Sonia Manzano, better known as Maria on “Sesame Street.” She just retired and told us about her childhood, as the puppets prepare for their first season without her since 1970.
And the Arabic cousin of “Sesame Street,” called “Iftah Ya Simsim,” returns this week to the Middle East for the first time since 1990.
• At the U.S. Open.
Serena Williams plays this afternoon, and her sister Venus also joins the second-round action tonight.
The defending men’s champion, Marin Cilic; the top seed, Novak Djokovic; and Rafael Nadal also play today (1 to 6 p.m. Eastern on ESPN, 6 to 11 p.m. on ESPN 2, and all day on our live blog).
• Older at heart.
Based on risk factors, nearly three out of four American adults have a heart that’s “older” than the rest of their body, according to a new government report.
For men, the average predicted “heart age” was nearly eight years greater than their real ages. For women, it was about five and a half years.
• Pure Janet.
A newly demure Janet Jackson, 49, kicked off this week her first world tour in four years. “Unbreakable,” her first studio album in seven years, is out this fall.
BACK STORY
Many people across the world think politics stinks, and that is particularly true today in Beirut, Lebanon.
The city’s main landfill closed this summer, leading to garbage being left on the streets and to a series of antigovernment protests. On Tuesday night, riot police officers cleared protesters from the Environment Ministry.
Unsurprisingly, trash is one of the most in-your-face signs of political dysfunction, no matter where it piles up.
In 2008, Silvio Berlusconi won Italy’s national elections, in large part by promising to clean up the garbage festering on the streets of Naples during long periods of political paralysis.
And in the 1980s, a barge piled high with New York trash roamed the coast of North America for five months, rejected by governments in six states and three countries.
As the piles of trash grow in Lebanon, so do calls for political change. The garbage is a rare point of unity in a country where top government posts are divided among Shiites, Sunnis and Christians.
But the paralysis is likely to endure. Today, for the 28th time since May 2014, the Lebanese Parliament is expected to fail to elect a president because of a boycott by lawmakers.
Victoria Shannon contributed reporting.
