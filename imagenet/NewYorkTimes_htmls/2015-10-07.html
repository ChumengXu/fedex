Your Wednesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Migrant operation begins.
European Union vessels today are patrolling the southern Mediterranean, aiming to board and seize boats being used for human smuggling, though some are skeptical about the program’s effectiveness.
France’s president, François Hollande, and Germany’s chancellor, Angela Merkel, address the European Parliament today on the migrant crisis.
• Nobel Prize in Chemistry.
Tomas Lindahl of Sweden, Paul L. Modrich of the U.S. and Aziz Sancar of Turkey were awarded the prize today for their studies of DNA repair.
• Crowdfunding for Syria.
At the White House’s request, Kickstarter is raising money for Syrian refugees in its first social service campaign.
In Syria, experts believe the Islamic State militant group is using chemical weapons using chlorine and sulfur mustard.
• Death penalty cases at the high court.
Supreme Court justices are scheduled today to hear the cases of three people whose death sentences were overturned by the Kansas Supreme Court last year. All three faced lethal injection.
Last term, the justices upheld Oklahoma’s lethal-injection drug protocol. They will also consider this month whether death sentences in several states violate the Constitution.
• Day of freedom nears for many.
The Justice Department will release about 6,000 inmates from federal prison in less than a month, many of whom are nonviolent drug dealers.
It’s part of a bipartisan effort to reduce prison overcrowding in the U.S., which has a quarter of the world’s prison population.
• Focus on workers.
President Obama speaks today at the White House Summit on Worker Voice and joins a conversation with workers, employers, unions and other advocates at coworker.org.
• A bleak anniversary.
Today is the 14th anniversary of the U.S-led invasion of Afghanistan, which sought, among other things, to topple the Taliban.
But the Taliban’s recent gains and the U.S. airstrike on a hospital appear to have restarted a debate in the Obama administration about whether to move forward with plans to cut by about half the current American force of 10,000 soldiers.
• Behind mass killings.
Experts see mass shootings less as isolated expressions of rage and more as acts that build on previous rampages.
The most recent, in Oregon, has renewed worries for U.S. colleges and schools.

MARKETS

• The United Automobile Workers union set tonight as the deadline for Fiat Chrysler to agree to a new contract or risk a nationwide strike in the U.S. by the company’s 36,000 hourly workers.
• Anheuser-Busch InBev raised its bid to acquire SABMiller to $104 billion, after the rejection of two earlier proposals to combine two of the world’s largest brewers.
• Samsung Electronics ended its two-year streak of shrinking profits, saying today in a preliminary report that its operating profit had increased nearly 80 percent in the most recent quarter.
• Peet’s Coffee & Tea is buying Stumptown Coffee Roasters, a high-end specialty coffee company with a cult following, but plans to keep the chains separate.
• The New York attorney general began an inquiry into the prospect that employees of daily fantasy football sites won lucrative payouts based on inside information not available to the public.
• Wall Street stock futures indicate rising prices this morning. European and Asia shares are higher.

NOTEWORTHY

• Beans sprout.
Across China, grown-ups are wearing plastic decorations on their heads in the shapes of vegetables, fruit and flowers, a fad that has no single explanation.
It began with a humble bean sprout clipped to the hair, erect like a flagpole. Now heads are sporting clovers, sunflowers, chrysanthemums, lavender, mushrooms, chiles, cherries, gourds and pine trees.
• One and done.
The Houston Astros’ ace Dallas Keuchel pitched six shutout innings at Yankee Stadium on Tuesday night, helping end New York’s season on the first day of the playoffs.
Tonight’s single-elimination game is the Chicago Cubs at the Pittsburgh Pirates (8 p.m. Eastern, TBS).
• The pope’s next Americas trip.
Pope Francis will visit Mexico next year, the Vatican announced, including a stop at the shrine of Our Lady of Guadalupe, the patron of Mexico and of the Americas, in Mexico City.
• Pumpkin pie forecast.
Record rainfall in June could make pumpkin pies in November hard to come by.
Libby, a top maker of canned pumpkin, says yields could be off by as much as one-third this year in Illinois, home of about 90 percent of U.S. pumpkins.
But pumpkins themselves are not expected to be in short supply.
• The puck drops.
The N.H.L. season opens tonight with new rules for overtime. The defending Stanley Cup champion Chicago Blackhawks host the New York Rangers (8 p.m., NBCSN) and the San Jose Sharks are at the Los Angeles Kings (10:30 p.m., NBCSN).
• Season premieres.
“American Horror Story: Hotel” returns for a fifth season, with murders, a musty Los Angeles setting and Lady Gaga (10 p.m., FX).
And Hulu’s latest original, “Casual,” is a melancholy comedy about 30-something siblings — she’s divorced, he’s depressed — sharing a house (with her teenage daughter).
Here’s the episode recap for “Scream Queens.”
• Down on drones.
The F.A.A. wants to levy a record $1.9 million fine against an aerial photography company, SkyPan International, for flying drones in crowded New York and Chicago airspace without permission.
And a new California law intends to block paparazzi from using drones by adding the airspace above someone’s land to the definition of invasion of property.

BACK STORY

Last week was not the first time that the front page of The New York Times reported that there is water on Mars.
More than 100 years ago, in an article about the astronomer Percival Lowell, the Page 1 headline even went as far as to declare that Mars was inhabited, at least in the judgment of the professor.
Mr. Lowell, who died in 1916, was long a believer that the “canals” visible on the planet’s surface via telescope were not geological formations, but were built there.
“It is a direct sequitur from this that the planet is at present the abode of intelligent constructive life,” he said in the 1907 Times article. We have our reporter David W. Dunlap to thank for unearthing this gem.
The astronomer’s belief in a planetwide complex of irrigation canals spurred a wave of novels about Martians, particularly books by Edgar Rice Burroughs and H. G. Wells.
By the 1930s, scientists had deemed water on Mars unlikely, and science fiction moved on to conjecture about a dry planet, with classics by Ray Bradbury and Arthur C. Clarke.
After NASA’s confirmation last week of liquid water — a critical requirement for life — watch for a return to the tradition of water-soaked Mars fiction.
