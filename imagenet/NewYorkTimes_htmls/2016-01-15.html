Your Friday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Republican front-runners tangle.
Donald J. Trump and Ted Cruz sharply attacked each other at the Republican candidates’ debate Thursday night, with Mr. Trump questioning the Canadian-born Mr. Cruz’s eligibility to be president and the Texas senator denouncing Mr. Trump’s “New York values.”
Who won? Here’s what the pundits say. One of our political reporters writes that Mr. Cruz outmaneuvered everybody else onstage.
But Mr. Trump’s popularity has climbed to its highest level yet, according to a national NBC/Wall Street Journal poll released on Thursday.
We have a quick debate summary, more extensive highlights, fact-checking of the candidates’ statements and a full transcript to help you catch up.
• Next up, the Democrats.
As the Republican candidates leave South Carolina, the Democrats move in this weekend for Sunday’s prime-time debate (9 p.m. Eastern, NBC).
Bernie Sanders has received the endorsement of Paul G. Kirk Jr., a former Democratic National Committee chairman.
• A new Ebola case in West Africa.
A body has tested positive for Ebola in Sierra Leone, hours after the World Health Organization declared an end to the deadliest outbreak of the virus on record.
Officials had cautioned that despite the declaration, more flare-ups of the disease were likely.
• White House climate move.
The Obama administration will announce today an end to new coal mining leases on public lands.
President Obama will be interviewed at 2:15 p.m. Eastern on the White House’s YouTube channel. Some questions submitted on social media, with the hashtag #YouTubeAsksObama, will be posed to the president.
• At the U.S. Supreme Court.
Cases added to the court’s docket today could include a challenge from 26 states to President Obama’s immigration overhaul, as well as the appeal by former Gov. Bob McDonnell of Virginia of his corruption conviction.
• Should Harvard be free?
Outsider candidates running for the Board of Overseers at Harvard are asking that question, saying that the university makes so much money from its $37.6 billion endowment that it should stop charging tuition to undergraduates.
They say a free-of-charge Harvard would no longer have trouble balancing its student body for racial or ethnic diversity.
BUSINESS
• Asian stocks sank today, and European markets are lower. Wall Street stock futures point to a significant drop at the opening.
• Wells Fargo and Citigroup report fourth-quarter results today.
• The U.S. Commerce Department releases retail sales data today for December, the most crucial month for retailers’ balance sheets.
• Goldman Sachs will pay up to $5 billion to resolve claims stemming from the marketing and selling of faulty mortgage securities to investors.
• The North American International Auto Show in Detroit opens to the public on Saturday (slide show).
The U.S. government is proposing spending $4 billion to encourage the development of self-driving cars.
NOTEWORTHY
• At the movies.
“13 Hours: The Secret Soldiers of Benghazi” premieres across the U.S. today.
Directed by Michael Bay, known for four “Transformers” movies, the action film is based on a book by Mitchell Zuckoff about the 2012 attack on a U.S. diplomatic compound in Libya that killed Ambassador J. Christopher Stevens and an American technician.
Separately, the Oscar nominees — a nearly all-white list — have been announced.
• Super Bowl memories, and dreams.
There’s no copy of the original broadcast of Super Bowl I, but a stitched-together version airs for the first time since the game was played 49 years ago (8 p.m. Eastern, NFL Network).
Eight teams still have hopes of playing in Super Bowl 50 next month. Here’s this weekend’s divisional playoff schedule and previews. We’ve also got an early look at the commercials.
• Panorama of premieres.
The 46th season of “Sesame Street” begins Saturday morning on HBO, not PBS. It’ll have a new, upscale look.
On Sunday, Rashida Jones stars in “Angie Tribeca,” a police procedural satire (9 p.m. TBS). Paul Giamatti and Damian Lewis play adversaries in “Billions,” a drama about Wall Street power brokers. (10 p.m., Showtime).
And PBS has “Mercy Street” (10 p.m.), an original costume drama about nurses on opposite sides of the Civil War.
• Walk among the stars.
Tim Peake and Tim Kopra are scheduled to take a spacewalk today outside the International Space Station.
• Tight quarters.
The Museum of Natural History in New York unveils today the cast of a 122-foot-long dinosaur, one of the largest ever found — too big, in fact, to fit completely in its new home.
BACK STORY
The modern encyclopedia burst out of the Enlightenment as a lofty compendium of human intelligence. It had no peer for centuries.
Then, in 2000, the online encyclopedia Nupedia was launched. You may not remember it, because it was a flop.
But you’ve probably heard of the follow-up effort: Wikipedia, which went live 15 years ago today.
Wikipedia wasn’t a surefire success. Its creators had stumbled with Nupedia, whose peer-review process took so long that it produced only a few dozen articles.
This time they embraced the idea of a wiki (derived from the Hawaiian word for “quick”), a software tool invented in 1994 that allows collaborative editing. Today, there are more than five million articles in English Wikipedia. There are many millions more in another 300 languages.
The crowdsourcing of Wikipedia has sometimes inspired concerns about accuracy. But it got a boost when a study by the journal Nature concluded that its science-oriented entries were only slightly less accurate than those in the Encyclopaedia Britannica.
While Britannica is the oldest continuously published encyclopedia in the English language, Wikipedia can claim to continuously publish around the clock, around the world.
