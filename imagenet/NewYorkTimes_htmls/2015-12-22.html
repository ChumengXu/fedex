Your Tuesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• No indictment in Sandra Bland’s death.
A Texas grand jury declined to indict anyone in the death of Sandra Bland, who was found hanged in her jail cell three days after she was arrested during a routine traffic stop in July.
Grand jurors will reconvene next month to discuss other aspects of the case, a prosecutor says.
• Six U.S. soldiers killed in Afghanistan.
A Taliban suicide bomber on a motorcycle struck a military convoy near Bagram Air Base on Monday, in one of the deadliest attacks against American forces this year. The Taliban are making a major push in southern Helmand Province, home to the base.
Among the dead was Joseph Lemm, 45, a New York police detective serving in the Air National Guard.
• Not a natural disaster.
Sunday’s “man-made” landslide of mud and construction debris in Shenzhen, near Hong Kong, is symbolic of rapid construction in China. Our interactive shows how it happened.
Thirty-three buildings, including apartments, worker dormitories and factories, were buried or toppled. At least 80 people are still missing.
• Fewer gun limits, more gun deaths.
The gun homicide rate in Missouri was 16 percent higher in the six years since 2007, when the state repealed the requirement for comprehensive background checks and purchase permits, than it had been in the six years before.
In the same period, the national rate fell 11 percent.
• Political roundup.
The major push by Congress to wrap up the year is giving plenty of insight into the players and policy fights coming in 2016.
On the campaign trail, Donald J. Trump’s rise is dividing his black celebrity friends. Mr. Trump is also demanding that Hillary Clinton apologize for saying that terrorists were using him as a recruiting tool.
Lindsey Graham’s failed bid for the Republican nomination has one silver lining: The candidate gave voters some humorous one-liners during his campaign.
And Chelsea Clinton is expecting her second child.
• Season’s greetings, from Capitol Hill.
We reached out to all 100 senators and asked for their 2015 holiday cards.
• Culture war in the dining hall.
Students at Oberlin College in Ohio are accusing the campus dining hall of cultural appropriation and insensitivity because of what they say is a poor effort at multicultural cooking.
• Martin Shkreli is fired, again.
Martin Shkreli, who was arrested last week on securities fraud charges and posted $5 million in bail, has been ousted from his second pharmaceutical company, KaloBios.
What makes the Shkreli case interesting is that a lawyer has been charged as an accomplice for not protecting the first company, Retrophin, which Mr. Shkreli is accused of using essentially as a personal piggy bank.
BUSINESS
• Apple is pushing back against lawmakers seeking court-ordered access to iPhone communications, as governments threaten to soften encryption.
• The New York City skyline is growing at a pace not seen since the postwar boom, and promotional materials for some of the new towers have omitted photos of their supertall neighbors.
• Debt buyers are using the courts to sue consumers and collect debt, and are then preventing those same consumers from using the courts to challenge the companies’ tactics.

NOTEWORTHY
• A gentle landing.
A SpaceX rocket came back to Earth after launching satellites into orbit.
The launch and landing could become a familiar sight in spaceflight, allowing rockets to be refurbished and reused.
• N.F.L. suspends star.
The N.F.L. is suspending Odell Beckham Jr., the New York Giants’ star wide receiver, for one game for his flagrant hit on Sunday against a defenseless Carolina Panthers player.
• Fresh reads.
Amy Cuddy, a Harvard Business School professor who might be best known for her TED talk on body language, releases “Presence” today.
Gregg Easterbrook’s “The Game’s Not Over: In Defense of Football” is out in time for college bowl games and the N.F.L. playoffs in January. (Full disclosure: Mr. Easterbrook recently joined The Upshot.)
• Comedian in chief.
President Obama will ride with Jerry Seinfeld in a ’63 Corvette Sting Ray and will talk about what it’s like to live in the White House, in an episode of Mr. Seinfeld’s web series “Comedians in Cars Getting Coffee,” to be released on Dec. 30.
• Queen of the charts.
Adele’s “25” is at the top of the Billboard album chart for a fourth straight week.
The album has sold 5.98 million copies, more than Taylor Swift’s “1989” (5.57 million), and it is now the best-selling album since Adele’s previous one, “21,” in 2011.
BACK STORY
The day for “El Gordo” — meaning “The Fat One” — is here.
It is the name of Spain’s Christmas lottery — the world’s largest, with about $2.4 billion in prize money — not the rotund fellow who lives at the North Pole.
While it is gambling, some might say that it captures the holiday spirit because people buy tickets together and the tax-exempt prizes are widely shared. It has turned entire villages into millionaires.
Well over half of the Spanish population participates in El Gordo. Lottery playing is very popular in Spain, where King Carlos III began the drawings as a vehicle for charity in 1763.
He also began the tradition of having the winners selected by orphans. Today, children from the San Ildefonso School (formerly an orphanage) will stand beside two giant spinning tumblers, which will spit out the wooden balls with the winning numbers, on a stage in Madrid. Most of the country will be watching the event, broadcast live on TV and radio.
Nothing interrupts the lottery, not even the civil war, from 1936 to 1939. At that time, the Republicans and the Fascists each had their own draw.
Fittingly, the Italian word “lotto” means destiny or fate, and the Dutch word “loterij” comes from the noun “lot,” also meaning “fate.”
