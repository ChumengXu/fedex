Your Tuesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Two weeks to Iowa.
Many of the presidential candidates are fanning across the state’s 99 counties today, in preparation for the Feb. 1 caucuses there. But national issues have displaced the local matters that once shaped early primary contests.
Hillary Clinton positioned herself during Sunday’s debate as the candidate most capable of advancing President Obama’s policies, while Bernie Sanders took on Mrs. Clinton more forcefully. The Clinton campaign is preparing for a nomination fight that could stretch into late April or early May.
On the Republican side, Heidi Nelson Cruz took a leave of absence from Goldman Sachs to court wealthy donors for her husband, Ted Cruz. While the British Parliament debated whether to bar Donald J. Trump, he was trying to sway evangelicals.
• U.S.-Iran relations.
The landmark nuclear agreement between Iran and global powers is now in effect. Longstanding sanctions have been lifted and the regional landscape is being reshaped, but President Obama imposed modest new sanctions for banned missile tests.
And with three Americans long held in Iran returning to the U.S., Mr. Obama urged young Iranians to “pursue a new path” with the West. Seven Iranians are part of the prisoner swap between the estranged nations.
• Focus on refugees.
The U.S. Senate is to consider this week new screening procedures for Syrian and Iraqi refugees.
• Economic stimulus for France.
President François Hollande has announced a $2.2 billion package to combat what he called “an economic and social emergency.”
• El Chapo’s anniversary.
The Mexican drug kingpin Joaquín Guzmán Loera escaped from prison for the first time on this day in 2001.
He was arrested for a third time this month when the movie star Sean Penn, and a big order of tacos, gave the authorities the break they needed to recapture him.
BUSINESS
• China’s economy grew at its slowest pace since 2009 in the fourth quarter. That’s no comfort to investors worrying about the health of the world’s second-largest economy.
• U.S. stock indexes open today for the first time since Friday, when they tumbled more than 2 percent. Here are snapshots of the U.S. and world markets.
• Oil prices have also been plummeting. The global oversupply will most likely only grow as Iran increases production.
• Walmart will close 269 stores around the world. The closings could eliminate 16,000 jobs, including 10,000 in the U.S.
OVER THE WEEKEND
• The University of Cincinnati agreed to pay $4.85 million to the family of an unarmed black man who was killed by one of its police officers in July.
• In West Africa, a terrorist attack in Burkina Faso’s capital left 29 people dead and dozens wounded.
• Drug overdoses are driving up the death rate of young white adults in the U.S. to its highest level in decades, a New York Times analysis found.
• President Obama declared a state of emergency in Flint, Mich., where residents have been exposed to toxic levels of lead in water.
• The Times looks at how the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. and other prominent black Americans were regarded when they died.
• Taiwan elected its first female president. Tsai Ing-wen faces a weak economy and China’s growing might.
• The mosquito-borne Zika virus is spreading in Latin America; and Mr. Obama’s Malaria Initiative faces serious challenges.
• In the N.F.L. playoffs, the Patriots, Broncos, Panthers and Cardinals advanced to this weekend’s conference championship games.
• Glenn Frey, a founder of the band the Eagles, died at 67.
• “Ride Along 2,” starring Ice Cube and Kevin Hart, ended the monthlong run of “Star Wars: The Force Awakens” as the No.1 film in North America.
• Catching up on TV: Episode recaps for “Downton Abbey,” and the series premiere of “Billions.”
NOTEWORTHY
• Income inequality rising fast.
Just 62 people own as much wealth as the 3.5 billion people in the bottom half of the world’s income scale, the charity Oxfam has reported.
Five years ago, it took 388 billionaires to reach that mark.
• At the Australian Open.
Rafael Nadal and Venus Williams lost in their first-round matches today in Melbourne.
And tennis officials deny that the sport has a match-fixing problem. Here’s what to look forward to in the 2016 season.
• What to watch.
“Supplements and Safety” is a new “Frontline” documentary, in collaboration with The Times, investigating the hidden dangers of vitamins and supplements (10 p.m. Eastern, PBS, but check local listings).
BACK STORY
The death in 1849 of Edgar Allan Poe, the writer and poet recognized for inventing the detective story, gave way to a mystery many decades later.
Poe was born on this day in Boston, but he’s buried in Baltimore, where he lived, married and died. Each year on the night of his birthday, a person (or persons) known as the Poe Toaster would dress in black, raise a glass and leave a half-full bottle of cognac and three red roses at the poet’s grave.
The roses were believed to honor three people buried there: Poe, his wife and his mother-in-law. In his poem “To F-,” Poe wrote of his earthly path, “Drear Path, alas! where grows / not even one lonely rose.” The significance of the cognac is unknown.
Although small crowds would gather outside the cemetery to see the toaster, the person was never identified. Perhaps no one wanted to interfere with the dark homage.
The Poe Toaster has failed to appear since 2009, the bicentennial of the poet’s birth, ending a tradition that began at least 60 years earlier.
Poe once wrote: “There are some secrets which do not permit themselves to be told.”
