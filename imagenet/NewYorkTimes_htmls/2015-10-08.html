Your Thursday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Renewed push for gun control.
Senate Democrats plan to block virtually all other bills starting today until their gun control measures get a vote. It’s in response to last week’s and other mass killings.
At the same time, many people around Roseburg, Ore., where the massacre took place, say the shooting rampage only increased their belief in the importance of owning guns.
• Elsewhere on Capitol Hill.
A preliminary vote for House speaker, to replace John A. Boehner, begins behind closed doors at noon today. Representative Kevin McCarthy of California remains the favorite.
And Gen. John F. Campbell, the U.S. commander in Afghanistan, testifies before Congress on the hospital bombing in the city of Kunduz. Three groups — NATO, a joint U.S.-Afghan group and the Defense Department — are investigating the attack.
• Political maneuverings.
Hillary Clinton unveils proposals today to curb risk-taking on Wall Street and hold more financial managers accountable for misconduct, a campaign aide said.
She also announced that she doesn’t support President Obama’s Trans-Pacific Partnership, which she helped draft as secretary of state.
Both positions could help her fend off a challenge from Senator Bernie Sanders in the race for the Democratic Party’s presidential nomination.
• Syria’s war, in Europe.
NATO’s chief said today that the alliance was ready to defend its allies, including Turkey, against any threats. Turkey is concerned about Russia’s military buildup in neighboring Syria.
As they try to cope with the surge of Syrian refugees, European Union officials discuss today a list of countries whose citizens would not be eligible for political asylum.
• Hispanics in the limelight.
President Obama delivers the main address tonight at the Congressional Hispanic Caucus Institute’s awards gala in Washington. It streams live at 7 p.m.
It overlaps with the first Latin American Music Awards show (8 p.m., Telemundo).
• He said what?
Rupert Murdoch, the chief of News Corporation, suggested that President Obama isn’t a “real black president” in a tweet that showed his support for Ben Carson, a black man running for the Republican presidential nomination.
BUSINESS
• Dell is in advanced talks to buy EMC, the data storage business, potentially uniting two stalwarts of the technology industry, people with knowledge of the talks say.
• For the second time, the U.A.W. and Fiat Chrysler reached a tentative agreement on a new labor contract today.
The deal faces a vote by 36,000 hourly workers and 4,000 salaried union members, who turned down last week’s settlement.
• Deutsche Bank, the giant German bank with a big presence on Wall Street, said it expected a loss of nearly $7 billion in the third quarter.
• Amazon stepped into the handcrafted-goods ring today with an online arts-and-crafts bazaar called Handmade at Amazon that takes aim at a market dominated by Etsy.
• Wall Street stock futures are trending lower. European and Asian indexes are mixed.
NOTEWORTHY
• Around the bases.
The Chicago Cubs defeated the Pittsburgh Pirates, 4-0, in a wild-card game on Wednesday night to advance to the division series against the St. Louis Cardinals, beginning Friday.
And the Toronto Blue Jays host their first playoff game in 22 years, against the Texas Rangers, for the start of a best-of-five division series (3:30 p.m. Eastern, Fox Sports 1).
• Trump’s court date.
A leading Republican presidential contender, Donald J. Trump, doesn’t want offshore wind turbines ruining the view near one of his golf courses in Scotland.
So he’s taking his case against the proposal to Britain’s Supreme Court today. A decision is not expected right away.
• Rock and rollers.
First-time nominees for the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame this year include Janet Jackson, Chicago, the Cars, Cheap Trick, Steve Miller, the J.B.s and Los Lobos.
The disco band Chic earns a record 10th nomination. Fans can vote, and the choices are announced in December.
• Curtains up, for a record.
It’s the 30th anniversary of “Les Misérables,” the world’s longest-running musical, in London. Proceeds from tonight’s special performance at the Queen’s Theatre will aid the Save the Children charity in Syria. The show is also in revival on Broadway.
• Don’t miss this.
Watch Russia’s president, Vladimir V. Putin, celebrate his 63rd birthday by teaming up with N.H.L. legends to play hockey against Russian tycoons and officials. He scored seven goals as his team won, 15-10.

BACK STORY
Seventy years ago today, President Harry S. Truman announced that the secrets of the atomic bomb would be shared only with Britain and Canada.
The U.S., of course, had used the bombs to attack Japan in World War II and was the only country with nuclear capability.
Polls showed that 70 percent of Americans and 90 percent of congressmen didn’t want to share atomic secrets with anyone.
But Britain and Canada already had some of that knowledge because their scientists had come to the U.S. to help build the bombs.
Should the U.S. also share information with the Soviet Union, another World War II ally, in negotiations on banning the bomb as a weapon? When the war ended, President Truman decided no.
The Soviets got it anyway — thanks partly to Klaus Fuchs, a German scientist and Communist who fled Hitler and was part of the British mission at Los Alamos, N.M., from 1944 to 1946.
He gave design details to the Soviets that some say advanced their atomic bomb program by at least two years.
The Soviets exploded a bomb that resembled that of the Americans about four years later. And the nuclear arms race was on.
