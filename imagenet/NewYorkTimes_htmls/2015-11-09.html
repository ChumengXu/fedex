Your Monday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Israel’s leader in Washington.
Benjamin Netanyahu meets with President Obama today in his first visit to the White House since the Iran nuclear accord, which the prime minister strongly opposed.
The encounter is likely to be strictly transactional, as the two leaders have an uneasy relationship. They will discuss extending a 10-year military aid agreement for Israel.
• Myanmar’s opposition claims victory.
The party of Daw Aung San Suu Kyi, the Nobel Peace Prize laureate, says it is confident of a landslide victory in parliamentary elections, while the governing military-backed party acknowledged its poor showing.
Sunday’s election was the first time in 25 years that voters in Myanmar were able to choose their leaders freely.
• Relying on New Hampshire.
Some lagging Republicans are staking their presidential candidacies on one-on-one campaigning in the state.
And Ben Carson is the latest candidate to be challenged on the truthfulness of some of his statements. Confronted with inaccuracies, several others have ducked, dodged and ridiculed assertions, mostly from media outlets they dislike.
• “A real game changer.”
That’s what an international sports lawyer says about a global report to be released today that will show a new level of corruption in track and field.
The document is expected to describe a pervasive culture of doping among Russia’s sports programs and to implicate international track officials.
BUSINESS
• Volkswagen is expected to announce today that it will offer cash to U.S. owners of its diesel cars to recover some of the good will it lost after admitting the vehicles had been programmed to cheat on emissions tests.
• Bonuses in the financial industry this year are expected to fall 5 percent to 10 percent, according to an annual report being released today, the first decrease since 2011.
Investment and commercial banking bonuses will be especially hurt, down 30 percent from 2009 levels, the study says.
• The world economy will see growth of only 2.9 percent this year, according to today’s forecast from the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development.
• Maggi noodles are back on shelves in India today, five months after the popular snack made by Nestlé was found to contain lead above permissible limits.
• Wall Street stock futures show a small decline today. European shares are down slightly, and Asian indexes ended widely mixed, with the main Chinese barometer up another 1.6 percent.
OVER THE WEEKEND
• The presidents of China and Taiwan met for the first time since 1949, agreeing on small steps to bridge their divisions.
• Sierra Leone was declared free of Ebola, but the outbreak persists in neighboring Guinea.
• Two Louisiana police officers were charged in connection with the shooting death of a 6-year-old boy during a pursuit of his father in a sport utility vehicle. The boy’s funeral is today.
• The U.S. Supreme Court on Friday took up a case about whether some religious employers must provide free insurance coverage for birth control under the Affordable Care Act, the fourth time it has considered an aspect of the health care law.
• Black football players at the University of Missouri said they would refuse to play until the university president resigns or is fired. They are protesting the administration’s handling of racial tensions.
• “Spectre,” the latest James Bond film, was the weekend box office winner in North America. Meet the family that controls the 007 franchise.
• Catching up on TV: A review of Donald J. Trump on “Saturday Night Live,” and episode recaps for “The Knick,” “The Affair,” “Homeland,” “The Walking Dead” and “The Leftovers.”
NOTEWORTHY
• Football roundup.
The Carolina Panthers and the New England Patriots stayed undefeated, the Denver Broncos lost their first game, and the Pittsburgh Steelers lost quarterback Ben Roethlisberger to injury in Sunday’s N.F.L. action.
Alabama handed L.S.U. its first loss, and Nebraska shocked Michigan State with a 30-yard touchdown pass with 17 seconds left.
• Masterpieces for sale.
“Nu couché” (“Reclining Nude”), a 1917-18 canvas by Modigliani, is expected to sell for more than $100 million tonight. The previous auction record for one of his works is $70.7 million.
Auctions of big-ticket 20th- and 21st-century art are taking place all week in New York. It all got started Sunday night with a “crossover” sale.
• A French “Banksy” invades N.Y.
The anonymous Paris street artist with the pseudonym Invader arrived in New York over the weekend with plans to put up artful mosaics on buildings in the city.
• In memoriam.
Yitzhak Navon, who took part in the founding of Israel and served as its fifth president, died at his home in Jerusalem. He was 94.
BACK STORY
The Slinky, which first appeared in the Gimbels department store in Philadelphia 70 years ago this month, didn’t start out as a toy.
A mechanical engineer at a shipyard in Philadelphia, Richard James, was trying to come up with an anti-vibration device for ship instruments.
He knocked some springs off a desk and was startled when they took slinking steps, almost as if they were strolling away. He saw their potential as a plaything.
The simplicity of a Slinky belies its scientific complexity.
Each ring of a slinking Slinky is pulled up by another and pulled down by gravity in equal amounts.
Try holding the top of a Slinky, while letting the bottom dangle over a step. The bottom of a Slinky doesn’t move until you let go and the top of the Slinky comes down and is completely compressed.
Astronauts on the shuttle Discovery in 1985 found that the Slinky did not behave in weightlessness the way it does on Earth.
One astronaut said: “It sort of droops.”
More than 300 million Slinkys have been sold since Mr. James and his wife, Betty, demonstrated the toy at Gimbels.
