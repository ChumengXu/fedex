Your Monday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• New slide in stocks.
Global markets are plunging today, with stocks across Asia sliding sharply, led by a free fall in China.
The main Shanghai index was down 8.4 percent, erasing its gains so far this year.
Our Your Money columnist offers some tips and consolation to individual investors.
• Relaxed and ready.
President Obama, back from a two-week vacation, promotes his climate agenda at the National Clean Energy Summit in Las Vegas today, looking ahead to U.N.-sponsored climate negotiations in Paris in December.
Mr. Obama faces a daunting September schedule, including legislative deadlines and visits from world leaders.
• After the foiled train attack.
The attempted attack by an armed gunman aboard a high-speed train in Europe on Friday may push the Continent into a new climate of uncertainty, security experts fear, because of the nearly impossible task of monitoring all suspected militants.
Three Americans who subdued the gunman on the Paris-bound train are resisting being called .
• The sentencing of James E. Holmes.
At least 100 victims and witnesses of the July 20, 2012, attack that killed 12 people and wounded 70 at a Colorado movie theater are expected to testify, beginning today, about the impact it has had on their lives.
Jurors have determined that the gunman will spend the rest of his life in prison without parole, but he will be formally sentenced this week.
• Speculation about Biden.
Vice President Joseph R. Biden Jr. met with Senator Elizabeth Warren of Massachusetts over the weekend, the latest indication that he is seriously considering a bid for the White House.
And evidence suggests that Donald J. Trump’s dominance in Republican primary polling is no summer fling.
• One year after the Gaza cease-fire.
None of the nearly 18,000 homes destroyed or severely damaged in last summer’s war are habitable.
And our reporters walked “in war’s path” to show how routine walks for residents of Gaza and Israel are full of reminders of the war.
• Air show death toll rising.
The number of people killed when a fighter jet crashed onto a busy road near an air show in England on Saturday could climb to nearly 20, British authorities said today. Seven deaths have been confirmed.
MARKETS
• Oil prices are tanking. A barrel of the U.S. benchmark crude was down 4 percent to $38.76 today, while the European benchmark fell to $43.38.
• Privately held start-ups with billion-dollar valuations now number at least 131, according to the research firm CB Insights.
It identified an additional 50 companies that might be the next to turn into “unicorns,” so called because of their rarity.
OVER THE WEEKEND
• North and South Korea talked all weekend and into Monday about breaking a tense military standoff.
• The British Embassy reopened in Iran’s capital, Tehran, four years after the ambassador was expelled in retaliation for his country’s support of sanctions on Iran.
• Firefighters across the West saw no relief as wildfires continued raging.
• Militants from the Islamic State destroyed an ancient temple, an architectural treasure, in the ruins of Palmyra in Syria.
• Violent clashes between thousands of protesters and the police broke out in Beirut, Lebanon. The protesters are angry about the government’s inability to remove enormous heaps of garbage from the streets.
• Nepal opened Mount Everest to climbers for the first time since a deadly avalanche in April.
• A jury awarded Michael Jordan $8.9 million in his lawsuit against a grocery store chain that used his name in an ad without his permission.
• The Chinese writer Liu Cixin has won the 2015 Hugo Award for best science-fiction novel, a first for a Chinese writer. “The Three-Body Problem” is the first volume of a trilogy that has been a best seller in China.
• “Straight Outta Compton” repeated as the North American box-office winner over the weekend, despite its glossing over of Dr. Dre’s history of physically abusing women.
• Catching up on TV: Episode recaps for “Hannibal,” “Masters of Sex” and the premiere of “Fear the Walking Dead.”
NOTEWORTHY
• No-tipping trend.
Prompted by a series of new minimum-wage proposals, an expanding number of restaurants are experimenting with no-tipping policies and mandatory service charges.
Although such charges are common around the globe, restaurant tipping is deeply ingrained in the American psyche.
• “Bemusement park” opens.
The British graffiti artist Banksy’s latest creation is a satirical take on theme parks called “Dismaland,” featuring works by about 60 artists. We went for a visit.
• Sports milestones.
Ohio State, the defending national college football champion, became the first unanimous No. 1 selection in the 65-year history of The Associated Press’s preseason poll.
Usain Bolt defeated Justin Gatlin to win gold in the 100-meter dash at the world championships in Beijing on Sunday.
On Friday, Mike Fiers of the Houston Astros fired the fifth no-hitter in the Major Leagues this season, for a 3-0 win over the Los Angeles Dodgers.
• In memoriam.
Doudou N’diaye Rose, a master drummer and bandleader from Senegal who played with Miles Davis, Dizzy Gillespie, the Rolling Stones and Peter Gabriel, died in Dakar. He was 85.
Jimmy Evert, a highly regarded tennis instructor whose students included his daughter Chris and Jennifer Capriati, died at age 91 in Fort Lauderdale, Fla.
BACK STORY
Given last month’s laid-back introduction of Windows 10, it’s hard to imagine what a big deal Windows 95 was 20 years ago today.
Buyers lined up at midnight at computer stores across the U.S.; an ad campaign featured the Rolling Stones’s “Start Me Up,” at a cost of millions of dollars; the Empire State Building shined colored lights for a business for the first time; the front page of The Times of London carried Windows 95 ads...
All of this for a product that, as one pundit put it, doesn’t really do anything on its own.
Yet in hindsight, Windows 95 was such a breakthrough in personal computing that some of the hype was probably unnecessary for an upgrade to an operating system that went on to capture 97 percent of the global computer market.
Noting its simple interface, one midnight shopper said at the time, “Windows 95 is to computing what color TV was to black and white TV.”
It also prominently featured software for easy access to the Internet.
That thrust Microsoft into an epic antitrust battle with the U.S. Justice Department.
But it also instantly put the just-burgeoning World Wide Web within the reach of the masses faster than anything else until then.
Victoria Shannon contributed reporting.
