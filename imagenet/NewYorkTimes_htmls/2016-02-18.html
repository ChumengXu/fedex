Your Thursday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Next step in U.S.-Cuba thaw.
President Obama will become the first U.S. leader to visit Cuba in nearly nine decades next month. Calvin Coolidge was the first and only to do so, in 1928.
An announcement is expected today. Plans to normalize relations between the two nations were made public at the end of 2014, and this would be the biggest step after more than 50 years of estrangement.
• The U.S. Supreme Court vacancy.
Liberal and conservative advocacy forces have mobilized in record time to fight the battle over a nomination on three fronts: the White House, Congress and the campaign trail.
Some senators appear ready to flip-flop on their positions on judicial nominees. We asked all of them where they stand on whether President Obama should select Antonin Scalia’s successor.
Some African-Americans see the move by Republicans to delay confirmation hearings and a vote as another attempt to deny the legitimacy of the country’s first black president.
• The day in politics.
Marco Rubio won the endorsement of Nikki R. Haley, the governor of South Carolina. Jeb Bush is fighting for his political life in Saturday’s primary there, and the more Donald J. Trump defies the party, the more his supporters cheer.
Mr. Trump, Mr. Bush and John R. Kasich will appear at a town-hall-style meeting (8 p.m. Eastern, CNN).
MSNBC will host a forum in Nevada with Bernie Sanders and Hillary Clinton at 9 p.m. Eastern. We’ve taken a look at the underrated power of black voters in the Democratic primaries.
• Apple versus the government.
The company is fighting a court order to unlock an iPhone used by one of the gunmen in San Bernardino, Calif., in December, and others in the tech industry have reacted cautiously and quietly.
We explain Apple’s situation in a Q. and A., and look into whether the authorities should be able to access your smartphone.
• European Union’s crucial moment.
The British prime minister, David Cameron, will try today to persuade 27 other European leaders to renegotiate the terms of his country’s membership in the European Union.
Mr. Cameron has promised to hold a referendum on membership by the end of 2017, but if he gets his way he could hold it as soon as June.
• Help for Syrians.
The U.N. has begun sending aid convoys to five besieged areas.
Germany is urging Syria’s president, Bashar al-Assad, to reach an agreement on a no-fly zone in the country.
• Nevada rancher indicted.
A federal grand jury has indicted Cliven Bundy and four others in connection with an armed standoff in 2014 over unpaid grazing fees.

• The Fed left out an assessment of its economic outlook after the latest meeting of its policy-making committee because officials simply didn’t know what to say.
• A backlash is growing in Washington as Chinese companies try to acquire American ones.

The company is ending its contract with the boxing champion Manny Pacquiao, who described gay people as “worse than animals.”
Pacquiao, a congressman in the Philippines, is favored to win a Senate seat in May’s elections.

Major league pitchers and catchers are reporting today for their first workouts. Success in spring training typically signals a good regular season, but as some disappointed teams and players know, that’s not always the case.
Do you have high hopes for your team? Then don’t read this.
• Unpublished black history.
Recent entries in our monthlong project included a look at Sammy Davis Jr., Zora Neale Hurston and Harlem.
Separately, the Black Reel Awards honoring achievements of African-Americans in film and television will be announced tonight in Washington.
• Dueling definitions.
Mayor Bill de Blasio tweeted a photo of a Carnegie Deli sandwich labeled “America” in reply to Jeb Bush’s tweet of a photo of a gun with the caption “America.”
• How about a salad?
Tonight’s recipe is a Canlis salad with bread, to save time to get ahead on Friday’s recipe for make-ahead coquilles St.-Jacques.

Back Story

The Academy Awards are in 10 days, and one lucky cinephile will be able to show off his or her own trophy of sorts after an auction today.
What’s for sale is the stuff that Hollywood dreams are made of — a 45-box archive of the author Mario Puzo’s papers.
When Puzo was 45 years old and in debt, triumphs in the literary and film worlds may have seemed only a dream. He had made $6,500 off his first two novels.
Then he followed the writing adage: Write what you know. Puzo knew some Mafia tales, and he decided to write a book about Italian-Americans in organized crime.
His novel “The Godfather” (1969), a multigenerational epic about the Corleone family from Sicily, rocketed to No. 1 and was on the New York Times best-seller list for more than a year. It has sold more than 21 million copies worldwide.
The director Francis Ford Coppola later immortalized “The Godfather” in three films. The trilogy’s Oscar haul totaled 28 nominations and 9 wins.
Puzo’s 744-page draft of the novel, the 1965 Olympia typewriter on which he is thought to have written it, and his binder of the director’s notes during filming are among the treasures up for auction.
