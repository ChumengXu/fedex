Your Wednesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Pivotal day for Republicans.
The Republican Party’s presidential candidates will be in Boulder, Colo., tonight for their third debate, with a focus on economic issues (8 p.m. Eastern, CNBC).
The dynamics of the debate may be altered because there is a new front-runner in the polls, Ben Carson.
And House Republicans hold a conference vote today on whether to select Paul D. Ryan to succeed John A. Boehner as speaker. The full House votes on Thursday, Mr. Boehner’s last day after almost five years as speaker.
• Ex-speaker Hastert in court.
The two charges against J. Dennis Hastert, the former House speaker, should be revealed today when he files a plea agreement in federal court in Chicago.
The guilty plea he is expected to enter is in connection with accusations that he agreed to pay $3.5 million in hush money to hide claims of past sexual misconduct and that he lied about it to the F.B.I.
• Life as an ISIS captive.
In their first interviews, dozens of Arab prisoners of the Islamic State militant group who were freed in a military raid last week described their beatings and torture as well as daily life under exacting restrictions.
On Tuesday, Defense Secretary Ashton B. Carter said ground raids would be launched, if necessary, to target the extremist group.
• Next steps for the budget.
The House vote on the government’s budget plan is due today, but Senate action is not yet nailed down.
The agreement would prevent a sharp increase in Medicare premiums and a deep cut in Social Security benefits for disabled workers.
• It’s now a federal case.
The Justice Department will investigate an attack at a high school in Columbia, S.C., in which a white police officer is seen in a video pulling a black female student backward in her desk and dragging her.
BUSINESS
• Volkswagen today posted its first decline in quarterly profit in more than a decade, as it set aside $7.4 billion to help cover the fallout from the company’s emissions-cheating scandal.
• Walgreens is snapping up another pharmacy chain, Rite Aid, for $9.4 billion, but this combination may draw U.S. government antitrust scrutiny.
• Most economists expect no change from the Federal Reserve today, as the case for increasing interest rates has weakened since the summer.
• Northrop Grumman, maker of the B-2 bomber, beat a Boeing-Lockheed Martin team to win the Pentagon’s biggest contract in more than 10 years.
The contract for the next long-range strike bomber starts at $21.4 billion.
• Wall Street stock futures are edging ahead. European shares are largely higher, and Asia indexes closed mostly lower.
On Tuesday, Apple stock was up in after-hours trading after its quarterly profit report surpassed expectations, while IBM stock was down after it disclosed an S.E.C. investigation.
NOTEWORTHY
• The Royals in 14.
Early this morning, Kansas City’s Eric Hosmer drove in the winning run in the bottom of the 14th inning, as the Royals edged the Mets, 5-4, in the longest Game 1 in World Series history.
For four minutes, the game disappeared from the air. Game 2 is tonight (8 p.m. Eastern, Fox).
• Out there.
NASA’s Cassini spacecraft today dives through an icy spray erupting from the Saturn moon Enceladus, looking for evidence of water and thus possible life.
• Student test scores slip in U.S.
The math skills of American students have dropped for the first time since 1990, according to results of a nationwide test released today by the Education Department.
Officials said the decline in scores was unexpected, but that it could be related to changes ushered in by the Common Core standards.
• Harassment in game culture.
SXSW Interactive, the annual tech gathering in Austin, Tex., is facing a backlash over a decision to cancel two panels on video games.
They were expected to touch on GamerGate, a loosely connected online movement that coalesced to counteract criticism of sexism in video games and game culture.
• Retiring is now her goal.
Abby Wambach, the leading scorer in international soccer history with 184 goals, says she is retiring at the end of the year.
• Pre-Halloween chocolates.
The Salon du Chocolat, a global meeting of chocolatiers, begins today in Paris. There’s a fashion show featuring chocolate “couture” and the final of the World Chocolate Masters, a competition for chocolate and pastry chefs.
It’s also National Chocolate Day in the U.S., which means deals and freebies.
BACK STORY
We’ve been down this road before: Federal highway funding is expected to run out on Thursday. Ho-hum.
In the end, we expect Congress to come through with another extension to let the Highway Trust Fund finance transportation infrastructure improvements. Those are badly needed, as American roads get a D grade from civil engineers.
The nation’s poor roads led President Dwight D. Eisenhower to support the Federal-Aid Highway Act in 1956, authorizing the creation of what came to be more than 45,000 miles of mostly toll-free high-speed roads.
Mr. Eisenhower’s passion for better roads went back to 1919. As a young lieutenant general back from World War I, he was part of an 80-vehicle military convoy that took 62 days to travel to San Francisco from Washington.
The vehicles took a beating on the mostly unpaved roads, when they weren’t slipping into a ditch or getting blown off a cliff. Crossing water sometimes meant building a bridge.
“Difficult, tiring, and fun,” is what he called the trip, made at an average crawl of 6 miles per hour.
Mr. Eisenhower’s unforgettable road trip in 1919 ended up being a boon for American drivers.
