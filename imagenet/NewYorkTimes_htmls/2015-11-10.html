Your Tuesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Economic plans take center stage.
Eight Republican presidential candidates hold a fourth debate, which will focus on the economy, in Milwaukee tonight (9 p.m. Eastern, Fox Business Network). The “undercard” debate begins at 7.
And “Destiny and Power,” the biography of the first President Bush, is out today.
• Racial turmoil on campus.
The University of Missouri has announced plans to offer diversity training to all new students, as well as faculty and staff members, and the governing board will appoint an officer to oversee diversity and equality at all four campuses.
The changes come after months of student and faculty protests over racial issues led to the resignations of the president of the university and the chancellor of the flagship campus on Monday.
Reports of racism and bigotry have ignited protests at colleges across the U.S., and social media has amplified students’ messages beyond campuses.
• What Britain wants.
Prime Minister David Cameron today outlined his objectives for overhauling the European Union ahead of a referendum on Britain’s membership in the bloc, to be held by the end of 2017.
He wants to exclude Britain from the promise of an “ever closer union,” and to restrict welfare payments for European migrants.
• Setback for Obama’s immigration plans.
A federal appeals court said on Monday that President Obama could not move forward with his plans to provide up to five million immigrants with work permits and protection from deportation.
Mr. Obama’s lawyers can appeal the decision to the Supreme Court for consideration next year.
And Mr. Obama now has a personal Facebook page.
• On Capitol Hill.
The Senate debates a bill to fund the Department of Veterans Affairs, after it passed in the House. The bill, which is expected to pass as soon as today, would also ban moving the remaining 112 prisoners held at Guantánamo Bay, Cuba, to the U.S.
• Use of long-acting birth control rises.
The share of American women on birth control who use long-acting reversible methods like intrauterine devices and implants has nearly doubled in recent years, the federal government reports today.
• Show us the evidence, Kremlin says.
Russia today is denying allegations in a World Anti-Doping Agency report about systemic state-backed cheating, released on Monday.
BUSINESS
• United Automobile Workers officials endorsed a proposed labor agreement with Ford that is slightly richer than the contracts with General Motors and Fiat Chrysler.
• Campbell Soup is altering the recipe for its famous chicken noodle soup, whittling down the ingredients to 20 from 30. Global soup sales have stagnated since 2012.
• Canadian Pacific, one of Canada’s biggest railroad operators, has held preliminary discussions about a merger with Norfolk Southern.
• NASA will be posting job openings for astronauts next month. The annual salary ranges from $66,026 to $144,566.
• The Food and Drug Administration is setting a limit on the amount of sugar recommended in your daily diet to 50 grams, about the same amount of sugar found in a can of Coke.
• Wall Street stock futures are edging down. European shares are little changed, and Asian indexes ended mostly lower.
NOTEWORTHY
• Nine-figure nude.
Modigliani’s “Nu Couché,” a painting of an outstretched nude woman, sold on Monday night for $170.4 million in a packed sales room at Christie’s in New York. It was the second-highest price paid for an artwork at auction.
Think you know the value of art? Take our interactive quiz.
• Fresh reads.
Music fans have the releases of Peter Guralnick’s “Sam Phillips: The Man Who Invented Rock ’n’ Roll” and Warren Zanes’ biography of Tom Petty to look forward to today.
In fiction, Mitch Albom’s “The Magic Strings of Frankie Presto” chronicles the life of the world’s greatest guitarist from the vantage point of his funeral.
• Learning Stonehenge’s secrets.
Researchers using aerial imagery, ground-penetrating radar and other techniques are discovering new details in the landscape around the giant stone monument in England.
• Red cup stirs outcry.
The annual red holiday cup of Starbucks — minus its seasonal snowflakes, ornaments and reindeer — is getting a lot of attention, much of it negative.
• What’s new to watch.
The “almosts,” the difficulties astronauts faced that could have been catastrophic, are the focus of “Secret Space Escapes,” a series that debuts tonight (10 p.m. Eastern, Science Channel).
“Debt of Honor,” directed by Ric Burns, looks at how the U.S. government and society have treated disabled veterans, from the Revolutionary War to today (9 p.m. Eastern, PBS, but check local listings).
And here are your episode recaps for “Fargo” and “Blindspot.”
BACK STORY
We are nearing the time of year not only for giving thanks but also for giving in general — donations, time and money.
The World Giving Index, which ranks 145 countries by the prevalence of charitable behavior, places the U.S. second in its annual results, released today.
The much poorer country of Myanmar, just off its first democratic elections in 25 years, claims the new No. 1 title. (It tied with the U.S. for first in the previous survey.)
In fact, a lot of the top 20 is made up of developing countries, according to Charities Aid Foundation, which compiles the index.
Thomas Fuller, our Southeast Asia correspondent, notes that giving is woven into the daily lives of Buddhists in Myanmar.
“Fund-raisers parade through cities on trucks or wander the streets with loudspeakers that blare pleas for donations,” he told us. The donations are small but frequent, and have significant religious meaning.
In the West, an increasing number of countries are focusing their charity efforts on Giving Tuesday, created by the 92nd Street Y in New York three years ago.
Coming right after Black Friday and Cyber Monday, Giving Tuesday (Dec. 1 this year) is used by organizations and companies to rally individuals.
