Your Thursday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• California shooting aftermath.
Two suspects, said to be married, were killed in a shootout with the police on Wednesday after the shooting at a social services center in San Bernardino, Calif., in which at least 14 people died and 17 were wounded, the police said.
One of the suspects, Syed Rizwan Farook, 28, worked for the county health department. After a dispute at a holiday party, he returned with Tashfeen Malik, 27, who family members said was his wife, both in tactical gear and carrying assault rifles, officials said. We will continue to follow developments today.
Records show that, on average, there is a mass shooting every day in the U.S.
• Pistorius guilty of murder.
A South African appeals court today found the Olympic star Oscar Pistorius guilty of murdering his girlfriend, Reeva Steenkamp, on Valentine’s Day 2013.
The court overturned the decision last year of a lower-court judge, who gave Mr. Pistorius a five-year prison sentence for the lesser charge of culpable homicide. He is expected to remain under house arrest pending a new sentence.
• New arrests of FIFA officials.
More than a dozen people are expected to be charged, including several South and Central American soccer leaders, after a series of pre-dawn arrests in Switzerland today.
• At the White House.
President Obama meets today with members of Congress to discuss overhauls to the criminal justice system.
Federal and state officials, advocates and experts will also be on hand. Topics include fees and fines imposed on defendants by local districts, and the fixed-money bail system, which the Justice Department has said is unconstitutional.
• Campaign update.
Most of the Republican field is in Washington — the city they love to hate — at a donor-filled forum sponsored by the Republican Jewish Coalition.
And Senate Republicans up for re-election are being urged to keep their distance from Donald J. Trump, who is leading in national polls in the race for the Republican nomination.
• Doubts about C.I.A.-trained forces.
The U.S. spy agency has trained thousands of Afghan fighters, but Afghan officials say they are largely unaccountable to the government and that raids the fighters conducted killed at least six civilians.
• The search for Flight 370.
Investigators today refined the possible flight path of the Malaysia Airlines jet missing since March 8, 2014, using a set of probability tools that they say takes them closer to locating the plane in the southern Indian Ocean.
BUSINESS
• Plaintiffs’ lawsuits against Volkswagen could be consolidated into one case. A panel of judges is expected to make that decision today.
The automaker is accused of defrauding customers, who say the value of their cars has dropped, when it used deceptive software to evade emissions tests for some of its diesel models. Millions of cars were affected.
• Shareholders of Anthem and Cigna, two of the largest U.S. health insurance companies, will vote today on whether to proceed with a merger, valued at nearly $50 billion. Regulators have the final say, though.
• Janet L. Yellen, the Federal Reserve chairwoman, heads to Capitol Hill today to testify on the U.S. economic outlook in an annual appearance before the congressional Joint Economic Committee.
• The European Central Bank has signaled that it is likely to intensify measures to stimulate the eurozone economy when it meets today.
• Wall Street stock futures are trending positive. European markets are lower, and Asian indexes finished mixed.
NOTEWORTHY
• No more Harvard “house masters.”
Harvard College plans to change the title of its heads of residential housing from the traditional “house master” to a new term, yet to be chosen, that will better reflect “the 21st-century needs of residential student life,” it announced.
The change follows long discussions over whether the term “master” has connotations of slavery.
• Scoreboard.
The Golden State Warriors extended the best start in N.B.A. history to 20-0, with a 116-99 win over the Charlotte Hornets on Wednesday night.
The Detroit Lions host the Green Bay Packers, who have lost four of their last five games (8:25 p.m. Eastern, CBS, NFL Network).
And N.F.L. owners are leaning toward voting on whether to allow one or two teams to move to Los Angeles when they meet next month.
• Party for a poet.
The Library of Congress is celebrating today the 750th anniversary of Dante’s birth with a panel discussion, followed by a display of treasures related to the author of “The Divine Comedy.”
• What’s new to watch.
Mary J. Blige, the R&B star, stars as the Wicked Witch of the West and Queen Latifah has the title role in the musical “The Wiz,” based on “The Wonderful Wizard of Oz” by L. Frank Baum. It’s broadcast live at 8 p.m. Eastern on NBC.
And the lighting of the National Christmas tree happens tonight on the Ellipse in Washington (live on PBS or online).
BACK STORY
On this day in 1967, surgeons in South Africa, led by Dr. Christiaan N. Barnard, performed the first human heart transplant. The patient, a man in his 50s, died 18 days later of pneumonia.
The first in the U.S. came just three days later, when Dr. Adrian Kantrowitz removed the heart of a brain-dead baby and implanted it into the chest of a baby with a fatal heart defect. The baby lived for only six and a half hours.
Though they failed to greatly lengthen the lives of the patients, the operations paved the way for routine transplants.
Another advance occurred 15 years later, when a permanent artificial heart was implanted in a human for the first time, on Dec. 2, 1982.
Doctors at the University of Utah Medical Center were able to extend the life of Dr. Barney B. Clark by 112 days with the device, which required tethering him to a large compressor and consoles.
Today, artificial hearts allow patients to move around freely, but they’re mainly used during the wait for a transplant.
Companies are still trying to develop a permanent artificial replacement heart, one that might make the transplants that Dr. Barnard pioneered a relic.
