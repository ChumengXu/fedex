Your Thursday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Stocks swoon.
A 7 percent dive in China’s stock market today is reverberating in plummeting shares around the world.
Futures trading on Wall Street suggests U.S. markets will open with a 2 percent drop. European and Asian stocks are down nearly 3 percent.
The underlying worry is over China’s currency and its slowing economy, which could crimp global growth. The markets in China closed after only 29 minutes of trading today and did not reopen.
• On the Korean Peninsula.
South Korea announced today that it would restart the cross-border propaganda broadcasts that North Korea considers acts of war, one day after the North said it had tested a hydrogen bomb.
While North Korea’s claim is being treated with skepticism, the North may still be on the verge of turning a small nuclear arsenal into a good-size one.
• Pushing to restrict guns.
President Obama will hold a live, televised town-hall-style meeting tonight (8 p.m. Eastern, CNN) to discuss gun violence and emphasize the need for more gun restrictions, two days after announcing executive actions to close loopholes in gun sales.
• On the campaign trail.
Bill Clinton is campaigning for his wife, Hillary, in Iowa today, and Donald J. Trump is holding a rally in Burlington, Vt., the hometown of Bernie Sanders.
Mrs. Clinton, Mr. Sanders and Martin O’Malley were at a Democratic fund-raising dinner in Las Vegas on Wednesday night, where they pointedly criticized Republicans more than they did one another.
And Vice President Joseph R. Biden Jr. says that he regrets not running for president but that it was “the right decision.”
• Use of medical marijuana grows.
New York joins the ranks of nearly half the states today in allowing the use of medical marijuana.
Eight dispensaries serving syrups, concentrates and other forms of the drug will be open for people with certain diseases.
• Spewing methane in Los Angeles.
More than 2,000 families have left the affluent Porter Ranch neighborhood because of a natural gas leak’s smell and side effects, which include nosebleeds, headaches, dizziness and vomiting.
The leak was detected on Oct. 23; Gov. Jerry Brown declared a state of emergency on Wednesday.
• France remembers.
A special issue of the satirical newspaper Charlie Hebdo is on sale this week in tribute to the 17 people who were killed one year ago in attacks at the publication’s office and elsewhere in the Paris area.
• Climate hindsight.
We should learn more this morning about 2015’s weird weather when the U.S. government releases its annual National Climate Monitoring Annual Report.
BUSINESS
• The price of crude oil is down by two-thirds since 2014, to less than $35 a barrel, after falling 5 percent on Wednesday.
Traders seem to be focused on rising global oil supplies rather than on instability in the Middle East.
• Macy’s is citing unseasonably warm weather across much of the U.S., which led to lower winter clothing sales, as part of the reason for 4,500 layoffs. The jobs make up about 3 percent of its work force.
• United Airlines’s president and chief executive, Oscar Munoz, had heart transplant surgery and could be back at work in March or April. He had a heart attack in October shortly after being named to the posts.
NOTEWORTHY
• For laughs.
A streaming comedy subscription site called Seeso, from NBC, goes live today for $3.99 a month.
It will offer old shows like “Monty Python’s Flying Circus” and all of “Saturday Night Live,” as well as new content, in an effort to compete with Netflix, Amazon and Hulu.
Netflix became available on Wednesday in nearly every country in the world — but not, notably, in China — an unexpected acceleration of the company’s expansion.
• New year’s TV premieres.
Jennifer Lopez stars as a New York police detective working with an F.B.I. anti-corruption unit in “Shades of Blue” (10 p.m. Eastern, NBC).
“Growing Up Hip Hop” is a reality series about the scions of prominent hip-hop figures (10 p.m., WE).
And Jane Lynch plays a guardian angel who’s essentially a well-meaning, pathologically needy stalker on “Angel From Hell” (9:30 p.m., CBS).
• Military honors.
Drone operators will be eligible for American military honors similar to those given to pilots who flew over the battlefields of Iraq and Afghanistan.
• Wednesday’s victors.
Ellen DeGeneres and Johnny Depp were among the top winners at the People’s Choice Awards.
And Ken Griffey Jr. and Mike Piazza were voted into the Baseball Hall of Fame
• Fit for a king.
Today is the official kickoff to a four-day celebration for what would have been Elvis Presley’s 81st birthday on Friday, at his Graceland mansion in Memphis.
• So you didn’t win?
Don’t worry, no one won Wednesday night’s Powerball lottery drawing, which means this weekend’s may be the largest jackpot in U.S. history.
BACK STORY
He didn’t take a giant leap for mankind, but Thomas Stevens took a giant spin, becoming the first person to complete a trip around Earth by bicycle, on this day in 1887.
Considering that 71 percent of the planet’s surface is covered with water, an explanation is in order.
Mr. Stevens moved to the United States from England in the 1870s. He bought his first bicycle, a penny-farthing with one large wheel, in San Francisco and dreamed of becoming the first person to cross America on a bicycle.When he felt ready, he donned a jacket that doubled as a tent and headed for the East Coast on April 22, 1884.
He made it to Boston 103 days later. Plans for a global journey began to take shape.
The next spring, Mr. Stevens started in England and headed east through Europe and the Ottoman Empire. He continued on to Persia, Afghanistan, India, China and Japan, before returning to San Francisco.
Throughout the trip, he sent letters to Harper’s Magazine, which were compiled in the best-selling two-volume book “Around the World on a Bicycle.”
But wait: How did he make it across two oceans? He was a passenger on steam ships. It is not known if he rode his bike on deck.
Victoria Shannon contributed reporting.
