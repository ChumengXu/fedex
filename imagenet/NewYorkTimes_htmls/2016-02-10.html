Your Wednesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Decisive wins in New Hampshire.
Donald J. Trump and Bernie Sanders dominated the Republican and Democratic primaries on Tuesday, winning almost every town. Their victories are a rebuke to the political establishment and signal a long fight for the presidential nominations.
The race among the candidates aligned with the Republican Party establishment remains muddled.
We have the full results, along with some takeaways from the vote, and video of the candidates’ remarks.
South Carolina’s primary and Nevada’s caucuses begin on Feb. 20. Here is the full calendar of the contests.
• On Capitol Hill.
The mayor of Flint, Mich., is among those attending a House hearing held by Democrats today on protecting children. Michigan’s governor won’t be there.
State investigators say that their inquiry into the contamination of Flint’s tap water could bring charges ranging from involuntary manslaughter to official misconduct.
• Back to the beginning.
President Obama travels to Springfield, Ill., where he announced his run for president nine years ago, to address the Illinois General Assembly today about growing partisanship in the U.S. He said last month that was a regret of his presidency.
He’s also expected to speak about the $4 trillion budget proposal he sent to Congress on Tuesday. It includes a detailed agenda to ease workers’ anxieties.
• A setback for President Obama.
The Supreme Court has temporarily blocked the president’s emissions restrictions on coal-fired power plants. It’s a sign the rules might be overturned after further review.
The court had never before granted a request to stop a regulation before review by a federal appeals court.
• A first for ISIS.
Islamic State extremists have, for the first time, claimed responsibility for an attack in Syria’s capital, a car bomb that killed several people and wounded dozens.
Syrian opposition groups say they can sense U.S. support fading, and the European Union says that 10,000 child refugees who entered Europe are missing.
BUSINESS
• Janet L. Yellen, the Fed chairwoman, will speak publicly for the first time in almost two months when she testifies today before the House Financial Services Committee.
Investors will be analyzing her message because of concerns about turmoil in financial markets and weakness in the global economy.
• Twitter will report fourth-quarter earnings today. It’s a chance for its chief executive, Jack Dorsey, to show he can reset the ailing company.
• Falling oil prices have some companies fighting for survival.
• Japan’s stock market dropped an additional 2 percent today, as worries about the health of global banks and economic growth grows.
Here are snapshots of the U.S. and world markets.
NOTEWORTHY
• Ash Wednesday.
Pope Francis will say Mass today at St. Peter’s Basilica in the Vatican for the start of Lent.
On Tuesday, Hampton Court Palace near London, where King Henry VIII broke off ties with the Vatican in 1530 so that he could divorce his first wife and marry his mistress, held its first Roman Catholic service in more than 450 years.
• Winter heat wave.
February temperature records are being set in Southern California, with readings in the upper 80s and 90s.
• Defying stereotypes.
Sophina DeJesus of the U.C.L.A. gymnastics team has been performing hip-hop dance step during her floor exercises, and a video of her moves has gone viral.
Ibtihaj Muhammad, a fencer, is expected to be the first American Olympian to compete while wearing a hijab.
• In case you missed it …
One of our reporters accompanied 10 Central Americans as they made a desperate trek through Mexico to get to the U.S. The article also appears on The Times en Español, our new website for a Spanish-speaking audience.
The wildly popular messaging app Kik — used mostly by teenagers — is worryinging law enforcement agencies because it has been linked to a growing number of abuse cases.
• What’s on TV.
A new Spike Lee documentary looks at Michael Jackson’s rise to fame (8:15 p.m. Eastern, Showtime).
Bernie Sanders appears on the “Late Show With Stephen Colbert.” (11:30 p.m. Eastern, CBS).
And YouTube Originals releases its first films today, but they’re not free.
• Dog-themed paintings.
The Doyle Dogs in Art Auction takes place today in New York.
• Neighboring music legends.
Beginning today, you can visit Jimi Hendrix’s London home, and the home of the classical composer Handel.
• What to cook.
A recipe for cod cakes is our pick for tonight.
BACK STORY
Bob Dylan recorded “The Times They Are A-Changin’ ” a month before John F. Kennedy’s assassination, and when the album was released on this day in 1964, it appeared as though Mr. Dylan was an oracle.
The lyrics to the title track included:
There’s a battle outside and it is ragin’
It’ll soon shake your windows and rattle your walls
For the times they are a-changin’
The album, Mr. Dylan’s third, quickly became an anthem of both the civil rights and anti-Vietnam War movements.
It contained some of the songs that helped establish him as a defining voice of his time, like “The Lonesome Death of Hattie Carroll,” about the fatal caning of a black barmaid by a wealthy young white man.
Mr. Dylan grew up as Robert Allen Zimmerman in Minnesota, listening to Appalachian ballads, blues, gospel and pop. He took the last name Dylan — after the poet Dylan Thomas — before moving to Greenwich Village in New York in 1961.
His socially conscious approach was echoed by rock ’n’ roll bands in the 1960s and, decades later, in rap music.
Five years ago, Mr. Dylan performed where he may never have imagined: Vietnam.
