Your Wednesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• The president’s new campaign.
President Obama explains and defends the nuclear agreement reached last month with Iran at American University in Washington this morning. President John F. Kennedy spoke there in 1963 on nuclear disarmament and peace.
Mr. Obama will portray the deal as the most consequential foreign policy decision for Congress since the vote to go to war in Iraq.
Persian Gulf countries issued a cautious endorsement of the deal this week, delivering the U.S. president a diplomatic win.
• Ready for prime time?
Making the cut for Thursday night’s Republican candidate debate are Donald J. Trump, Jeb Bush, Scott Walker, Mike Huckabee, Ben Carson, Ted Cruz, Marco Rubio, Rand Paul, Chris Christie and John Kasich.
That relegates Rick Perry, Rick Santorum, Bobby Jindal, Carly Fiorina, Lindsey Graham, George E. Pataki and James Gilmore to an earlier debate on Thursday.
On the Democrats’ side, Senator Bernie Sanders of Vermont is tightening the race with Hillary Rodham Clinton in New Hampshire, according to a local opinion poll released on Tuesday.
• Secret military courts approved.
Pakistan’s Supreme Court ruled today that the tribunals are legal and can pass death sentences on civilians.
More than 200 people have been put to death there since December, when a moratorium on executions was lifted after Taliban gunmen massacred 150 people at an army-run school, including 13 children.
• Flight 370.
Malaysian, Australian and French officials today begin their examination of a wing part that washed up on the island of Réunion last week.
They have not said when they will announce whether it is part of Malaysia Airlines Flight 370, which disappeared 17 months ago.
• “Cecil” trial postponed.
The Zimbabwe poaching trial for the guide who led a Minnesota dentist on the hunt that killed Cecil the lion was postponed today until the end of September, so that his lawyer can prepare.
The dentist, Dr. Walter J. Palmer, has admitted to killing the lion, but he has not been charged in Zimbabwe. Legal experts say the case may be difficult to prosecute in the U.S.
MARKETS
• The pay gap between chief executives and the rest of us will be made public under a rule expected to be adopted today by the Securities and Exchange Commission.
Commissioners are likely to reject business objections that the pay-ratio requirement — part of the 2010 Dodd-Frank Act — would cost money to follow and give investors scant information.
• The high-end retailer Neiman Marcus is preparing for a public stock offering after more than a decade under private equity ownership and two different sets of investors.
• Earnings reports today from Time Warner, 21st Century Fox and CBS will give a sense of the financial health of the media business.
• Wall Street stock futures are edging into positive territory. European shares have moved higher, and Asian indexes ended mixed.
NOTEWORTHY
• Does that include Santa’s workshop?
Russia has formally staked a new claim to a vast area of the Arctic Ocean that includes the North Pole.
Under the 1982 Law of the Sea, a nation may lay claim to an economic zone over the continental shelf abutting its shores.
Russia supported its claim with scientific evidence that the U.N. said was lacking in a similar submission in 2002.
• Attention, back-to-school shoppers.
Ohio and Florida start sales-tax holidays this weekend. They are among at least 17 states that are giving shoppers a break this year from state or local taxes for back-to-school purchases.
Tax policy experts call the holidays a gimmick, but their popularity has grown over the years.
• New to watch.
Craig Robinson stars as a bar-band singer who doubles as a substitute music teacher in a Chicago high school in the new comedy “Mr. Robinson” (9 p.m. Eastern, NBC).
And “Difficult People” is a comedy centering on two underachieving, self-absorbed friends in New York (streaming, Hulu).
• Hollywood split.
Miss Piggy and Kermit the Frog have announced their separation, conveniently setting the stage for ABC’s revival of “The Muppets,” which will begin airing in September.
“After careful thought, thoughtful consideration and considerable squabbling, Kermit the Frog and moi have made the difficult decision to terminate our romantic relationship,” Miss Piggy says on Facebook.
• Vanished.
Natalia Molchanova, 53, a record-setting free diver, is presumed dead after she did not surface from a dive in the Balearic Sea, off the eastern coast of Spain.
• More chiles, please.
Eating spicy food is associated with a reduced risk of premature death, a study of more than 485,000 people found.
BACK STORY
In Tuesday’s Morning Briefing, we wrote that several Republican presidential candidates were “on the bubble” for spots in Thursday’s debate on Fox News.
But more than one reader told us that they were unfamiliar with the phrase.
One theory is that the idiom comes from the notion that something on the surface of a bubble can slide off in one direction or another.
In this case, the candidates were equally likely to be in or out of the debate, depending on opinion poll results.
Sports fans see the term a lot when the N.C.A.A. prepares to announce the basketball teams for the March tournament. Those with flickers of hope of making it are “on the bubble.”
And it’s popular in references to the qualifying heats of the Indianapolis 500, when racecar drivers have their hopes dashed (translation: bubbles burst) by others with faster times.
The phrase is used across the sporting world. In February, a Times headline referred to the golfer Tiger Woods as “on the bubble for qualifying.”
Maybe it’s best to leave sports lingo to the Sports section.
