Your Tuesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• New Hampshire’s decision.
A new poll says 23 percent of undeclared voters in the state are still open to changing their minds in today’s primaries. Forty percent of eligible voters there are undeclared and are allowed to vote in either the Democratic or Republican primary.
Marco Rubio’s difficulties with talking points continue, and he had a tense exchange with a gay voter on Monday.
Bernie Sanders and Donald J. Trump are leading New Hampshire polls, but a winter weather advisory could affect turnout.
And we take a close look at why a loss for Hillary Clinton today could be especially painful for her: In 1992, New Hampshire voters saved Bill Clinton’s presidential campaign.
• At the White House.
President Obama submits his final budget proposal to Congress today. The $4 trillion plan includes $7.5 billion to escalate the fight against Islamic State extremists.
The Republican chairmen of the House and Senate budget committees have already snubbed the proposal, sight unseen. They said they would not invite the president’s budget director to a hearing on the proposal, an extreme break from tradition.
• On Capitol Hill.
The Senate Armed Services Committee hears testimony today from James R. Clapper Jr., the director of national intelligence, and others about worldwide security threats, including from the Islamic State.
A Senate energy bill is expected to be put to a vote this week, and with it a large infusion of money for Flint, Mich., where high lead levels have been found in tap water.
• Water contamination.
In addition to Flint, lead contamination has hit scores of U.S. communities in recent years, a problem that experts attribute to holes in rules and procedures intended to keep water free of poisons.
We look at the latest research on long-term damage from lead exposure. Between three million and six million miles of pipe lines from water mains to U.S. homes contain lead, so the problem could take a long time to resolve.
• Train collision in Germany.
At least eight people are dead and more than 100 injured after two commuter trains crashed head-on this morning in the state of Bavaria.
It’s not immediately clear why the trains were traveling on the same stretch of track at the same time.
• Updates on police shootings.
A Chicago officer being sued for the fatal shooting of a black 19-year-old has countersued, and he is seeking more than $10 million in damages from the teenager’s estate for “extreme emotional trauma.”
In Baltimore, prosecutors have asked a judge to delay the trials — possibly for several months — of three officers charged in the Freddie Gray case.
• The SAT gets a makeover.
Some educators worry that the revised, reading-heavy test — one of its biggest redesigns ever — will penalize certain categories of students, like immigrants and the poor.
Try these questions, taken from a practice test.
• A tinkerer with more patents than Edison.
Artur Fischer, an inventor who registered more than 1,100 patents, including the first synchronized camera flash and an anchor that millions of do-it-yourselfers use to secure screws into walls, died at age 96.
BUSINESS
• Viacom is scheduled to report earnings today. The resignation of Sumner M. Redstone last week as executive chairman has added to a power struggle at the company.
• The Walt Disney Company announces its quarterly earnings, which should get a big lift from sales of “Star Wars” merchandise. But concerns about ESPN remain.
• Japanese stocks fell more than 5 percent today. Here are snapshots of the U.S. and world markets.
NOTEWORTHY
• New reads.
The Pulitzer Prize winner Jhumpa Lahiri releases her nonfiction debut, “In Other Words,” about her love of the Italian language.
Jean Stein chronicles the bad behavior of Hollywood’s ruling class in “West of Eden.” Chris Offutt’s memoir, “My Father, the Pornographer,” is about a man who wrote and published more than 400 books.
• What’s on.
“Frontline,” in collaboration with The Times, follows the growth of fantasy sports and online sports betting in the U.S. and beyond (10 p.m. Eastern, PBS, but check local listings).
• Health remedy.
Our Personal Health column today looks at chronic constipation, a common problem that is rarely discussed in polite company or even during medical checkups.
• Mardi Gras recipes.
How about a recipe for gumbo tonight, plus bananas Foster bread pudding for dessert?
• Winter’s not the same.
Last week we told you about festivals that embrace winter and its frigid temperatures. Towns in northern New York, though, are struggling to celebrate the season because of warm weather.
BACK STORY
“Dear God, I am fourteen years old. I have always been a good girl. Maybe you can give me a sign letting me know what is happening to me.”
So begins “The Color Purple,” the story of Celie, a poor, black child-bride who struggles to escape her abusive stepfather in 1930s Georgia.
The author of the book, Alice Walker, born on this day in 1944 in Eatonton, Ga., told the tale mostly through Celie’s letters. The authenticity of her voice catapulted the novel to best-sellers lists, as well as a Pulitzer Prize and a National Book Award.
Ms. Walker was born to sharecroppers, the youngest of eight children. At age 8, she was blinded in one eye when her brother accidentally shot her with a BB gun.
She later wrote that the disfiguring scar and blindness left her feeling isolated and introspective. She started a journal and began to develop her writing voice.
“I think writing really helps you heal yourself,” Ms. Walker once told The Times. “I think if you write long enough, you will be a healthy person. That is, if you write what you need to write, as opposed to what will make money, or what will make fame.”
