Call her a romantic: Since her introduction to the market 56 years ago, Barbie (or at least, Mattel, the company that created her) has had a single dream - convincing little girls that she's their friend. Now, with the help of artificial intelligence, cloud computing and a writer's room that's all her own, Barbie might actually succeed - which raises a whole host of questions.
Joining forces with new start-up ToyTalk, Mattel has created Hello Barbie, available in November - a doll that can not only talk but also hold a conversation. Barbie is at once friendly and evasive ("I think a person's beliefs are very personal to them," she might reply after being asked about her religion) and wholly you-go-girl ("'Of course you're pretty, but you know what else you are? You're smart, talented and funny''). The writers envisioned her as the world's best babysitter. But psychologists are worried: Will a conversational doll keep children from developing their own imaginations?
In a special web-only follow up to his sobering photo essay in our Sept. 6 issue, the photographer Paolo Pellegrin has once again captured haunting photographs of the migration crisis underway in the Mediterranean. As many as 20,000 refugees from Syria and Afghanistan were sleeping in parks or vacant lots on the Greek island Lesbos earlier this month after arriving by sea from Turkey, waiting for the registration papers allowing them to continue their journeys. Pellegrin was on hand as tensions rose and the scene turned violent, with refugees clashing with the police and with each other.
Elsewhere in the magazine, Jay Caspian Kang profiles Kareem Abdul Jabbar, the legendary basketball player who is at once deeply revered and misunderstood. Rachel Donadio, formerly the Rome bureau chief for The Times, recounts her life on the papal beat as Pope Francis makes a landmark visit to the Americas this month. The photographer James Balog and the writer Phillip Connors, who works as a fire lookout in New Mexico, contemplate the frightening new landscape of wildfire in the West. Jenna Wortham writes about Bri Luna, proprietor of the online shop the Hoodwitch; Maureen Dowd asks Kate McKinnon about cat ladies and her Hillary Clinton impression; and Charles Siebert rhapsodizes about the Art Institute of Chicago's tiny and obsessively detailed Thorne Miniature Rooms.
Happy reading,
Jake Silverstein
