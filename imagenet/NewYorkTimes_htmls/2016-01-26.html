Your Tuesday Briefing
By ADEEL HASSAN
Good morning.
Here’s what you need to know:
• Grand jury clears Planned Parenthood.
A Houston grand jury investigating accusations that Planned Parenthood sold fetal tissue for profit has cleared the group of wrongdoing.
Instead, jurors indicted two opponents of abortion who made undercover videos purporting to show the sales.
• On the campaign trail.
Republican leaders are growing alarmed by the ferocious ways the party’s mainstream candidates are attacking one another, and fear that time is running out for any of them to emerge as a credible alternative to Donald J. Trump or Ted Cruz. Mr. Trump says he might skip Thursday’s debate.
On Monday night, the Democratic candidates presented contrasting views to Iowans at a town-hall-style event. And Hillary Clinton is opening up and has even shared her views on Christianity.
• Rights for juvenile offenders.
President Obama has banned the practice of holding juveniles in solitary confinement in federal prisons, saying it could lead to “devastating, lasting psychological consequences.”
And the Supreme Court has granted a new chance at release for hundreds of inmates serving life sentences without the possibility of parole for murders they committed in their youth.
• Don’t have babies, El Salvador pleads.
The government is advising all women in the country not to get pregnant until 2018, as it tries to deal with a mosquito-borne virus linked to brain damage in infants.
The virus, known as Zika, has also been linked to paralysis in adults; and now scientists face another ominous possibility: that on rare occasions, the virus might be transmitted through sex.
• Syrian peace talks rescheduled.
The U.N. aims to begin the meetings on Friday, but it has declined to identify the members of the Syrian opposition who are invited.
Iran’s president, Hassan Rouhani, is expected to discuss peace efforts in Syria and Iraq when he meets Pope Francis today at the Vatican.
And Denmark’s Parliament is voting today on a proposal to confiscate asylum seekers’ valuables to pay for their care.
• Behind the Philadelphia rail disaster.
The National Transportation Safety Board is expected to release a report this morning on the Amtrak train derailment in May, which killed eight people and injured dozens.
The Times Magazine looks in depth at what caused the derailment, the worst American rail disaster in decades.
• Climate deal’s big challenge.
Low oil prices could make it harder for governments to favor policies that encourage or require the use of renewable energy sources.
BUSINESS
• Fed officials gather today and Wednesday for their first policy meeting of 2016, to consider how long to pause after having raised interest rates in December.
• Many eyes will be on iPhone sales today, as investors grasp from Apple’s quarterly earnings announcement just how much the company’s growth is slowing.
• China shares fell today as oil prices continued to slide and investor worries led to a sell-off. Here are snapshots of the U.S. and world markets.
• Many African economies are weakening, as China growth slows and its hunger for African commodities wanes.
NOTEWORTHY
• Then came the locusts.
Argentina’s worst plague of locusts in more than half a century is threatening sunflower and cotton crops, as well as grasslands for cattle grazing.
The locusts are expected to grow to about two inches in 10 days and to mature into voracious flying swarms in search of food.
• Speaking of crawling things.
Hundreds of species of flies, spiders and other bugs live in our homes, according to a new study.
• Sports roundup.
Serena Williams earned her 18th straight victory against Maria Sharapova, to advance to the semifinals of the Australian Open. Here are the rest of today’s matches.
On Monday night, the Warriors crushed the Spurs, 120-90, in a matchup of N.B.A. championship contenders.
And Florida State University has agreed to pay $950,000 to a woman who accused the football star Jameis Winston of rape.
• In memoriam.
The British explorer Henry Worsley, who was trying to be the first person to cross the Antarctic alone and without support, died on Sunday, just 30 miles short of completing his 1,000-mile trek. He was 55.
Marvin Minsky, who laid the foundation for artificial intelligence, died at 88.
• Woody Allen dives into streaming TV.
His untitled project for Amazon, which is set in the 1960s, will star Miley Cyrus, Elaine May, and himself. It has a scheduled run of six half-hour episodes.
And here’s a recap of Episode 2 of “The X-Files.”
BACK STORY
It’s January, so gyms are teeming with people working on their New Year’s resolutions.
But the modern gym is a relatively recent innovation.
In ancient Greece, the gymnasion, or “school for naked exercise,” existed to train men for public competitions. During the Renaissance, exercise began to be more recognized as beneficial. And the Enlightenment embraced the idea of physical education for all.
A Prussian born in the late 1700s, Friedrich Ludwig Jahn, became known as the “father of gymnastics.” He wanted to make the youth fit for military service, and his ideas spread to other countries, including to the United States in the 1840s.
Around the same time, the Young Men’s Christian Association was founded in London, and the U.S. had its first Y.M.C.A. by 1851.
The growth of team sports like baseball and football toward the end of the 1800s gave young American men more reason to work out. And as the country grew more urban, so did the need to keep city dwellers fit.
President Dwight D. Eisenhower helped make fitness a national priority amid concerns that young Americans were trailing their European counterparts. The 1980s aerobics mania — headlined by Jane Fonda — further led to the coed gyms we attend today as we try to attain that Greek fitness ideal.
